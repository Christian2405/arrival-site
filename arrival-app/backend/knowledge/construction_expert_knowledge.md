# Construction Expert Knowledge

## About This Guide
This knowledge base comes from decades of residential and light commercial construction experience. Every number, every tip, every warning here has been earned on job sites — from framing tract homes in the desert to remodeling century-old Victorians in the Northeast. When you're on a job and something doesn't look right, trust your gut. But also trust the numbers.

---

## Residential Framing

### Lumber Basics
Modern framing lumber isn't what it used to be. A "2x4" actually measures 1-1/2" x 3-1/2". A "2x6" is 1-1/2" x 5-1/2". A "2x10" is 1-1/2" x 9-1/4". A "2x12" is 1-1/2" x 11-1/4". You need to know these actual dimensions cold because every rough opening, every header calculation, every layout depends on them.

Lumber grades matter. For structural framing, you want #2 or better SPF (spruce-pine-fir) or Doug Fir. #3 grade is suitable for blocking and non-structural uses only. Pressure-treated lumber is required for any wood in contact with concrete or within 6 inches of the ground — use ground-contact rated (UC4A minimum) for posts set in concrete.

Always check lumber for crown (the slight bow along the length). When installing joists and studs, crown goes UP. A stud with 1/4" crown in 8 feet is acceptable. More than that, set it aside for blocking or cripples.

### Stud Spacing and Layout
Standard stud spacing is 16" on center (OC) for most residential walls. You'll see 24" OC in some cases — engineered walls with higher grade lumber, or non-load-bearing partition walls when allowed by code. For exterior walls in high wind zones or when supporting heavy roof loads, stick with 16" OC.

Layout starts from the end of the plate. Hook your tape on the end, mark at 15-1/4" for the first stud (so the edge of the first sheet of sheathing or drywall lands centered on a stud), then every 16" after that: 31-1/4", 47-1/4", 63-1/4", and so on. Mark with an X on the side of the line where the stud goes, and mark your king studs and trimmers with K and T.

Common mistake: forgetting to account for the 1-1/2" thickness when layout reaches a corner or intersection. Always think about where your sheathing and drywall need to land.

### Headers and Load Paths
Every opening in a load-bearing wall needs a header. Header sizing depends on span and load:
- Up to 4' span: double 2x6 (or single 4x6)
- 4' to 6' span: double 2x8
- 6' to 8' span: double 2x10
- 8' to 10' span: double 2x12
- Over 10' span: engineered lumber (LVL, PSL, or steel)

These are general rules for single-story construction with standard roof loads. Two-story situations, heavy tile roofs, or snow loads may require larger headers — always check with your engineer.

Headers are built with 1/2" plywood spacer sandwiched between the two dimensional lumber pieces to bring the total thickness to 3-1/2", which matches a 2x4 wall. In a 2x6 wall, use two layers of 1/2" plywood or adjust accordingly to fill the wall depth.

Jack studs (trimmers) carry the header. One trimmer per side for openings up to 4 feet. Two trimmers per side for openings 4 to 8 feet. Three trimmers for openings over 8 feet. King studs run full height beside the trimmers and nail to them.

### Load-Bearing vs Non-Load-Bearing Walls
This is critical and getting it wrong can be catastrophic. Load-bearing walls carry weight from above — the roof, upper floors, or both. They transfer loads down to the foundation.

How to identify load-bearing walls:
- All exterior walls are load-bearing (with rare exceptions in post-and-beam construction)
- Interior walls running perpendicular to floor joists or roof rafters are usually load-bearing
- Any wall directly below another wall on the floor above is likely load-bearing
- Walls in the center of the house running the length of the building are often bearing a ridge or floor loads
- Look in the basement or crawl space: if a wall sits on a beam or has posts beneath it, it's load-bearing

When removing a load-bearing wall, you must install a beam (typically LVL or steel) to carry the load. This requires an engineer's calculation for beam size, post locations, and footing requirements. Never, ever remove a load-bearing wall without engineering.

### LVLs and Engineered Lumber
Laminated Veneer Lumber (LVL) is your go-to for long spans and heavy loads. Common sizes: 1-3/4" x 9-1/4" (comparable to 2x10), 1-3/4" x 11-7/8" (comparable to 2x12), 1-3/4" x 14", and 1-3/4" x 16".

For beams, you laminate multiple LVLs together. A triple 1-3/4" x 11-7/8" LVL beam (5-1/4" wide) can typically span 14-16 feet carrying a floor load. But always follow the manufacturer's span tables — Boise Cascade, Weyerhaeuser (Microllam), and Louisiana-Pacific all publish load tables for their products.

I-joists (TJI, BCI) are the standard for floor framing now. Depths from 9-1/2" to 16". They're lightweight, straight, and don't shrink or twist like dimensional lumber. But they require web stiffeners at bearing points and you cannot cut or notch the flanges — ever. Hole locations in the web are specified by the manufacturer. Get it wrong and you've destroyed the joist.

## Concrete Work

### Mix Ratios and Strength
Standard concrete mix for residential work:
- 3000 PSI: general purpose — sidewalks, patios, non-structural slabs
- 3500 PSI: driveways, garage floors, standard footings
- 4000 PSI: structural footings, foundations, post-tension slabs
- 4500+ PSI: commercial work, high-load applications

The water-cement ratio is the most important factor in concrete strength. More water makes it easier to work, but weaker when cured. Target a slump of 4 to 5 inches for most residential work. A 6-inch slump is acceptable for pumped concrete. If the ready-mix driver adds water on site, know that every gallon added per yard reduces strength by roughly 150-200 PSI.

For small jobs, bagged concrete: an 80-lb bag of Quikrete makes 0.6 cubic feet. You need 45 bags per cubic yard. For anything over 2 yards, order ready-mix from a batch plant — it's cheaper and more consistent.

### Curing Times
Concrete reaches about 70% of its design strength in 7 days and full design strength in 28 days at 70 degrees F. Cold weather slows curing dramatically. Below 50 degrees F, curing nearly stops. Below 40 degrees F, water in the mix can freeze and destroy the concrete.

Hot weather is tricky too. Above 90 degrees F, concrete sets too fast and can crack from rapid moisture loss. Use retarder admixtures, mist the surface, and get it covered with wet burlap or curing compound as soon as you can finish it.

Minimum cure time before loading: don't drive on a residential driveway for 7 days minimum (14 days for heavy vehicles). Don't put forms back for walls for at least 24 hours. Footings should cure at least 48 hours before building on them, 7 days is better.

### Rebar and Reinforcement
Standard rebar for residential:
- #3 rebar (3/8"): tie wire for positioning, light reinforcement
- #4 rebar (1/2"): most common — footings, slabs, retaining walls
- #5 rebar (5/8"): larger footings, grade beams, retaining walls over 4 feet

Rebar spacing in footings: typically #4 at 12" OC both ways for a standard spread footing. For a monolithic slab foundation, two #4 bars continuous in the turned-down edge (footing portion), with #4 at 18" OC in the slab for crack control.

Cover is critical: minimum 3 inches of concrete cover over rebar that contacts soil. 2 inches for formed surfaces exposed to weather. 1-1/2" for interior slabs. Use rebar chairs or dobies to maintain proper cover — don't let rebar sit on the ground.

Fiber mesh is not a substitute for structural rebar. It helps with shrinkage cracking in slabs but provides no structural reinforcement. Use it in addition to rebar, not instead of it.

### Foundation Types
- **Slab-on-grade (monolithic):** Common in warm climates. Footing and slab poured together. Turned-down edge typically 12" wide x 12" deep below grade. Slab is 4" thick minimum. Requires compacted fill, vapor barrier (10 mil poly minimum), and proper drainage.
- **Stem wall (crawl space):** Footing poured first, then stem wall on top. Footing width is typically twice the wall width (8" wall = 16" footing). Crawl space needs ventilation (1 sq ft per 150 sq ft of floor area) unless encapsulated.
- **Full basement:** 8" or 10" poured walls or CMU block. Waterproofing on the exterior is essential — not just damp-proofing. Use a drainage board and footing drain (4" perforated pipe in gravel) connected to daylight or sump.
- **Pier and beam:** Poured piers or CMU piers on footings with a wood beam structure above. Pier spacing depends on beam size — typically 6-8 feet for standard floor loads.

### Crack Diagnosis
Not all cracks are created equal:
- **Hairline cracks (less than 1/16"):** Shrinkage cracks — cosmetic only. Seal with elastomeric caulk if in a basement wall. Normal in slabs.
- **Vertical cracks in foundation walls:** Usually shrinkage or minor settling. Concerning if wider than 1/8" or if they're growing. Monitor with pencil marks and dates.
- **Horizontal cracks in foundation walls:** Serious. This indicates lateral pressure from soil — the wall is bowing inward. Needs engineering evaluation. Carbon fiber straps or wall anchors may be needed.
- **Stair-step cracks in block walls:** Settling. Usually at corners or near openings. If the crack follows mortar joints in a stair pattern, one side of the foundation is settling more than the other.
- **Slab cracks with vertical displacement (one side higher than the other):** Structural movement. Needs evaluation — could be soil issues, water erosion under the slab, or footing failure.

## Roofing

### Shingle Installation
Asphalt shingles (3-tab or architectural) are the standard residential roof covering. Architectural (dimensional) shingles are preferred now — thicker, better wind resistance, longer warranty (typically 30-50 years).

Installation sequence:
1. Install drip edge on eaves first (metal, min 2" face)
2. Install ice and water shield in cold climates — minimum 24" past the interior wall line (code requirement in ice dam regions). In warm climates, a single row at the eaves is still good practice.
3. Install synthetic underlayment (Deck Armor, FeltBuster, etc.) over the entire deck. Lap horizontally 4" minimum, 6" at hips and valleys. Old-school 15-lb felt works but tears easier and wrinkles in heat.
4. Install drip edge on rakes (over the underlayment)
5. Snap chalk lines for shingle courses — 5" exposure for standard architectural shingles
6. Install starter strip along eaves and rakes
7. Begin first course at the eave. Offset each subsequent course by 6" (for standard architectural) so cutouts don't align.

Nailing: 4 nails per shingle in standard wind zones, 6 nails in high wind zones (above 110 mph design speed). Nail placement is in the nailing strip — about 1" above the cutout line. High nailing (too high on the shingle) is the number one cause of shingle blow-offs. Low nailing (through the exposure area) causes visible nail heads and leaks.

### Flashing
Flashing failures cause more roof leaks than any other single issue. Key flashing locations:
- **Valleys:** Closed-cut valley (overlay) or open metal valley (26-gauge min). Open valleys with W-shaped metal are most durable.
- **Wall-to-roof:** Step flashing at sidewalls — each piece laps the one below by at least 2 inches. Kickout flashing at the bottom where a roof meets a sidewall is critical to prevent water from running behind siding.
- **Chimneys:** Base flashing, step flashing on sides, counter flashing embedded in mortar joints, and a cricket (saddle) on the uphill side of any chimney wider than 30".
- **Pipe boots:** Rubber boots over plumbing vents. Use quality boots (Oatey, Perma-Boot) — cheap ones crack in 5 years. In cold climates, consider metal pipe boots.
- **Ridge vent:** Must be backed by off-ridge baffles so wind-driven rain can't blow in. If you see staining on the attic sheathing near the ridge, the ridge vent may need baffled replacement.

### Ice Dam Prevention
Ice dams happen when heat from the attic melts snow on the roof, water runs to the cold eaves, and refreezes. The fix is air sealing and insulation in the attic — not heat cables (those are a band-aid).

Proper prevention: R-49 attic insulation minimum in cold climates (zones 5-8), all penetrations sealed (can lights, plumbing vents, electrical boxes, attic hatches), and proper soffit-to-ridge ventilation (1:150 or 1:300 ratio with balanced intake and exhaust). Every rafter bay needs an insulation baffle (Proper Vent, AccuVent) to maintain the ventilation channel from soffit to ridge.

## Insulation

### R-Values by Climate Zone
The IRC sets minimum insulation values by climate zone:
- **Zone 1 (South Florida, Hawaii):** Ceiling R-30, Wall R-13, Floor R-13
- **Zone 2 (Gulf Coast, South Texas):** Ceiling R-38, Wall R-13, Floor R-13
- **Zone 3 (Southeast, Desert SW):** Ceiling R-38, Wall R-20 or 13+5ci, Floor R-19
- **Zone 4 (Mid-Atlantic, Pacific NW):** Ceiling R-49, Wall R-20 or 13+5ci, Floor R-19
- **Zone 5 (Northern US):** Ceiling R-49, Wall R-20 or 13+10ci, Floor R-30
- **Zone 6 (Northern border):** Ceiling R-49, Wall R-20+5ci or 13+10ci, Floor R-30
- **Zone 7-8 (Alaska, far north):** Ceiling R-49, Wall R-20+10ci or 13+15ci, Floor R-38

(ci = continuous insulation on the exterior of the sheathing)

### Insulation Types
- **Fiberglass batts (R-3.2 per inch):** Cheapest option. Works fine if installed perfectly — no gaps, no compression, cut precisely around wires and pipes. Kraft-faced goes with paper toward the warm-in-winter side. In reality, batts almost always have gaps, which kills performance. R-13 batt in a 2x4 wall, R-19 or R-21 in a 2x6 wall.
- **Blown-in fiberglass (R-2.5 to R-4.2 per inch depending on density):** Better coverage than batts for attics. Needs a minimum depth — typically 14-16 inches for R-49 in an attic. Install depth rulers and don't let the crew under-blow.
- **Blown-in cellulose (R-3.2 to R-3.8 per inch):** Dense-packed cellulose in walls is excellent — R-13 in a 2x4 cavity, fills all gaps. In attics, it settles about 20% over time, so over-blow by 20%. It's treated with borate for fire and pest resistance.
- **Closed-cell spray foam (R-6.5 to R-7 per inch):** The gold standard for air sealing and insulation in one step. Also acts as a vapor barrier at 2" thickness. Expensive — roughly 3-4x the cost of fiberglass. 3" in a 2x4 wall gets you R-20 plus an air seal. Great for rim joists, crawl spaces, and cathedral ceilings.
- **Open-cell spray foam (R-3.6 to R-3.8 per inch):** Cheaper than closed-cell, good air sealer, but absorbs water — not for below-grade or exterior applications. Fills the entire cavity. Still needs a vapor retarder in cold climates (Zone 5+).
- **Rigid foam board:** XPS (extruded polystyrene, pink/blue board, R-5 per inch), EPS (expanded polystyrene, white beadboard, R-3.8 per inch), polyiso (foil-faced, R-6.5 per inch but drops in cold weather). Polyiso below about 40 degrees F loses R-value significantly. For exterior continuous insulation below grade, use XPS.

## Drywall

### Hanging
1/2" drywall for most walls and ceilings with 16" OC framing. 5/8" for ceilings with 24" OC framing (1/2" will sag). 5/8" Type X (fire-rated) required for garage walls and ceilings adjacent to living space, and for any fire-rated assembly.

Hang ceilings first, then walls. Butt the wall sheets tight to the ceiling board — it supports the edge. Sheets run horizontally on walls (fewer joints to tape on an 8' wall with horizontal 12' sheets). Stagger joints so no four corners meet at one point. Leave 1/4" gap at the floor — never let drywall contact the floor (moisture wicking).

Screw spacing: every 12" on ceilings, every 16" on walls. Screws should dimple the paper slightly without breaking through. If you break the paper face, add another screw 2" away — the broken one has no holding power.

For curved walls, use two layers of 1/4" flexible drywall or score the back of 1/2" board at 1" intervals.

### Taping and Finishing
Three coats minimum for a good finish:
1. **First coat (tape coat):** Embed paper tape in setting-type compound (hot mud — 45-minute or 90-minute). Fill screw heads. Setting compound is stronger and shrinks less than pre-mixed. Mesh tape is acceptable only on butt joints with setting compound — never use mesh on flat joints with pre-mixed mud; it will crack.
2. **Second coat (fill/block coat):** After the first coat is dry and sanded lightly, apply pre-mixed all-purpose compound. Feather out 6-8 inches beyond the tape. Fill screw heads flush. Let dry completely.
3. **Third coat (skim/finish coat):** Lightweight all-purpose or topping compound. Feather out 10-12 inches. This coat is thin — you're just filling imperfections and feathering edges.

Sand between coats with 120-150 grit. Final sand with 150-220 grit. Use a sanding light (raking light) to check for imperfections before priming — shadows reveal everything.

Common drywall cracks: a crack at a corner where the wall meets the ceiling almost always indicates truss uplift (the bottom chord of the truss lifts in winter). Fix with a floating corner detail — don't screw the ceiling drywall within 16" of the wall. Cracks above doors or windows usually mean the header is undersized or the framing has settled.

## Windows and Doors

### Rough Opening Sizing
Standard rough opening = window/door unit size + 1/2" on each side and top (1" total width, 1/2" total height). So a 36" x 48" window needs a 37" x 48-1/2" rough opening. But always check the manufacturer's spec sheet — every brand is slightly different. Anderson, Pella, Marvin, and Milgard all publish rough opening guides.

For doors: a 36" pre-hung door (the most common exterior door) needs a rough opening of 38" wide x 82-1/2" tall. Interior pre-hung doors: 34" door = 36" RO width, 30" door = 32" RO width. Height is typically 82-1/2" for standard 80" doors.

### Installation
Window installation procedure:
1. Apply self-adhesive flashing tape (Flex Wrap, Zip System tape) to the rough opening sill — lap it over the bottom edge and onto the house wrap/WRB
2. Apply sill pan flashing or sloped sill dam (some codes require a metal sill pan)
3. Dry-fit the window to verify it fits. Check for square with a diagonal measurement — diagonals should be within 1/8" of each other
4. Apply sealant (OSI Quad, Big Stretch) to the back of the nailing flange on the sides and top ONLY — never seal the bottom; water needs to drain out
5. Set the window, check for level and plumb. Shim with composite or cedar shims at the jamb behind strike plates and hinge locations
6. Nail through the flange with 2" roofing nails or screws every 6-8 inches
7. Flash the sides (over the nailing flange, under the house wrap) and then the top (over the house wrap)

The bottom must be able to drain. If you seal the bottom flange, you're creating a bathtub that holds water against the sill.

## Deck Building

### Posts and Footings
Deck footings must extend below the frost line — 36" in most northern states, 42" in Minnesota, 12" in the South. Footing size depends on tributary load: typically 16" to 24" diameter, 8-12" thick, with a post anchor (Simpson ABU, CB series) embedded in the concrete.

Post sizing: 6x6 posts are standard for decks over 4 feet above grade or with long beam spans. 4x4 posts are acceptable for low decks (under 4 feet) with beam spans under 6 feet — but many builders just use 6x6 everywhere to be safe. Post-to-beam connection: use a Simpson post cap (BC series), not just toenailing.

### Joist Sizing
Based on #2 grade SPF or Doug Fir, 40 psf live load:
- 2x6 joists at 16" OC: maximum span 9'-9"
- 2x8 joists at 16" OC: maximum span 13'-1"
- 2x10 joists at 16" OC: maximum span 16'-5"
- 2x12 joists at 16" OC: maximum span 20'-0"

These are for standard residential decks. For hot tubs or other heavy loads, you need engineering — a hot tub can weigh 4000-6000 lbs when full. That's like parking a car on your deck.

### Ledger Board Attachment
The ledger board connection to the house is the most critical structural connection on a deck. More deck collapses are caused by ledger failure than any other single issue.

Requirements per IRC:
- 1/2" lag screws or through-bolts minimum, staggered pattern
- Spacing per IRC Table R507.9.1.3: typically 12-19 inches depending on joist span and species
- Must be into the band joist (rim board), not just into siding or sheathing
- Flashing: Z-flashing or self-adhesive membrane above the ledger, tucked up behind the siding/WRB. Water must not be able to get behind the ledger.
- Spacers: some codes now require 1/2" standoff spacers between the ledger and the house to allow drainage. Simpson and Maine Deck Brackets make purpose-built spacers.
- No nails. Never attach a ledger with nails only. This is a life-safety connection.

## Moisture Management

### The Rain Screen Concept
Modern building science centers on four layers of moisture control:
1. **Water management (rain screen):** Keep bulk water out with a drainage plane behind the cladding. A 3/8" to 3/4" air gap between the siding and the WRB allows water to drain and air to dry.
2. **Air barrier:** Prevent air-transported moisture. Taped sheathing (Zip System) or a properly detailed WRB (Tyvek, Typar). Every seam, every penetration sealed.
3. **Vapor control:** Slow vapor diffusion through the wall. Interior vapor retarder (kraft paper, smart membranes like Certainteed MemBrain) in cold climates. In hot-humid climates, the vapor drive reverses — keep the vapor retarder toward the exterior or use a smart membrane.
4. **Thermal control:** Insulation — covered above.

### House Wrap and WRB
House wrap (weather-resistive barrier) goes over the sheathing and behind the siding. Lap horizontal seams shingle-style (upper over lower). Tape all seams with manufacturer-approved tape. Integrate with window and door flashing.

Common mistakes: reverse lapping (lower over upper, creating a shelf for water), not taping seams, not integrating with window flashing properly, and using staples that create thousands of tiny holes. Cap staples are better than regular staples.

Zip System sheathing combines the WRB with the sheathing — tape the seams with Zip tape and you have a structural panel and air/water barrier in one. Great product if installed correctly. The tape must be rolled with a J-roller for proper adhesion.

### Weep Holes and Drainage
Brick veneer requires weep holes at the base of the wall — every 24" to 33" along the bottom course, above all flashing, and above all lintels. Use open head joints, plastic weep inserts, or cotton rope wicks. Behind the brick, a 1" air gap and through-wall flashing (copper, stainless, or self-adhesive membrane) direct water back out through the weep holes.

Stucco (traditional three-coat) requires a drainage plane behind it — two layers of Grade D building paper or one layer of WRB plus a drainage mat. Weep screeds at the bottom of stucco walls are code-required and must be a minimum of 4" above grade (6" is better).

Never cover or seal weep holes. Homeowners and painters do this all the time, and it traps moisture in the wall, leading to rot, mold, and structural failure.

### Vapor Barriers in Crawl Spaces
Crawl space moisture control: install a minimum 6 mil poly vapor barrier on the ground (10 mil is better — reinforced like Stego Wrap). Lap seams 12 inches, tape with waterproof tape. Extend 6 inches up the stem wall and attach with concrete fasteners or mastic. In an encapsulated (sealed) crawl space, the poly goes all the way up the walls, seams are taped, vents are closed, and a dehumidifier maintains humidity below 60%.

A vented crawl space in a humid climate (Southeast US) is a moisture disaster. The warm, humid outside air enters the cool crawl space, hits the dew point, and condensation forms on everything. Encapsulation is the solution in these climates. In dry climates, vented crawl spaces can work fine.

## Pro Tips From the Field

1. **Measure twice, cut once** is a cliche because it's true. But also: measure from the same reference point every time. Cumulative measuring errors add up fast.

2. **Square, level, and plumb** are the foundation of everything. Check after every step. A wall that's 1/4" out of plumb at the bottom is 1/2" out at the top. That compounds through every floor.

3. **Water always wins.** Every building detail should answer the question: "Where does the water go?" If you can't answer that, you have a problem.

4. **If something doesn't fit, don't force it.** Find out why. Forcing a window into an out-of-square rough opening means it won't operate properly and it'll leak.

5. **Keep your tools sharp and your saw blades clean.** A dull circular saw blade doesn't just cut poorly — it makes the motor work harder, heats up the material, and increases the risk of kickback.

6. **On every job, the first thing you build is the relationship with the inspector.** Ask questions. Show your work. Make it easy for them to approve it. Fighting with inspectors costs you time and money.

7. **Treated lumber needs proper fasteners.** ACQ-treated wood is corrosive to regular galvanized fasteners. Use hot-dip galvanized (HDG), stainless steel, or manufacturer-approved fasteners (Simpson Strong-Tie publishes compatibility charts). Regular electro-galvanized nails will corrode in months.

8. **Document everything.** Take photos before you close up walls. Photo the rebar before the pour. Photo the flashing before the siding goes on. When a callback comes in 3 years from now, those photos are worth their weight in gold.
