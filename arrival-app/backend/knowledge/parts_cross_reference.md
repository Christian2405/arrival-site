### Dual vs Single Run Capacitors

**Dual run capacitor:** Has three terminals — C (common), HERM (compressor), and FAN. One cap serves both the compressor motor and the condenser fan motor. This is what 90% of residential condensing units use.

**Single run capacitor:** Has two terminals. Used when the compressor and fan motor each have their own separate capacitor, or for single-motor applications like indoor blower motors.

**When you need each:**
- Replacing a dual cap: Use a dual cap with matching specs. In a pinch, you can use two single caps wired to the same common.
- Indoor blower motor: Usually a single oval or round cap, commonly 5 µF, 7.5 µF, or 10 µF at 370V or 440V.
- Single-stage compressor with separate fan cap: Use individual single caps.

### Voltage Ratings: 370V vs 440V

**370V capacitors** are the older standard. **440V capacitors** are rated for higher voltage tolerance.

**Key rule:** A 440V cap can always replace a 370V cap of the same µF rating. A 370V cap should NOT replace a 440V cap.

- 440V caps run cooler and last longer in 208-230V systems
- Always carry 440V — they work everywhere a 370V would
- Cost difference is negligible, reliability improvement is significant
- If unit data plate says 440V, do NOT downgrade to 370V

### Start Capacitors and Hard Start Kits

Start capacitors provide a temporary boost to compressor starting torque. They always require a potential relay or PTCR (positive temperature coefficient relay) to disconnect after startup.

**Common hard start kits:**

| Kit | Type | Capacitor Range | Application |
|-----|------|----------------|-------------|
| Supco SPP6 | PTCR | 88-108 µF 250V | Up to 3-ton, single phase |
| Supco SPP7 | PTCR | 108-130 µF 250V | 3-5 ton, single phase |
| Supco SPP8 | PTCR | 145-175 µF 250V | 4-5+ ton, single phase |
| 5-2-1 Compressor Saver CSR-U1 | Potential relay | 43-53 µF 370V | 1-3 ton |
| 5-2-1 Compressor Saver CSR-U2 | Potential relay | 64-77 µF 370V | 2-3.5 ton |
| 5-2-1 Compressor Saver CSR-U3 | Potential relay | 88-108 µF 370V | 3-5 ton |
| Kickstart KS1 | PTCR | Universal | Up to 5 ton |

**PTCR kits** are two-wire, simple install, but less precise disconnect timing. **Potential relay kits** are three-wire, more reliable disconnect, better for larger compressors.

**When to install a hard start kit:**
- Compressor struggling to start (high LRA, slow startup)
- Low voltage situations (long line runs, rural areas)
- Compressor drawing close to RLA on startup
- Scroll compressor on short-cycle protection trips
- Customer reports lights dimming when AC kicks on

### How to Read Capacitor Labels

Every capacitor label tells you what you need:

- **µF (microfarads):** The capacitance value. Must match within tolerance.
- **Tolerance:** Usually +/- 5% or +/- 6%. A 35 µF cap reading 33-37 µF is still in spec.
- **Voltage rating:** Maximum voltage the cap can handle. Match or exceed, never go below.
- **50/60 Hz:** Frequency rating. Standard for North American power.
- **Dual cap terminals:** C = Common, HERM = Hermetic (compressor), FAN = Fan motor.

**Testing a capacitor:**
- Meter must be set to capacitance (µF) mode
- Discharge cap first (short terminals with insulated screwdriver)
- Read µF and compare to label rating
- Replace if reading is more than 5-10% below rated value
- A weak cap will cause high amp draw, hard starting, or motor overheating

| Coil Voltage | Application |
|-------------|-------------|
| 24V AC | Standard residential — thermostat signal energizes coil via transformer |
| 120V AC | Some commercial rooftop units, older residential |
| 208/240V AC | Commercial systems, line-voltage controlled equipment |

**Always verify coil voltage before replacing.** A 24V coil on a 120V circuit will burn instantly. A 120V coil on a 24V circuit will not pull in.

### When to Upgrade Contactor Rating

- Pitted or welded contacts: Replace, consider going one size up
- Compressor RLA exceeds 80% of contactor rating: Upgrade
- Frequent contactor failures on same unit: Upgrade amp rating
- Adding a hard start kit to a borderline system: Consider upgrading
- Never downgrade amp rating — always match or exceed OEM spec

### Motor Details — What to Match

**Critical specs to match when replacing a blower motor:**
1. **Horsepower** — Must match or be within one step of OEM
2. **Voltage** — 115V or 208-230V. Must match.
3. **RPM** — 1075 RPM is standard for direct-drive blowers. 1625 RPM for belt-drive.
4. **Rotation** — CW or CCW as viewed from lead end. Many universals are reversible (swap two leads).
5. **Shaft diameter** — 1/2" is standard residential. 5/8" on some larger motors.
6. **Shaft length** — Must be long enough for the blower wheel set screw. 4" to 7" common.
7. **Frame size** — 48-frame is standard residential furnace blower.
8. **Speed taps** — Match the number of speeds (3-speed or 4-speed typical).

**Mounting types:**
- **Belly band:** Adjustable metal strap around motor body. Most common for universal replacements.
- **Resilient mount (rubber ring):** Ring around the motor with rubber grommets. Quieter. Common on Carrier, Trane.
- **Stud mount:** Threaded studs on the motor endbell. Older units.
- **Direct drive:** Motor shaft connects directly to blower wheel.
- **Belt drive:** Motor uses a pulley and belt to turn the blower. Mostly commercial now.

### Common Gas Valve Failure Modes

| Symptom | Likely Cause | Action |
|---------|-------------|--------|
| No gas flow, valve does not open | Bad coil, no 24V signal, or stuck valve | Check 24V at valve terminals. If 24V present and no click/opening, replace valve. |
| Gas smell but no ignition | Valve opens but gas pressure low or igniter failure | Check manifold pressure. If valve output is weak, replace. |
| Intermittent operation | Sticking valve, intermittent coil, or loose wiring | Tap test — if tapping valve makes it work, replace it. |
| Valve won't close | Debris in valve seat, or internal failure | Immediate replace — gas leak hazard. |
| LP conversion issues | Wrong orifice, regulator not adjusted | Verify LP kit installed correctly, check manifold pressure (3.5" WC natural, 10" WC LP). |

### MERV Ratings — What They Filter and Airflow Impact

| MERV | Filters | Best For | Airflow Impact |
|------|---------|----------|---------------|
| 1-4 | Pollen, dust mites, large debris | Minimal protection, equipment only | Negligible |
| 5-8 | Mold spores, pet dander, dust | Standard residential. Best balance of filtration and airflow. | Low |
| 9-12 | Fine dust, Legionella, auto emissions | Allergy sufferers, better air quality | Moderate |
| 13-16 | Bacteria, smoke, sneeze droplets | Hospital-grade, severe allergies | High — can restrict airflow on standard systems |

### Filter Recommendations by Situation

| Situation | Recommended MERV | Reasoning |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------|
| Standard residential, no allergies | MERV 8 | Best balance. Catches most irritants without starving the system. |
| Allergies or pets in home | MERV 11 | Better particle capture. Check static pressure — some systems handle it, some do not. |
| Severe allergies, respiratory issues | MERV 13 | Fine particle filtration. Verify system can handle the pressure drop. May need filter rack upgrade. |
| Older system, undersized ductwork | MERV 8 max | Higher MERV will kill airflow and cause coil icing or heat exchanger overheating. |
| New high-efficiency system, good ductwork | MERV 11-13 | Modern ECM blowers can compensate for higher restriction. |

**Always measure static pressure** when upgrading filter MERV rating. Total external static should not exceed the equipment rating (typically 0.5" WC on residential systems). A MERV 13 in a system designed for MERV 8 can cause frozen coils, cracked heat exchangers, and premature blower failure.

## Water Heater Parts

### Anode Rods

Anode rods protect the tank from corrosion through sacrificial galvanic action. They are the primary maintenance item that extends tank life.

| Spec | Standard Residential |
|------|---------------------|
| Thread size | 3/4" NPT (hex head) |
| Diameter | 0.75" (3/4") standard, 0.9" (magnesium or aluminum) |
| Length (40-gal tank) | 33-44 inches |
| Length (50-gal tank) | 42-46 inches |
| Length (short/lowboy) | 24-33 inches |

| Material | Pros | Cons | Best For |
|----------|------|------|----------|
| Magnesium | Best corrosion protection, most reactive | Depletes faster in hard water, can cause rotten egg smell with certain bacteria | Soft water areas, standard municipal water |
| Aluminum/zinc | Lasts longer in hard water, reduces sulfur smell | Less cathodic protection than magnesium | Hard water, well water, areas with sulfur smell |
| Powered (impressed current) | Never depletes, longest life | Requires power, more expensive | Customers who want set-and-forget, commercial |

**Flexible anode rods** are segmented and flex through tight spaces — use when headroom above the tank is limited (closet installs, low ceilings). They provide the same protection as rigid rods.

### Water Heater Elements

| Spec | Standard Residential |
|------|---------------------|
| Wattage | 4500W (standard), 3500W (some 30-gal), 5500W (some 65+ gal) |
| Voltage | 240V (standard residential), 120V (some small point-of-use) |
| Thread type | Screw-in (1" NPT hex, most common) or bolt-in (4-bolt flange, older units) |
| Length | 10-14 inches depending on tank diameter |

**Screw-in vs bolt-in:**
- Screw-in: 1" NPT hex head, requires element wrench (1-1/2" socket). 90% of residential tanks.
- Bolt-in: 4-bolt flange mounting. Older tanks, some commercial. Cannot convert bolt-in to screw-in without adapter plate.

**Element types:**
- Copper sheath: Standard, good heat transfer, but scales up in hard water
- Stainless steel (incoloy): Resists corrosion and scale, lasts longer in hard water
- Low-watt density (fold-back): Lower surface temperature, less scale buildup — best for hard water areas
- Ultra-low-watt density: Maximum surface area, minimum scaling — commercial or severe hard water

**Common element part numbers:**
- Camco 02142: 4500W 240V screw-in, 14" copper — universal standard
- Camco 02143: 4500W 240V screw-in, 12" copper
- Reliance 9000405-045: 4500W 240V screw-in, incoloy
- AO Smith 9001829-045: 4500W 240V, OEM equivalent

### Water Heater Thermostats

Standard residential electric water heaters use Therm-O-Disc brand thermostats — upper and lower.

| Position | Part Type | Function | Common Part Numbers |
|----------|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| Upper | Thermostat with high-limit (ECO) | Controls upper element, has manual reset high-limit at 170-180F | Therm-O-Disc 59T, Camco 08143 |
| Lower | Thermostat only | Controls lower element, no high-limit | Therm-O-Disc 59T, Camco 08123 |

**How they work together:**
The upper thermostat has priority. When the upper tank is cold, the upper element runs. Once the upper tank reaches setpoint, the upper thermostat switches power to the lower element via an internal transfer switch. Both elements never run simultaneously (standard residential wiring).

**High-limit (ECO) reset:** The red button on the upper thermostat. If it trips, press to reset. If it trips repeatedly, check for: crossed wiring (elements running simultaneously), failed thermostat, grounded element, or sediment-insulated element overheating.

### T&P (Temperature and Pressure) Relief Valves

| Spec | Standard Residential |
|------|---------------------|
| Pressure rating | 150 PSI |
| Temperature rating | 210 degrees F |
| Inlet thread | 3/4" NPTM (male) |
| Discharge | 3/4" NPTF (female) or built-in extension |
| BTU rating | Must meet or exceed water heater BTU input |

**Common T&P valve part numbers:**
- Watts 100XL: 3/4" 150 PSI/210F — most common universal replacement
- Cash Acme/Reliance FVX-5C: 3/4" 150 PSI/210F
- Rheem SP20060: OEM Rheem/Ruud replacement
- AO Smith 9001830-045: OEM AO Smith replacement

**T&P valve rules:**
- Never plug or cap a T&P valve. This is a life-safety device.
- Discharge pipe must run downhill to a safe drain point (6 inches above floor or to exterior)
- No reducer fittings in the discharge pipe — full 3/4" all the way
- No valves in the discharge pipe
- Replace if dripping continuously (usually means thermal expansion or high water pressure — fix root cause first)
- Test annually by lifting the lever — water should flow and stop when released

**Diagnosing gas control valve issues:**
- No hot water, no pilot: Check thermopile/thermocouple output. Should be 300+ mV (thermopile) or 25+ mV (thermocouple) under load. If voltage is good but valve will not open, replace gas control.

- Water temperature too hot: Check thermostat setting. If set correctly and water is scalding, the gas control valve thermostat has failed — replace.
