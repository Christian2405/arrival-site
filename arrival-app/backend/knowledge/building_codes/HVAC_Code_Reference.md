# HVAC Code Reference — Field Quick Reference

## Gas Piping

### Pipe Sizing (Natural Gas, 0.5 PSI or Less)
Gas pipe sizing depends on total BTU load AND total pipe run length. Use manufacturer's sizing tables or code tables.

Common residential sizing (approximate, under 2 PSI, natural gas):
| Pipe Size | 10 ft run | 20 ft run | 50 ft run | 100 ft run |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|------------|
| 1/2 inch | 175,000 BTU | 120,000 BTU | 75,000 BTU | 52,000 BTU |
| 3/4 inch | 360,000 BTU | 250,000 BTU | 155,000 BTU | 110,000 BTU |
| 1 inch | 680,000 BTU | 470,000 BTU | 295,000 BTU | 205,000 BTU |
| 1-1/4 inch | 1,220,000 BTU | 850,000 BTU | 535,000 BTU | 375,000 BTU |

**Rule of thumb for residential:**
- Single furnace (up to 100,000 BTU): 3/4 inch black iron if run under 50 feet
- Tankless water heater: 3/4 inch minimum, some high-output units need 1 inch
- Total house with furnace + water heater + range: 1 inch main, branch to each appliance

### Gas Piping Requirements
- Approved materials: Black iron (steel), CSST (corrugated stainless steel tubing), copper (some jurisdictions)
- CSST must be bonded to grounding electrode system per manufacturer instructions
- Sediment trap (drip leg) required at each appliance: minimum 3 inch nipple + cap
- Gas shutoff valve: required at each appliance, within 6 feet, in same room, accessible without tools
- No gas piping in ducts, chimneys, elevator shafts, or concealed spaces without proper protection
- Underground gas piping: minimum 12 inches deep for residential, 18 inches under driveways
- Test before covering: 15 PSI air test for minimum 15 minutes (varies by jurisdiction)

### Flex Connector Rules
- Maximum length: 3 feet in most jurisdictions (6 feet in some)
- Cannot pass through walls, floors, or ceilings
- One connector per appliance (no daisy-chaining)
- Must be accessible — no concealed connectors

## Furnace Installation

### Clearances to Combustibles
Follow manufacturer's installation instructions — they supersede code minimums. Typical:
- Sides: 1 inch minimum (check rating plate)
- Front: working space for service access (varies, typically 24-30 inches)
- Top: check manufacturer, varies widely. 6 inches minimum for some, 1 inch for others
- Flue pipe: 6 inches from combustibles for single-wall, 1 inch for Type B vent

### Combustion Air
Gas appliances in confined spaces need combustion air supply:
- **Confined space:** Less than 50 cubic feet per 1,000 BTU/hr input of all appliances combined
- Two permanent openings to outside or to larger interior space
- Each opening: minimum 1 square inch per 4,000 BTU/hr (if direct to outside)
- Each opening: minimum 1 square inch per 1,000 BTU/hr (if to interior space)
- Openings: one within 12 inches of ceiling, one within 12 inches of floor

### Condensate Drainage
- 90%+ AFUE furnaces produce condensate — must be drained
- Condensate line: minimum 3/4 inch PVC or approved material
- Must pitch toward drain or pump
- Cannot discharge to ground surface where it can freeze
- Neutralizer recommended but not always required
- Drain line clogging is the #1 service call for high-efficiency furnaces

### Return Air
- Return air cannot be taken from: bathrooms, kitchens, garages, mechanical rooms
- No return air ducts in concrete slab unless approved material
- Transfer grilles or jump ducts acceptable for bedroom return air

## Venting Requirements

### Categories of Venting
- **Category I:** Non-positive vent pressure, non-condensing (most 80% furnaces) — use Type B vent
- **Category II:** Non-positive vent pressure, condensing — rare
- **Category III:** Positive vent pressure, non-condensing — stainless steel or AL29-4C vent
- **Category IV:** Positive vent pressure, condensing (most 90%+ furnaces) — PVC or CPVC vent

### Type B Vent (Category I)
- 1 inch clearance to combustibles
- Must terminate above roof per height table (based on distance from roof peak)
- Minimum 5 feet of vertical vent rise
- Cannot be installed in exterior wall chases that don't maintain vent temperature
- No screen on termination cap (can cause blockage)

### PVC/CPVC Vent (Category IV — 90%+ Furnaces)
- Schedule 40 PVC or CPVC — follow manufacturer's approved materials list
- Horizontal runs: slope back toward furnace (1/4 inch per foot minimum) for condensate drainage
- Termination: 12 inches above grade, 12 inches above anticipated snow line
- Clearance from windows/doors/air intakes: varies (typically 4-12 feet depending on jurisdiction)
- Intake and exhaust must be separated per manufacturer specs (usually 12-36 inches)

### Common Venting Issues
- Orphaned water heater: when a furnace is replaced with high-efficiency, the old water heater left on the original vent may not draft properly (flue is now oversized for single appliance)
- Solution: resize the vent liner, add a power vent, or replace the water heater

## Ductwork Requirements

### Duct Materials
- Galvanized sheet metal (most common)
- Fiberglass duct board (insulated)
- Flex duct: maximum 25 feet per run, must be supported every 4 feet, must be fully extended (not bunched up)
- Flex duct connections: mechanical fastener + mastic or approved tape (no duct tape — it fails)

### Duct Sizing
Rule of thumb for residential:
- 400 CFM per ton of cooling
- Main trunk: size for total CFM
- Branch runs: size for room CFM requirement
- 6 inch round: ~100 CFM
- 7 inch round: ~150 CFM
- 8 inch round: ~200 CFM
- 10 inch round: ~350 CFM
- 12 inch round: ~550 CFM

### Duct Sealing
- All joints and seams must be sealed with mastic or approved tape
- Return side leaks pull in unconditioned air (garage fumes, attic insulation fibers)
- Supply side leaks waste conditioned air
- Duct leakage testing may be required (varies by jurisdiction and energy code)

### Duct Insulation
- Supply ducts in unconditioned spaces: minimum R-6 insulation (R-8 in many jurisdictions)
- Return ducts in unconditioned spaces: minimum R-6
- Ducts in conditioned spaces: insulation not required
- Vapor barrier required on exterior of insulation in cooling climates

## Refrigerant Line Requirements

### Line Set Sizing
- Follow manufacturer specifications exactly — wrong line set size = poor performance
- Suction line: larger diameter (gas returns to compressor)
- Liquid line: smaller diameter (liquid goes to evaporator)
- Common residential: 3/8 inch liquid, 3/4 inch suction for 2-3 ton systems

### Line Set Installation
- Insulate suction line (cold line) — prevents condensation and efficiency loss
- Liquid line insulation optional but recommended
- Support every 6-10 feet on horizontal runs
- Protect from physical damage
- Minimum bend radius: 3x pipe diameter for ACR tubing
- Braze joints with nitrogen flowing (prevents oxidation inside tubing)
- Pressure test: typically 400-500 PSI nitrogen for leak check
- Evacuate to 500 microns or below before charging

### Refrigerant Handling
- EPA 608 certification required to purchase or handle refrigerant
- Cannot vent refrigerant to atmosphere (Clean Air Act violation, heavy fines)
- Must recover refrigerant before opening system for service
- R-410A: standard since 2010, operating pressures ~120/350 PSI
- R-454B (A2L): replacing R-410A in new equipment starting 2025
- R-22: phased out, no longer manufactured — retrofit or replace system

## Thermostat and Controls

### Thermostat Wire
- 18 AWG typical for residential (18/5, 18/8 common)
- Low-voltage (24V) — does not require conduit
- Cannot share conduit with line-voltage wiring
- "C" wire (common wire) needed for most smart thermostats — run 18/5 minimum

### Equipment Disconnect
- Outdoor condensing unit: disconnect switch required within sight of unit and readily accessible
- Must be lockable in the off position
- Can be a non-fused disconnect or fused disconnect per manufacturer's MCA/MOCP ratings

### MCA and MOCP
- **MCA (Minimum Circuit Ampacity):** Minimum wire size = MCA × 1.0 (round up to standard wire size)
- **MOCP (Maximum Overcurrent Protection):** Maximum breaker size allowed
- Always check the equipment nameplate — these override any rules of thumb

## Energy Code Requirements

### IECC / State Energy Code
- Duct leakage testing: many jurisdictions require 4 CFM25 per 100 sq ft or less
- HVAC equipment efficiency minimums (SEER2, HSPF2, AFUE) vary by region
- Manual J load calculation required for new construction/major renovations
- Manual D duct design recommended/required
- Programmable or smart thermostat required in most new construction
- Insulation requirements affect HVAC sizing — better insulation = smaller system

### Regional Equipment Minimums (2023+ Federal Standards)
- **North region:** Air conditioners: 14 SEER2 / Heat pumps: 15 SEER2, 8.8 HSPF2 / Furnaces: 80% AFUE
- **South region:** Air conditioners: 15 SEER2 / Heat pumps: 15 SEER2, 8.8 HSPF2 / Furnaces: 80% AFUE
- **Southwest region:** Air conditioners: 15 SEER2 (split) / Heat pumps: 15 SEER2

Note: SEER2 ratings are lower numbers than old SEER ratings for the same equipment — new test procedure.

## Permit and Inspection Notes

Most jurisdictions require permits for:
- New HVAC system installation
- System replacement (even like-for-like in many areas)
- Adding ductwork or modifying existing duct system
- Gas line installation or modification
- Installing a mini-split system

Common inspection points:
1. **Rough-in:** Ductwork, gas piping, flue venting, refrigerant lines before concealment
2. **Final:** Equipment operational, thermostat working, gas leak check, combustion analysis

## Common Code Violations

1. Flex duct too long, kinked, or unsupported
2. Missing sediment trap on gas connection
3. Improper venting termination location (too close to window or air intake)
4. Condensate line not properly sloped or trapped
5. Missing combustion air openings in confined space
6. No disconnect switch at outdoor unit
7. Return air taken from garage (carbon monoxide risk)
8. Oversized equipment (no Manual J calculation — bigger is NOT better)
9. Missing duct insulation in unconditioned spaces
10. Duct connections sealed with duct tape instead of mastic

## State-Specific Notes

- **California:** Title 24 energy standards are the strictest in the country. HERS testing required for duct leakage. All HVAC replacements require permit and Manual J calculation.
- **Florida:** Hurricane code requirements for outdoor equipment mounting and protection. High humidity climate requires careful attention to duct sealing and vapor barriers.
- **Texas:** State minimum is IRC/IMC, but many cities adopt amendments. Equipment ratings must account for extreme heat (design temperature 100°F+ in many areas).
- **Northern states (MN, WI, MI, etc.):** Furnace minimum AFUE requirements may exceed federal minimum. Freeze protection for condensate lines is critical.
- **Arizona:** Extreme heat design temperatures. Evaporative cooling (swamp coolers) allowed in some areas as alternative to AC. Desert-rated equipment recommended.

**Always verify with local AHJ.** Mechanical codes (IMC, UMC) are adopted at state level but cities and counties can add amendments.
