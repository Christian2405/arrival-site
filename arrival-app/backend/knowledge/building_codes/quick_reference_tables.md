## 4. BTU Calculation Quick Reference

### Heating Load Estimation

**Manual J is the correct method for accurate sizing. These rules of thumb are for rough estimation only.**

| Climate Zone | BTU/sq ft Factor | Example: 1,500 sq ft |
|--------------|------------------|---------------------|
| Zone 1-2 (Southern US, FL, TX coast) | 25-30 | 37,500-45,000 BTU |
| Zone 3 (Southeast, Southwest) | 30-35 | 45,000-52,500 BTU |
| Zone 4 (Mid-Atlantic, Midwest South) | 35-40 | 52,500-60,000 BTU |
| Zone 5 (Northern Midwest, Northeast) | 40-50 | 60,000-75,000 BTU |
| Zone 6-7 (Northern US, Mountains) | 50-60 | 75,000-90,000 BTU |

**Adjustments:** Add 10-20% for poor insulation. Subtract 10-15% for excellent insulation (2x6 walls, R-49 attic, triple-pane windows). Add 5-10% for high ceilings (above 8 feet). Add 10-15% for large window area.

### Cooling Load Estimation

**General rule of thumb:** 400-600 square feet per ton of cooling (1 ton = 12,000 BTU/hr).

| Climate Zone | Sq Ft per Ton | Example: 1,500 sq ft |
|--------------|---------------|---------------------|
| Zone 1-2 (Hot/humid) | 400-450 | 3.5-4.0 tons |
| Zone 3 (Warm) | 450-500 | 3.0-3.5 tons |
| Zone 4 (Moderate) | 500-550 | 2.5-3.0 tons |
| Zone 5+ (Cool) | 550-600 | 2.5-3.0 tons |

**Cooling load is more sensitive to orientation, window area, and solar gain than heating load.** A house with large west-facing windows will need significantly more cooling capacity per square foot.

### Water Heating BTU Calculation

**Formula:** BTU/hr = GPM x Temperature Rise (°F) x 500

Where:
- GPM = gallons per minute flow rate
- Temperature Rise = desired outlet temp minus inlet water temp
- 500 = constant (8.33 lbs/gal x 60 min/hr = 499.8, rounded to 500)

**Example:** Raising 40°F inlet water to 120°F at 3 GPM:
- BTU/hr = 3 x 80 x 500 = 120,000 BTU/hr

**Common water heater sizing:**
- 40-gallon tank, 40,000 BTU input: Adequate for 2-3 people
- 50-gallon tank, 40,000 BTU input: Adequate for 3-4 people
- Tankless (on-demand): Size by GPM and temperature rise. A 199,000 BTU tankless delivers approximately 4.7 GPM at an 80°F rise

## 5. Electrical Formulas

### Ohm's Law

| To Find | Formula |
|---------|---------|
| Voltage (V) | V = I x R |
| Current (I) | I = V / R |
| Resistance (R) | R = V / I |

### Power Formulas

**Single Phase:**

| To Find | Formula |
|---------|---------|
| Watts (P) | P = V x I |
| Watts (P) | P = V² / R |
| Watts (P) | P = I² x R |
| Current (I) | I = P / V |

**Three Phase:**

| To Find | Formula |
|---------|---------|
| Watts (P) | P = V x I x 1.732 x PF |
| Current (I) | I = P / (V x 1.732 x PF) |

Where PF = Power Factor (typically 0.80-0.95 for motors, use 1.0 for resistive loads).

### Voltage Drop Calculation

**Formula:** VD = (2 x K x I x D) / CM

Where:
- VD = Voltage drop (volts)
- K = Resistivity constant (12.9 for copper, 21.2 for aluminum)
- I = Current in amps
- D = One-way distance in feet
- CM = Circular mil area of the conductor

**NEC recommendation:** Maximum 3% voltage drop on the branch circuit, 5% total (feeder + branch).

## 7. Static Pressure Guidelines

### Residential HVAC Duct System

| Measurement | Target | Maximum |
|-------------|--------|---------|
| Total external static pressure (ESP) | 0.30-0.40" WC | 0.50" WC |
| Filter pressure drop (clean 1" filter) | 0.05-0.10" WC | 0.15" WC |
| Filter pressure drop (clean 4" media filter) | 0.10-0.15" WC | 0.25" WC |
| Filter pressure drop (clean HEPA) | 0.40-0.60" WC | 1.0" WC |
| Evaporator coil pressure drop (clean) | 0.10-0.20" WC | 0.30" WC |
| Supply duct system | 0.05-0.15" WC | 0.20" WC |
| Return duct system | 0.05-0.10" WC | 0.15" WC |

**Diagnostic static pressure readings:**
- High return static (above 0.15" WC): Undersized return duct, dirty filter, or restrictive filter type
- High supply static (above 0.20" WC): Undersized supply duct, too many elbows, dampers closed, or dirty coil
- High total ESP (above 0.50" WC): Reduced airflow, comfort complaints, high utility bills, potential equipment damage, frozen coils in cooling, cracked heat exchangers in heating

## 9. Common Torque Specs

### Flare Fittings (SAE 45-Degree Flare, Copper Tubing)

| Tube OD | Torque (ft-lbs) |
|---------|-----------------|
| 1/4" | 7-10 |
| 3/8" | 15-20 |
| 1/2" | 25-30 |
| 5/8" | 30-35 |
| 3/4" | 40-50 |
| 7/8" | 55-65 |

**Practical note:** Flare fittings are tightened by feel more than by torque wrench in the field. The rule of thumb is: hand-tight plus 1/6 to 1/4 turn with a wrench for soft copper, slightly less for hard-drawn copper. Over-tightening cracks the flare and causes leaks. Under-tightening also leaks. Use a drop of refrigerant oil on the flare face to reduce friction and ensure even seating. Always use a backup wrench on the fitting body to prevent twisting the tubing.

### Electrical Lugs and Terminals

| Lug Size / Application | Torque (in-lbs) |
|----------------------|-----------------|
| 14-10 AWG screw terminal (receptacles, switches) | 12-14 |
| 8-6 AWG mechanical lug | 35-45 |
| 4-2 AWG mechanical lug | 45-60 |
| 1-1/0 AWG mechanical lug | 50-70 |
| 2/0-4/0 AWG mechanical lug | 60-100 |
| 250-500 kcmil mechanical lug | 100-160 |
| Panel main lug (100-200A) | 200-300 |

**Important:** Always check the lug or terminal manufacturer's torque specification -- it is marked on the lug or in the panel documentation. Under-torqued connections cause overheating (high resistance), which causes melted wire insulation, burned lugs, and fires. Over-torqued connections damage the conductor strands and can crack the lug. Use a calibrated torque wrench (inch-pound type) for all feeder and service connections. This is now explicitly required by the NEC (110.14(D) in the 2017 and later editions).

### Refrigerant Service Valve Caps

| Valve Size | Torque (ft-lbs) |
|-----------|-----------------|
| 1/4" service port (Schrader) | 4-7 |
| 3/8" service valve cap | 10-14 |

**Note:** Service valve caps are the primary seal against refrigerant leaks at the service ports. Always replace the cap gasket when servicing. Tighten the cap firmly but do not use excessive force. ## 10. Temperature Split Reference

### Air Conditioning (Cooling Mode)

| Measurement | Target Range | Notes |
|-------------|-------------|-------|
| Supply-to-return temp split | 15-22°F | Measure at the equipment, not at the register |
| Target split at design conditions | 18-20°F | Most common range in properly operating systems |
| Low split (below 14°F) | Problem indication | Low charge, dirty evaporator, high airflow, TXV overfeeding |
| High split (above 24°F) | Problem indication | Low airflow (dirty filter, duct restriction), overcharged, TXV underfeeding |

**Measuring technique:** Insert a thermometer probe into the return air duct within 2 feet of the air handler. Insert another probe into the supply duct within 2 feet of the air handler on the supply side. Ensure the probes are in the center of the airstream, not touching duct metal. Let the system run for at least 15 minutes before taking readings. Measure wet-bulb and dry-bulb if possible -- wet-bulb readings give a better picture of total cooling capacity.

### Heat Pump (Heating Mode)

| Measurement | Target Range | Notes |
|-------------|-------------|-------|
| Supply-to-return temp split | 15-25°F | Lower than furnace due to lower supply temp |
| Normal split with outdoor temp above 40°F | 20-25°F | Best performance range |
| Normal split with outdoor temp 20-40°F | 15-20°F | Reduced capacity as outdoor temp drops |
| Normal split with outdoor temp below 20°F | 10-15°F (+ aux heat) | Aux heat supplements the heat pump |
| During defrost cycle | 0 or negative | Normal -- the system reverses briefly to defrost the outdoor coil |

**Heat pump discharge air:** Heat pumps produce supply air at 90-105°F in heating mode, compared to 120-140°F from a gas furnace. This is why customers sometimes complain that the air "feels cold" from a heat pump -- it is warm, just not as hot as they are used to from a furnace. Measure and document the split to prove the system is operating correctly.

### Gas Furnace (Heating Mode)

| Measurement | Target Range | Notes |
|-------------|-------------|-------|
| Temperature rise (supply minus return) | Per nameplate | Every furnace has a specific range printed on the rating plate |
| Typical single-stage | 35-65°F | Most common range |
| Typical two-stage (low fire) | 25-45°F | Lower rise on low fire is normal |
| Typical two-stage (high fire) | 40-70°F | Higher rise on high fire |
| Low temperature rise | Below nameplate range | Too much airflow (blower speed too high), underfire (low gas pressure) |
| High temperature rise | Above nameplate range | Not enough airflow (dirty filter, low blower speed, duct restriction), overfire |

**Critical warning:** A consistently high temperature rise is dangerous. It overheats the heat exchanger, trips the high-limit switch (short cycling), and over time will crack the heat exchanger. If you measure a rise above the nameplate range, do not leave the job until you have corrected it. Check the filter, check the blower speed setting, check the ductwork for restrictions, and verify gas pressure.

### Electric Furnace / Electric Heat

| Measurement | Target Range | Notes |
|-------------|-------------|-------|
| Temperature rise per stage | 15-25°F per element bank | Depends on number of stages and airflow |
| Total rise (all stages) | 40-80°F | Varies by kW rating and airflow |

**Sequencer operation:** Electric furnaces use sequencers to stage the heating elements on in sequence (one bank at a time) to prevent the full kW load from hitting the electrical panel simultaneously. If one sequencer fails, the temperature rise will be lower than expected because one element bank is not operating. Check each element bank individually with an amp clamp during operation.
