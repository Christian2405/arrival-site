# General Building Code Reference — Field Quick Reference

## Permit Requirements Overview

### Work That Typically Requires a Permit
- New construction (residential and commercial)
- Additions and structural alterations
- Electrical work (new circuits, panel upgrades, service changes)
- Plumbing work (new lines, water heater replacement, fixture relocation)
- HVAC installation or replacement
- Roofing replacement (over a certain area, varies by jurisdiction)
- Window and door replacements (structural openings)
- Deck and patio construction
- Fencing over a certain height (typically 6 feet)
- Water heater replacement
- Demolition

### Work That Typically Does NOT Require a Permit
- Painting, wallpapering, tiling, carpeting
- Like-for-like appliance replacement (non-gas)
- Cabinet and countertop replacement (no plumbing changes)
- Like-for-like light fixture and switch replacement
- Minor repair and maintenance
- Fences under 6 feet (varies)
- Retaining walls under 4 feet

**Important:** These are general guidelines. Every jurisdiction has different rules. When in doubt, call the local building department.

## Inspection Process

### Typical Residential Inspection Sequence
1. **Foundation/footing inspection** — before concrete pour
2. **Rough framing** — before insulation and drywall
3. **Rough plumbing** — before concealment
4. **Rough electrical** — before concealment
5. **Rough mechanical (HVAC)** — before concealment
6. **Insulation inspection** — before drywall
7. **Final plumbing** — fixtures installed, tested
8. **Final electrical** — devices installed, tested
9. **Final mechanical** — equipment operating, tested
10. **Final building** — everything complete, certificate of occupancy

### Inspection Tips for Contractors
- Call for inspection at least 24-48 hours in advance
- Have permit posted and visible on job site
- Have approved plans available for inspector review
- Ensure work area is accessible and safe for inspector
- Be present or have a knowledgeable representative present
- Address any corrections before calling for re-inspection

## Fire Safety

### Smoke Detectors
- Required in each bedroom
- Required outside each sleeping area (hallway)
- Required on every level of the home (including basement)
- Must be interconnected (when one sounds, all sound)
- Hardwired with battery backup in new construction
- Battery-only acceptable in existing homes when no remodel
- Replace every 10 years (per NFPA 72)

### Carbon Monoxide Detectors
- Required in every dwelling with fuel-burning appliances
- Required on every level with a sleeping area
- Required outside each sleeping area
- Combo smoke/CO units acceptable

### Fire Separation
- Garage-to-house separation: minimum 1/2 inch drywall on garage side (5/8 inch Type X on ceiling if living space above)
- Self-closing door from garage to house (1-3/8 inch solid core minimum, or 20-minute fire-rated)
- No openings from garage to sleeping rooms
- Furnace/water heater in garage: elevated 18 inches off floor if gas-fired (ignition source protection), or listed for garage installation

### Firestopping
- All penetrations through fire-rated assemblies must be firestopped
- This includes: plumbing pipes, electrical cables, HVAC ducts through walls and floors
- Use approved firestop materials (caulk, putty, wrap strips, pillows)
- Common violation: unsealed holes around pipes and wires through top plates and floor systems

## Egress Requirements

### Bedroom Egress Windows
- Every bedroom must have an emergency escape window or door
- Minimum net clear opening: 5.7 square feet (5.0 sq ft at grade floor)
- Minimum height: 24 inches
- Minimum width: 20 inches
- Maximum sill height: 44 inches from floor
- Must be operable from inside without tools or special knowledge
- Window wells: must have ladder or steps if well is over 44 inches deep

### Stairways
- Minimum width: 36 inches clear
- Maximum riser height: 7-3/4 inches
- Minimum tread depth: 10 inches
- Riser height variation: no more than 3/8 inch between tallest and shortest riser
- Headroom: minimum 6 feet 8 inches
- Handrail: required on at least one side, 34-38 inches above stair nosing
- Guard rail: required where drop exceeds 30 inches, minimum 36 inches high (42 inches for commercial)
- Baluster spacing: maximum 4 inches (a 4-inch sphere cannot pass through)

## Accessibility (ADA/State Requirements)

### Residential
- Fair Housing Act applies to multifamily with 4+ units (elevator building) or ground-floor units
- Accessible route, accessible doorways (32 inch clear minimum), reinforced bathroom walls for grab bars

### Commercial
- ADA compliance required for all public accommodations and commercial facilities
- Accessible parking, routes, restrooms, signage
- Door hardware: lever handles (no round knobs)
- Threshold height: maximum 1/2 inch
- Ramp slope: maximum 1:12 (for every inch of rise, 12 inches of run)

## Energy Code Highlights (IECC)

### Insulation Requirements (Climate Zone Dependent)
| Component | Zone 1-2 | Zone 3 | Zone 4-5 | Zone 6-8 |
|-----------|----------|--------|----------|----------|
| Attic | R-30 | R-38 | R-49 | R-60 |
| Wall cavity | R-13 | R-20 or 13+5 | R-20 or 13+5 | R-20+5 or 13+10 |
| Floor | R-13 | R-19 | R-30 | R-38 |
| Slab edge | 0 | R-5 | R-10 | R-15 |
| Basement wall | 0 | R-5 | R-10 | R-15 |

### Air Sealing
- Blower door testing: many jurisdictions require 5 ACH50 or less (3 ACH50 in energy-efficient programs)
- Seal all penetrations through building envelope
- Weatherstrip all exterior doors
- Caulk/foam around all window and door frames

### Window Requirements
- U-factor and SHGC (Solar Heat Gain Coefficient) requirements vary by climate zone
- Northern zones: lower U-factor (better insulation)
- Southern zones: lower SHGC (less solar heat gain)
- ENERGY STAR certification often meets or exceeds code minimum

## Common Code Violations Across Trades

1. **No permit pulled** — most common violation. Unpermitted work can cause problems at sale.
2. **Work not to approved plans** — changes during construction must be approved
3. **Missing or incorrect firestopping** — holes around pipes and wires through fire-rated assemblies
4. **Improper garage-to-house separation** — missing drywall, non-rated door
5. **Egress window too small or too high** — especially in finished basements
6. **Deck ledger not properly flashed and attached** — leading cause of deck failures
7. **Missing handrails or guard rails** — especially on stairs and elevated decks
8. **Improper grading/drainage** — water flowing toward foundation instead of away
9. **Overloaded electrical panel** — too many circuits, no space for required additions
10. **Missing anti-tip bracket on range/stove** — required since 1991, often forgotten

## Working with Inspectors

- Be respectful and professional — inspectors have the authority to approve or fail your work
- If you disagree with a correction, ask for the specific code section reference
- You have the right to appeal an inspector's decision (contact the building official)
- Build a good relationship — consistent quality work earns trust
- Don't cover work before it's inspected — biggest source of conflict
- Digital photos of concealed work are helpful for future reference

## Code Adoption by State

Building codes are adopted at the state level, then local jurisdictions can amend:
- **IBC/IRC:** Most states follow the International Building Code (commercial) and International Residential Code
- **NEC:** Electrical code adopted by all 50 states (different editions — 2017, 2020, or 2023)
- **UPC vs IPC:** Western states use Uniform Plumbing Code, eastern/central use International Plumbing Code
- **IMC vs UMC:** Same split — International Mechanical Code vs Uniform Mechanical Code

**Check your state's adopted code edition.** A requirement in NEC 2023 may not apply if your state is still on NEC 2017.
