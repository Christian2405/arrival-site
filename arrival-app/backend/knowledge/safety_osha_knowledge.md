# Safety & OSHA Knowledge for Trade Professionals

This document covers safety regulations, OSHA standards, and practical field guidance for HVAC, plumbing, electrical, and construction trades. All OSHA standard references are current as of 2025. State and local requirements may be stricter -- always follow the most protective standard.

---

## Fall Protection (OSHA 1926 Subpart M)

### When Fall Protection Is Required

**Construction (1926.501):** Fall protection is required at 6 feet above a lower level. This applies to all walking/working surfaces including roofs, scaffolds, leading edges, holes, formwork, rebar, steep roofs, wall openings, and residential construction.

**General industry (1910.28):** Fall protection is required at 4 feet above a lower level. This covers maintenance, repair, and service work in facilities such as mechanical rooms, equipment platforms, and mezzanines.

**Ladder work:** Fall protection is required on fixed ladders over 24 feet (new installations must have ladder safety systems per 1910.28(b)(9)). Portable ladders do not require fall arrest, but safe practices still apply.

**Residential construction exception (1926.501(b)(13)):** Workers on residential roofs 6 feet or less from the eave to the ground may use alternative methods if a written plan exists. This is narrow -- most residential roof work still requires conventional fall protection.

**Field reality:** If you are on a roof, near an open edge, or working above another level and there is no guardrail between you and a drop of 6 feet or more, you need fall protection. Period. Do not assume the general contractor set it up.

### Guardrail Systems (1926.502(b))

- **Top rail height:** 42 inches (+/- 3 inches) above the walking/working surface.
- **Mid-rail height:** 21 inches, or halfway between the top rail and the walking surface.
- **Top rail load capacity:** Must withstand 200 pounds of outward or downward force applied at any point along the top edge.
- **Mid-rail load capacity:** 150 pounds in any outward or downward direction.
- **Toeboard:** Required when there is a risk of objects falling to a lower level. Must be at least 3.5 inches tall, with no more than 1/4 inch clearance above the walking surface.
- **Openings in guardrails:** No opening can allow passage of a 19-inch sphere (except at gates/access points).
- **Material:** Wood, pipe, structural steel, wire rope (1/4 inch minimum diameter with max 3 ft between posts). Wire rope must be flagged with high-visibility material every 6 feet.

**Field tip:** If a guardrail flexes significantly when you lean on it, it does not meet the standard. Manila rope or caution tape is never an acceptable guardrail. Caution tape is a warning line, not a barrier.

### Personal Fall Arrest Systems (1926.502(d))

A personal fall arrest system (PFAS) consists of three components: an anchorage, a body harness, and a connecting device (lanyard or self-retracting lifeline).

**Harnesses:**
- Full-body harness is the only acceptable body wear in a fall arrest system. Body belts are prohibited for fall arrest (they are only allowed for positioning).
- D-ring placement for fall arrest: center of the back, between the shoulder blades.
- Inspect before each use: check stitching, webbing for cuts/burns/chemical damage, D-rings for cracks, buckles for function.
- Remove from service after a fall. A harness that has arrested a fall must be destroyed.

**Lanyards:**
- Maximum length: 6 feet for shock-absorbing lanyards.
- Deceleration distance: no more than 3.5 feet.
- Total fall distance: the worker must not contact any lower level. Calculate: lanyard length + deceleration distance + D-ring to feet distance + safety margin. A 6-foot lanyard with a 3.5 ft deceleration device needs at least 18.5 feet of clearance.
- Self-retracting lifelines (SRLs) reduce free-fall distance to about 2 feet. Use SRLs when clearance is limited.

**Anchor points:**
- Must support 5,000 pounds per attached worker, or be designed by a qualified person as part of a system with a safety factor of 2.
- Never anchor to conduit, ductwork, plumbing lines, or mechanical piping.
- Roof anchors must be rated and installed per manufacturer instructions. Do not improvise.
- When in doubt, use a certified portable anchor or structural beam clamp rated for fall arrest.

**Field tip:** Before you clip in, ask: "If I fall, is there enough clearance below me for the system to deploy fully?" If the answer is no, switch to a shorter lanyard or an SRL.

### Hole Covers (1926.502(i))

- Must support at least twice the maximum anticipated load (workers, equipment, materials).
- Must be secured against accidental displacement (screwed, nailed, or bolted -- not just laid over the hole).
- Must be marked with the word "HOLE" or "COVER" -- visible and clear.
- Applies to all floor holes, roof openings, and skylight openings.

**Field tip:** Plywood over a floor hole without fasteners or markings is not compliant and is one of OSHA's most-cited hazards. If you remove a cover to run pipe or wire, you are responsible for replacing it before you leave or for guarding the opening.

### Ladder Safety (1926.1053)

**Setup:**
- 4-to-1 ratio: For every 4 feet of ladder height, the base should be 1 foot from the wall. Quick check: stand at the base, extend your arms straight out -- your palms should touch the rung at shoulder height.
- Extension ladders must extend at least 3 feet above the landing or roof edge.
- Secure the top of the ladder (tie off, have someone hold it, or use a ladder stabilizer).
- Set on a firm, level surface. Use leg levelers on uneven ground, never shim with scrap.

**Use:**
- Three-point contact at all times (two hands and one foot, or two feet and one hand).
- Face the ladder when climbing. Do not carry tools in your hands -- use a tool belt or haul line.
- Do not stand on the top two rungs of a stepladder or the top three rungs of an extension ladder.
- One person on a ladder at a time unless it is specifically rated for two.
- Maximum load rating: check the duty rating label. Type IAA = 375 lbs, Type IA = 300 lbs, Type I = 250 lbs, Type II = 225 lbs, Type III = 200 lbs. Include your body weight plus tools and materials.

**Inspection:**
- Inspect before each use. Look for bent rails, cracked rungs, missing feet, damaged locks.
- Metal ladders: never use near electrical work. Fiberglass is the standard for electrical trades.
- Remove damaged ladders from service immediately. Tag "DO NOT USE" and report.

### Scaffold Requirements (1926.450-454)

- **Access:** Scaffolds over 2 feet above the lower level must have a safe access point (ladder, stairway, ramp, or direct walk-on).
- **Fall protection on scaffolds:** Required at 10 feet above a lower level in construction.
- **Guardrails on scaffolds:** Top rail at 38-45 inches (note: slightly different than standard guardrails), mid-rail, and toeboards.
- **Planking:** Scaffold platforms must be at least 18 inches wide. Planks must extend at least 6 inches past supports but no more than 12 inches (unless cleated). No gaps wider than 1 inch between planks.
- **Capacity:** Scaffolds must be designed by a qualified person and built to support 4x the maximum intended load.
- **Competent person:** A competent person must inspect scaffolds before each shift and after any event that could affect structural integrity (weather, impact, modification).
- **Tie-offs:** Scaffolds must be tied to the building at intervals specified by the manufacturer and at least every 26 feet vertically and 30 feet horizontally.
- **Base plates and mudsills:** Required on all supported scaffolds to distribute the load and prevent settlement.

**Field tip for HVAC/plumbing:** Baker scaffolds (rolling scaffolds) are common inside buildings. Lock all casters before climbing. Never move a rolling scaffold with anyone on it. Keep the height-to-base ratio within 4:1 for stability.

## Electrical Safety

### Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) -- OSHA 1910.147

LOTO prevents unexpected energization of equipment during service or maintenance. This standard kills more trade workers than almost any other single hazard when ignored.

**When LOTO is required:** Any time a worker could be exposed to unexpected startup, release of stored energy, or contact with energized parts during servicing or maintenance.

**The six steps of LOTO:**
1. **Notify** all affected employees that equipment will be locked out.
2. **Shut down** the equipment using normal operating controls.
3. **Isolate** all energy sources. This means opening disconnects, closing valves, bleeding pneumatic/hydraulic lines, and blocking mechanical motion.
4. **Apply** your personal lock and tag to each energy-isolating device. Each worker applies their own lock. No one else holds your key.
5. **Release stored energy.** Bleed capacitors, drain lines, release springs, lower suspended loads, vent pressure.
6. **Verify** the equipment is de-energized. Try to start it. Test with a meter. Confirm zero energy. Use the live-dead-live testing method: test your meter on a known live source, test the de-energized circuit, then test on the known live source again to confirm the meter is working.

**Key rules:**
- Only the person who applied the lock can remove it. Cutting someone else's lock requires a documented procedure with management authorization and verification that the worker is not in danger.
- Tags alone are not sufficient if a lock can be applied. Tags are supplementary.
- Group lockout/tagout requires a single authorized employee to coordinate.
- Shift transfer: the outgoing worker applies a lock, the incoming worker applies theirs, then the outgoing worker removes theirs. Locks must overlap.

**Field tip for HVAC techs:** Locking out a rooftop unit means more than flipping the disconnect. Verify that the unit has no alternative feeds, the gas valve is closed, and there is no pressurized refrigerant that could release unexpectedly. For plumbers working on boilers, LOTO the gas, electric, and isolate the water supply.

### Arc Flash Protection (NFPA 70E / OSHA 1910.335)

OSHA requires employers to protect workers from arc flash hazards. NFPA 70E provides the detailed framework that OSHA enforces.

**PPE Categories (NFPA 70E Table 130.7(C)(15)(a)):**

- **Category 1 (4 cal/cm2):** Arc-rated long sleeve shirt, pants, safety glasses, hearing protection. Typical for work on 208V-240V panels with low available fault current.
- **Category 2 (8 cal/cm2):** Arc-rated shirt, pants, arc-rated face shield, balaclava, hearing protection. Standard for most 480V work.
- **Category 3 (25 cal/cm2):** Arc flash suit hood, arc-rated coveralls, gloves, hard hat. Higher-energy 480V systems and low-voltage switchgear.
- **Category 4 (40 cal/cm2):** Full arc flash suit, multi-layer, full head/face/body coverage. Maximum protection level. If incident energy exceeds 40 cal/cm2, the work must not be performed while energized.

**Arc flash boundary:** The distance at which incident energy drops to 1.2 cal/cm2 (second-degree burn threshold). Labels on equipment panels should list this distance. Do not cross the arc flash boundary without appropriate PPE.

**Field tip:** If a panel does not have an arc flash label, treat it as dangerous. Do not open it while energized unless absolutely necessary and you have the proper PPE and training. Most trade work should be performed on de-energized equipment.

### Working Distances for Different Voltages (OSHA 1926.960 / NFPA 70E Table 130.4(E)(a))

**Approach boundaries for shock protection (qualified persons):**

- **50V - 150V:** Limited approach 3 ft 6 in. Restricted approach: avoid contact.
- **151V - 750V:** Limited approach 3 ft 6 in. Restricted approach 1 ft 0 in.
- **751V - 15kV:** Limited approach 5 ft 0 in. Restricted approach 2 ft 2 in.
- **15.1kV - 36kV:** Limited approach 6 ft 0 in. Restricted approach 2 ft 7 in.

**Unqualified persons** must stay outside the limited approach boundary at all times. For 50-750V, that means at least 3 feet 6 inches, but many safety programs use 10 feet as the unqualified boundary.

**Field tip:** For most trade work (120V-480V panels, motor starters, disconnects), the limited approach boundary is 3 feet 6 inches. If you do not have electrical training and authorization, stay at least 4 feet from open energized panels.

### Ground Fault Protection (OSHA 1926.405(a)(2))

**GFCI is required in the following situations:**
- All 120V, single-phase, 15A and 20A receptacle outlets on construction sites that are not part of the permanent wiring.
- Outdoor receptacles in wet or damp locations.
- Bathrooms, kitchens near sinks, garages, unfinished basements, and crawl spaces (NEC 210.8 for permanent installations).
- Rooftops (NEC 2020 added this requirement).
- Temporary power on job sites.

**Assured equipment grounding conductor program (AEGCP):** An alternative to GFCI on construction sites, but requires written documentation, testing, and competent person oversight. Most contractors just use GFCIs because it is simpler.

**How a GFCI works:** It monitors the current flowing on the hot and neutral conductors. If there is a difference of 5 milliamps or more (indicating current is flowing through an unintended path, like a person), the GFCI trips in 1/40 of a second. This is fast enough to prevent electrocution in most cases.

**Field tip:** Carry a portable GFCI adapter in your tool bag. If the outlet you are plugging into does not have GFCI protection, plug your adapter in first. Test GFCIs monthly -- press the TEST button; the power should cut. Press RESET to restore. If it does not trip, replace it.

### Energized Work Permits (NFPA 70E 130.2)

Energized electrical work is permitted only when de-energizing creates a greater hazard or is infeasible.

**An energized work permit must include:**
- Description of the circuit and equipment.
- Justification for why the work must be performed energized.
- A shock risk assessment and arc flash risk assessment.
- Required PPE based on the assessment.
- Safe work practices to be employed.
- Means of restricting access.
- Approval signature from a qualified person and management.

**Who authorizes:** The employer's safety program should define the authorization chain. Typically, the electrical safety program manager or project manager with specific authority.

**When you do NOT need a permit (but still need precautions):**
- Testing and troubleshooting that requires energized circuits to diagnose.
- Thermographic inspections.
- Voltage measurements.
These activities still require appropriate PPE and training, but a formal permit may not be required if covered under the employer's electrical safety program.

**Field tip:** "It will take too long to shut it down" is not a valid justification. Troubleshooting and diagnostics on energized equipment is generally acceptable because de-energizing would eliminate the condition being diagnosed. But replacing a breaker in a live panel because you do not want to coordinate a shutdown is not acceptable.

## Confined Space Entry (OSHA 1910.146)

### What Is a Confined Space?

A space is a confined space if it meets all three criteria:
1. **Large enough** for a worker to enter and perform work.
2. **Limited or restricted means of entry or exit** (hatches, small openings, tunnels).
3. **Not designed for continuous occupancy.**

### Permit-Required Confined Space (PRCS)

A confined space becomes permit-required if it has one or more of these hazards:
- Contains or has the potential to contain a hazardous atmosphere.
- Contains material that could engulf the entrant (grain, sand, water).
- Has an internal configuration that could trap or asphyxiate (converging walls, sloped floors leading to small sections).
- Contains any other recognized serious safety or health hazard.

### Common Confined Spaces in the Trades

- **Manholes:** Storm, sewer, telecom, and electrical manholes. Almost always permit-required.
- **Tanks and vessels:** Water heater tanks, boiler drums, storage tanks, grease traps.
- **Crawl spaces:** Under buildings, in attics with limited access. May be permit-required if gas lines run through them or if ventilation is poor.
- **Attic spaces:** Tight attics with gas appliances or refrigerant lines can accumulate hazardous atmospheres.
- **Utility vaults:** Underground vaults for water, gas, or electrical utilities.
- **Ductwork:** Large enough to enter for cleaning or repair.
- **Pits:** Elevator pits, valve pits, sump pits.
- **Septic tanks and grease interceptors:** Common for plumbers. Always permit-required due to decomposition gases.

### Atmospheric Testing Requirements (1910.146(c)(5))

Test the atmosphere before entry and continuously during occupancy. Test in this order:

1. **Oxygen (O2):** Safe range is 19.5% to 23.5%. Below 19.5% is oxygen-deficient (dangerous). Above 23.5% is oxygen-enriched (fire/explosion risk). Normal atmospheric oxygen is 20.9%.
2. **Combustible gases (LEL):** Lower explosive limit must be below 10% LEL. At 10% LEL or above, do not enter. Above 25% LEL is immediately dangerous.
3. **Hydrogen sulfide (H2S):** PEL is 20 ppm (ceiling). IDLH is 100 ppm. Common in sewers, manholes, and anywhere organic material decomposes. Smells like rotten eggs at low concentrations but deadens the sense of smell at higher levels -- do not rely on your nose.
4. **Carbon monoxide (CO):** PEL is 50 ppm (8-hour TWA). IDLH is 1,200 ppm. Common around combustion equipment, vehicle exhaust, and generators.

**Testing procedure:**
- Calibrate your 4-gas meter according to manufacturer schedule (bump test before each shift at minimum).
- Test at multiple levels: top, middle, bottom of the space. Different gases stratify at different levels (H2S is heavier than air and sinks, methane is lighter than air and rises, CO distributes relatively evenly).
- Continuously monitor during entry. An atmosphere can change quickly -- a worker's torch, a chemical reaction, or a change in ventilation can alter conditions in seconds.
- Do not trust readings from outside the space. Lower the sensor into the space on a probe line before anyone enters.

### Entry Team Roles

**Attendant:**
- Remains outside the permit space at all times. No exceptions.
- Monitors the authorized entrants and the conditions inside and outside the space.
- Maintains communication with entrants at all times (visual, voice, radio, or signal line).
- Orders evacuation if they detect a hazardous condition, if an entrant shows symptoms of exposure, or if an emergency outside the space could affect entrants.
- Does NOT enter the space for rescue. The attendant calls for rescue and uses non-entry retrieval if available.
- Can monitor more than one space simultaneously only if duties can be effectively performed for each space.

**Entrant:**
- Knows the hazards, signs, and symptoms of exposure.
- Uses equipment properly (harness, retrieval line, personal monitors).
- Communicates with the attendant continuously.
- Exits immediately upon order from the attendant, upon recognizing symptoms, or upon detecting a hazardous condition.

**Entry Supervisor:**
- Authorizes entry by signing the permit.
- Verifies all tests have been completed, equipment is ready, and procedures are in place.
- Terminates entry and cancels the permit when the work is complete or conditions change.
- Can also serve as the attendant or entrant if trained for those roles.

### Rescue Planning (1910.146(d)(9))

- A rescue plan must be in place before anyone enters a permit-required confined space.
- Options: on-site rescue team, off-site rescue service (fire department), or self-rescue.
- Non-entry retrieval (tripod and winch with retrieval line attached to the entrant's harness) is preferred whenever feasible. The retrieval line must be attached to the center of the entrant's back near shoulder level, or above the head if using a wristlet.
- If using an off-site rescue service, confirm their response time and capabilities. They must be able to reach the space and perform a rescue within a time frame appropriate to the identified hazards.

**Field tip:** Never attempt a "quick rescue" by jumping in after a downed worker. More than 60% of confined space fatalities are would-be rescuers. The atmosphere that knocked down the first worker will knock you down too. Follow the plan. Call for help. Use the retrieval system.

## Trenching & Excavation (OSHA 1926 Subpart P)

### When Protective Systems Are Required (1926.652)

- **Under 5 feet:** Protective systems are not required unless a competent person identifies a hazard (such as unstable soil or water accumulation). Best practice: use protection anyway.
- **5 feet to 20 feet:** A protective system (sloping, benching, shoring, or shielding) is required.
- **Over 20 feet:** A protective system designed by a registered professional engineer is required.

### Soil Classification (1926 Subpart P, Appendix A)

Soil classification determines what protective measures are needed. The competent person classifies soil using at least one visual test and one manual test.

**Type A (most stable):**
- Cohesive soils with unconfined compressive strength of 1.5 tsf or greater.
- Examples: clay, silty clay, hardpan.
- Maximum allowable slope: 53 degrees from horizontal (3/4H:1V).
- Cannot be classified as Type A if: the soil has been previously disturbed, is subject to vibration from heavy traffic or pile driving, has been exposed to water seepage, or is part of a layered system where weaker soil overlays Type A.

**Type B:**
- Cohesive soils with 0.5 to 1.5 tsf, or granular soils including angular gravel, silt, and silt loam.
- Also includes previously disturbed Type A soil and soil that meets Type A criteria but is subject to vibration.
- Maximum allowable slope: 45 degrees (1H:1V).

**Type C (least stable):**
- Cohesive soils with 0.5 tsf or less, granular soils including gravel, sand, and loamy sand, submerged soil, and soil from which water is seeping.
- Maximum allowable slope: 34 degrees (1.5H:1V).
- When in doubt, classify as Type C. It is the safest assumption.

**Field tip:** If water is accumulating in the trench, if the soil has been previously disturbed, or if there are vibrations from nearby traffic or equipment, the soil classification drops. Previously dug soil is never Type A. In practice, most urban excavations end up as Type B or C.

### Protective Methods

**Sloping:** Cutting the walls of the trench at an angle so they slope away from the excavation. Simple and effective but requires a lot of room. In Type C soil, a 10-foot trench needs 15 feet of slope on each side -- that is 40 feet of total width for a 10-foot-deep trench.

**Benching:** Cutting steps (benches) into the trench walls. Not permitted in Type C soil. Each bench must be no more than 5 feet high for Type A soil, 4 feet for Type B.

**Shoring:** Installing aluminum hydraulic shoring, timber shoring, or mechanical systems to brace the trench walls and prevent cave-in. Good for tight spaces where sloping is not practical. Hydraulic aluminum shoring is the most common in residential and commercial work.

**Shielding (trench boxes):** Prefabricated steel or aluminum shields placed in the trench to protect workers. The box does not prevent cave-in -- it protects workers if one occurs. Workers must stay inside the shield at all times. The shield must extend at least 18 inches above the surrounding soil if there is no sloping to the top of the shield.

### Competent Person Requirements (1926.650(b))

Every excavation must have a competent person who:
- Is capable of identifying existing and predictable hazards and has the authority to take prompt corrective measures.
- Classifies soil using visual and manual tests.
- Selects the appropriate protective system.
- Inspects the excavation, adjacent areas, and protective systems daily before work starts and after any event that could change conditions (rain, vibration, material surcharge, freeze/thaw).
- Has the authority to remove workers from the excavation immediately if conditions are unsafe.
- Monitors water removal equipment and atmospheric conditions.

### Utility Location Before Digging (1926.651(b))

**Before any digging:**
1. **Call 811** at least 48-72 hours before digging (timing varies by state). This is the national "Call Before You Dig" number. Utility companies will mark public utilities at no charge.
2. **Private locates:** 811 does not locate private utilities (lines between the meter and the building, private septic lines, irrigation lines, private communication cables). You need a private locator or as-built drawings.
3. **Hand-dig within the tolerance zone** around marked utilities (typically 18-24 inches on each side, varies by state).
4. **Confirm markings are still valid.** Markings expire (usually 10-30 days depending on state). If you have not dug within that window, call again.
5. **Potholing (vacuum excavation):** Use air or water vacuum excavation to expose underground utilities without damage. This is the safest method for confirming utility locations.

**Marking colors (APWA Uniform Color Code):**
- Red: Electric power lines and cables
- Yellow: Gas, oil, steam, petroleum
- Orange: Communications, cable TV, fiber optic
- Blue: Potable water
- Green: Sewer, storm drain
- Purple: Reclaimed water, irrigation
- Pink: Temporary survey markings
- White: Proposed excavation area (you mark this)

### Other Excavation Safety Requirements

- **Spoil pile:** Keep excavated material at least 2 feet from the edge of the excavation.
- **Access/egress:** Provide a ladder, ramp, or stairway within 25 feet of lateral travel for any trench 4 feet or deeper.
- **Water accumulation:** Do not work in excavations with accumulated water unless adequate precautions are in place (water removal, harnesses, etc.). A competent person must monitor water removal equipment.
- **Adjacent structures:** If the excavation could affect the stability of adjacent buildings, walls, or sidewalks, support systems must be designed by a registered professional engineer.
- **Falling load protection:** Do not allow workers under loads being lifted by excavation equipment. Keep workers clear of vehicles being loaded or unloaded.
- **Atmospheric hazards:** Excavations where oxygen deficiency or hazardous atmosphere exists or could reasonably be expected (near gas lines, landfills, sewers) require atmospheric testing and ventilation.

**Field tip:** A cubic yard of soil weighs approximately 2,700 lbs. A trench collapse buries a worker in seconds. Most victims cannot be rescued in time because the weight of soil on the chest prevents breathing. Never enter an unprotected trench, no matter how quick the task.

## PPE Requirements (OSHA 1910 Subpart I / 1926 Subpart E)

### Hard Hat Classifications (ANSI/ISEA Z89.1)

**Types:**
- **Type I:** Protects the top of the head from impact. Most common style.
- **Type II:** Protects the top and sides of the head from impact. Required when there is a lateral impact hazard (working under low structures, near swinging loads).

**Classes (electrical rating):**
- **Class E (Electrical):** Tested to 20,000 volts phase-to-ground. Standard for electrical workers and anyone working near overhead power or electrical equipment.
- **Class G (General):** Tested to 2,200 volts phase-to-ground. Suitable for general construction where electrical exposure is limited.
- **Class C (Conductive):** No electrical protection. Ventilated design for comfort in non-electrical environments. Never wear a Class C hard hat near electrical hazards.

**Inspection and replacement:**
- Replace after any significant impact, even if damage is not visible. The energy-absorbing capability is compromised.
- Replace if cracked, dented, or if the suspension is damaged, loose, or missing components.
- Replace per manufacturer recommendations (typically 2 years for the suspension, 5 years for the shell, depending on material and UV exposure).
- Do not drill holes, paint with solvents, or store in direct sunlight for extended periods. These degrade the shell material.
- Do not wear a hard hat backwards unless it is designed and tested for reverse wear (check the manufacturer markings).

**Field tip:** If you are an HVAC tech or electrician, wear a Class E hard hat whenever there is any overhead hazard or electrical exposure. A bump cap is not a hard hat and does not meet OSHA requirements for construction or general industry.

### Eye Protection (ANSI/ISEA Z87.1)

**Selection by hazard:**
- **Safety glasses (Z87+):** Impact protection for flying particles, dust, and small debris. Minimum for any construction or mechanical work. Must have side shields.
- **Safety goggles:** Splash protection for chemicals, dust-tight seal for grinding and cutting. Required when working with acids, caustics, refrigerant handling (liquid splash risk), and heavy grinding.
- **Face shield:** Worn over safety glasses or goggles. Required for grinding, chipping, and any operation that produces large particles or sparks. A face shield alone is not sufficient -- you need primary eye protection underneath.
- **Welding helmets/goggles:** Required for welding (shade 10-14 for arc welding depending on amperage, shade 3-8 for gas welding and cutting, shade 3-6 for brazing).

**Field tip:** Safety glasses must be stamped Z87+ on the lens and frame. Regular prescription glasses are not safety glasses unless they have Z87-rated lenses and side shields. Prescription safety glasses are available from optical suppliers and are a worthwhile investment.

### Hearing Protection (OSHA 1910.95 / 1926.52)

- **85 dB action level:** Employer must implement a hearing conservation program (monitoring, audiometric testing, hearing protection made available at no cost).
- **90 dB TWA (8-hour time-weighted average):** Engineering controls or hearing protection required. For every 5 dB increase, allowable exposure time is halved: 90 dB = 8 hrs, 95 dB = 4 hrs, 100 dB = 2 hrs, 105 dB = 1 hr, 110 dB = 30 min, 115 dB = 15 min.
- **Noise Reduction Rating (NRR):** To calculate effective protection, subtract 7 from the NRR, then divide by 2, then subtract from the noise level. Example: 100 dB noise with NRR 29 earplugs: (29-7)/2 = 11. Effective exposure: 100-11 = 89 dB.
- **Dual protection:** Earplugs under earmuffs adds approximately 5 dB additional reduction beyond the higher-rated protector alone. Required when noise levels exceed 105 dB.

**Common noise levels in trades:**
- Hammer drill in concrete: 110-120 dB
- Circular saw: 100-110 dB
- Impact wrench: 95-105 dB
- Chop saw / angle grinder: 95-110 dB
- Reciprocating saw: 90-100 dB
- Normal conversation: 60 dB
- HVAC compressor room: 85-95 dB
- Pneumatic nail gun: 100-110 dB

**Field tip:** If you have to raise your voice to be heard by someone 3 feet away, the noise level is above 85 dB and you need hearing protection. Hearing loss is permanent and cumulative. Foam earplugs are cheap. Use them.

### Respiratory Protection (OSHA 1910.134)

**Selection hierarchy:**
1. **Dust mask (filtering facepiece, N95/P100):** Particulates only. N95 filters 95% of particles, P100 filters 99.97%. Appropriate for nuisance dust, drywall dust, sawdust, and some mold remediation.
2. **Half-face respirator with cartridges:** Select cartridges for the specific hazard. OV (organic vapor) for solvents and PVC cement, P100 for particles, multi-gas for combination hazards. Required for silica, lead abatement, solvent exposure in confined areas.
3. **Full-face respirator:** Same as half-face plus eye protection. Required when the hazard also affects eyes or when higher protection factors are needed. Protection factor of 50 (vs 10 for half-face).
4. **Supplied air respirator (SAR) / SCBA:** Required in oxygen-deficient atmospheres (below 19.5% O2), IDLH environments, or when air-purifying respirators cannot adequately reduce exposure.

**Medical evaluation:** Workers must have a medical evaluation (questionnaire or exam) before being fit-tested for any tight-fitting respirator. This is an employer responsibility under 1910.134(e).

**Fit testing:** Required annually and whenever a different respirator model or size is used. Quantitative or qualitative methods per 1910.134(f).

**Field tip:** A dust mask with a beard does not work. Facial hair breaks the seal and reduces the protection factor to nearly zero. If you have a beard, you need a loose-fitting powered air-purifying respirator (PAPR) or supplied air hood for proper protection.

### Glove Selection by Hazard

- **Cut resistance (ANSI/ISEA 105):** Levels A1-A9. Sheet metal work typically requires A4-A6. Use cut-resistant gloves when handling ductwork, flashing, or sharp materials.
- **Chemical resistance:** Nitrile for general chemical splash and refrigerant oils. Butyl for ketones and esters. Neoprene for acids and caustics. PVA for chlorinated solvents. Always check the chemical compatibility chart for the specific substance.
- **Electrical (ASTM D120):** Rubber insulating gloves rated Class 00 (500V) through Class 4 (36,000V). Must be worn with leather protectors over them. Inspect before each use by rolling the cuff to trap air and checking for leaks. Test every 6 months per OSHA 1910.137.
- **Thermal:** Heat-resistant gloves for brazing, soldering, handling hot pipe. Rated by contact temperature. Cold-weather insulated gloves for winter work.
- **General purpose:** Leather or synthetic work gloves for material handling, framing, and general construction.

### High-Vis Requirements (ANSI/ISEA 107)

- **Class 1:** Minimum visibility. Off-road work with low traffic speeds. Not appropriate for roadway work.
- **Class 2:** Medium visibility. Typical construction site requirement. Required near roadways with speeds under 50 mph. Vest must have 201 square inches of background material and 201 square inches of retroreflective material.
- **Class 3:** Highest visibility. Required for work near roadways with speeds over 50 mph, flaggers, and high-risk environments. Requires both upper and lower body coverage (vest and pants, or full coverall).

**Field tip:** Many general contractors require Class 2 hi-vis vests as a site minimum. Keep one in your truck at all times. Hi-vis in good condition -- dirty, faded, or torn hi-vis does not meet the standard.

## Hazardous Materials

### Asbestos Awareness (OSHA 1926.1101 / 1910.1001)

**What it is:** A naturally occurring mineral fiber used extensively in building materials from the 1920s through the late 1980s. Microscopic fibers, when inhaled, cause mesothelioma, asbestosis, and lung cancer. There is no safe level of exposure. Still not fully banned in the US.

**Where you find it:**
- Pipe insulation (white chalky wrapping on older piping, especially in boiler rooms and basements).
- Boiler insulation and gaskets.
- Floor tiles (9x9 inch tiles are a classic indicator, though 12x12 can contain it too) and mastic adhesive (black cutback adhesive).
- Ceiling tiles and spray-on ceiling texture (popcorn ceilings applied before 1980).
- Roofing materials (shingles, felt, flashing cement, roof coatings).
- Duct insulation and duct tape (the original cloth type, not modern tape).
- Transite pipe (cement-asbestos pipe used for HVAC, plumbing vent stacks, and sewer).
- Electrical panel backing, arc chutes in older breakers, and wire insulation.
- Vermiculite attic insulation (especially from the Libby, Montana mine).
- Joint compound and plaster in pre-1980 buildings.
- Fireproofing spray on steel structures.

**What to do if you suspect asbestos:**
1. Stop work immediately. Do not disturb the material.
2. Do not sweep, vacuum (unless HEPA), sand, drill, cut, or break the material.
3. Notify the general contractor or building owner.
4. Have the material tested by a certified lab (polarized light microscopy) before proceeding.
5. Only licensed asbestos abatement contractors may remove or disturb asbestos-containing material.

**OSHA classifications for asbestos work:**
- **Class I:** Removal of thermal system insulation and sprayed-on/troweled-on surfacing. Most hazardous. Full containment and negative air required.
- **Class II:** Removal of other ACM that is not thermal system insulation (floor tile, roofing, siding, transite).
- **Class III:** Repair and maintenance operations that disturb small amounts of ACM.
- **Class IV:** Custodial activities that contact ACM or presumed ACM (cleaning debris from abatement work).

**Field tip:** If you are working in a building built before 1990 and encounter any insulation, flooring, or texture you are unsure about, assume it contains asbestos until proven otherwise. You cannot identify asbestos by sight alone -- it requires lab analysis. The $25 for a lab test could save your life.

### Lead Paint (OSHA 1926.62 / EPA RRP Rule 40 CFR 745)

- Lead-based paint was used in most homes and buildings built before 1978.
- Any renovation, repair, or painting work that disturbs lead-based paint in pre-1978 housing or child-occupied facilities requires EPA RRP (Renovation, Repair, and Painting) certification.

**RRP Rule requirements:**
- Firms must be EPA-certified (or state-certified in delegated states).
- At least one certified renovator must be assigned to each job.
- Lead-safe work practices must be followed: containment (plastic sheeting), HEPA vacuuming, wet methods for dust suppression, prohibition of certain practices.
- Post-renovation cleaning verification is required.

**Prohibited practices under RRP:**
- Open flame burning or torching of lead-based paint.
- Using heat guns above 1100 degrees F.
- Machine sanding or grinding without HEPA vacuum attachment.
- Uncontained hydroblasting or high-pressure washing.
- Using a volatile chemical stripper in poorly ventilated spaces.

**OSHA exposure limits:**
- PEL: 50 micrograms per cubic meter of air (8-hour TWA).
- Action level: 30 micrograms per cubic meter.
- Blood lead level action: medical surveillance required at 40 micrograms/dL. Removal from exposure at 50 micrograms/dL. Return at 40 micrograms/dL.

**Field tip:** Plumbers and HVAC techs frequently disturb lead paint when cutting into walls, running new lines, or replacing fixtures in older homes. If the home was built before 1978, ask if lead testing has been done. Use wet methods and HEPA vacuum when cutting or drilling. A simple swab test (3M LeadCheck or similar) gives on-site results in seconds for painted surfaces.

### Silica Dust (OSHA 1926.1153)

Crystalline silica is found in concrete, brick, block, stone, mortar, and sand. Cutting, grinding, drilling, or demolishing these materials generates respirable silica dust that causes silicosis, lung cancer, COPD, and kidney disease.

**OSHA Table 1 (1926.1153(c)(1)):** Specifies the required exposure control methods for 18 common construction tasks. If you follow Table 1, you do not need to measure silica exposure levels.

**Key Table 1 requirements:**
- **Handheld power saw (cutting concrete/masonry):** Use saw with integrated water delivery to suppress dust. Operate and maintain per manufacturer. Respirator not required if water is used effectively.
- **Handheld grinder (concrete/masonry, mortar removal):** Use grinder with commercially available dust shroud and HEPA-filtered vacuum. Respirator: APF 10 (N95 half-face or better).
- **Rotary hammer drill (concrete/masonry):** Use drill with commercially available dust collection system and HEPA filter, or use a shroud with vacuum. Respirator: APF 10 when dust collector is used.
- **Jackhammering/chipping:** Use tool with water delivery to suppress dust continuously. Respirator: APF 10 (N95 minimum).
- **Indoor concrete grinding (walk-behind):** Use grinder with integrated vacuum and HEPA filter. Respirator: APF 10. Additional ventilation may be needed indoors.
- **Mixing mortar or concrete:** Use enclosed mixer or wet method. Respirator: APF 10 when pouring dry mix.

**PEL for silica:** 50 micrograms per cubic meter (8-hour TWA). Action level: 25 micrograms per cubic meter.

**Field tip:** Dry-cutting concrete without dust controls is one of the most dangerous common practices in the trades. The silica exposure from one hour of uncontrolled dry cutting can exceed the daily limit by 10 to 50 times. Always use water or HEPA vacuum dust collection. An N95 alone without engineering controls is not enough if you are generating visible dust clouds.

### Refrigerant Handling (EPA Section 608)

- **EPA 608 certification** is required for any person who maintains, services, repairs, or disposes of equipment that uses regulated refrigerants (CFCs, HCFCs, HFCs, and HFOs covered under the AIM Act).
- **Certification types:** Type I (small appliances under 5 lbs charge), Type II (high-pressure equipment like residential AC using R-410A, R-22, R-134a), Type III (low-pressure equipment like centrifugal chillers using R-123, R-245fa), Universal (all types).
- **Venting prohibition:** It is illegal to knowingly vent or release regulated refrigerants into the atmosphere (Section 608, Clean Air Act). Violations can result in fines up to $44,539 per day per violation as of 2024.
- **Recovery requirements:** Refrigerant must be recovered to required evacuation levels before opening a system for repair. Recovery equipment must be certified by an EPA-approved testing organization.
- **Leak repair requirements:** Commercial refrigeration and comfort cooling systems with 50+ pounds of charge must be repaired if the leak rate exceeds the applicable threshold. For commercial AC/heat pumps: 10% annual leak rate. For commercial refrigeration: 20%.
- **Record keeping:** Maintain records of refrigerant purchases, amounts recovered, and amounts charged for systems with 50+ pounds.

**Safety hazards of refrigerants:**
- R-410A operates at approximately 400 psi at 100 degrees F. A line rupture can cause frostbite and projectile injuries.
- Refrigerants are heavier than air and displace oxygen in enclosed spaces. Leaking refrigerant in a mechanical room can create an asphyxiation hazard.
- Some refrigerants (R-32, R-290/propane, R-454B) are flammable (A2L or A3 classification). Follow additional handling precautions for these refrigerants.
- Refrigerant in contact with open flame or hot surfaces can decompose into toxic compounds including phosgene gas and hydrofluoric acid.

**Field tip:** Log every pound of refrigerant you add or recover. Keep records of recovery cylinder certifications. If you find a system low on charge, find and fix the leak before just topping it off -- it is both the law and good practice.

### Common Chemical Hazards on Job Sites

- **PVC cement and primer:** Extremely flammable (flash point below 0 degrees F for many products). Vapors are heavier than air and accumulate in low areas like trenches and crawl spaces. Use in well-ventilated areas. Cap containers immediately after use.
- **Flux and solder:** Lead-free solder is required for potable water per the Safe Drinking Water Act. Flux fumes (particularly zinc chloride and fluoride-based) are irritating and toxic. Provide ventilation. Acid flux is corrosive to skin.
- **Cutting oils and metalworking fluids:** Skin contact can cause dermatitis. Mist inhalation can cause respiratory irritation and metal fume fever. Use gloves and ventilation.
- **Spray foam insulation (isocyanates -- MDI and TDI):** Potent respiratory sensitizers. Once sensitized, even tiny exposures can trigger severe reactions. Proper respiratory protection (full-face with organic vapor cartridges or supplied air) and ventilation are critical during and for 24 hours after application.
- **Concrete and mortar:** Wet concrete is highly alkaline (pH 12-13) and causes chemical burns with prolonged skin contact. "Concrete burns" often appear hours after exposure. Wash any concrete off skin immediately. Long pants, waterproof boots, and gloves for concrete pours.
- **Degreasing solvents:** Many are CNS depressants at high concentrations and can cause cardiac sensitization (sudden cardiac arrest from arrhythmia). Use only in ventilated areas.

**SDS access:** Every chemical on the job site must have a Safety Data Sheet (SDS) available to all workers at all times. Know where they are kept. The SDS tells you the hazards, PPE required, first aid measures, and spill response for every product.

## Hot Work (Welding, Brazing, Soldering) -- OSHA 1926.352-354 / 1910.252

### Fire Watch Requirements (1910.252(a)(2)(iii))

- A fire watch is required whenever welding, cutting, brazing, or soldering is performed in a location where a fire could develop.
- **Conditions requiring a fire watch:** Combustible materials closer than 35 feet that cannot be moved. Combustibles more than 35 feet away that could be ignited by sparks. Wall or floor openings within 35 feet that expose combustibles. Combustible materials on the opposite side of metal walls, ceilings, or partitions.
- The fire watch must remain in the area for at least **30 minutes after hot work is completed**.
- The fire watch must have appropriate fire extinguishing equipment immediately available (minimum 2A:10BC rated extinguisher).
- The fire watch must know how to activate the fire alarm and call the fire department.
- The fire watch must have no other duties during the watch period.
- Fire watch personnel must be trained in the use of fire extinguishing equipment.

### Hot Work Permits

- Required in most facilities and on most construction sites before hot work begins.
- The permit is valid for one shift (typically 8-12 hours) and one specific location.
- Before the permit is issued, the area must be inspected for combustibles and fire hazards.
- The permit must specify: work to be done, exact location, fire prevention measures, names of workers and fire watch, time period.
- The person issuing the permit must physically inspect the area before signing.
- Post the permit at the hot work location.

### Clearance Distances from Combustibles (1910.252(a))

- Remove all combustible materials within **35 feet** of the hot work location.
- If combustibles cannot be removed, cover them with fire-resistant blankets, welding curtains, or metal shields.
- Seal floor openings, wall gaps, pipe penetrations, and duct openings within 35 feet to prevent sparks from reaching concealed combustibles.
- Check the other side of walls, floors, and ceilings for combustibles -- sparks and heat conduct through metal and thin walls.
- Sweep or vacuum combustible dust and debris from the floor.
- Wet down combustible floors if they cannot be protected by other means.

### Brazing and Soldering Safety for Plumbers and HVAC

- **Torch safety:** Use a striker or automatic igniter to light torches, not a cigarette lighter. Keep a fire extinguisher within arm's reach. Use a heat shield (fire cloth or metal) behind the joint to protect nearby materials. Check behind walls before applying heat.
- **Flux fumes:** Brazing flux (fluoride-based) generates toxic fumes. Work in ventilated areas or use local exhaust ventilation. Wear an appropriate respirator (half-face with P100/OV cartridges) in confined or poorly ventilated spaces.
- **Acetylene safety:** Never use acetylene above 15 psi (gauge pressure) -- it becomes shock-sensitive and can spontaneously decompose. Store cylinders upright, secured with chains, and capped when not in use. Keep oxygen and acetylene cylinders at least 20 feet apart or separated by a 5-foot firewall rated at 30-minute fire resistance. Never use oil or grease on regulators or fittings.
- **MAP gas and propane torches:** Lower risk than oxy-acetylene but still present significant fire hazards. Never leave a lit torch unattended. Close the tank valve when done -- do not just turn off the torch handle (gas can leak past the valve seat). Store fuel cylinders away from heat sources.
- **Nitrogen purge:** When brazing on refrigerant lines, always flow dry nitrogen through the pipe to prevent oxide formation inside the joint. This also prevents the creation of toxic fumes from residual refrigerant.
- **After brazing/soldering:** Feel around the work area (carefully) for residual heat before leaving. Insulation, wood framing, and plastic pipe near the joint may be smoldering without visible flame. Check at 15 minutes and 30 minutes.

**Field tip:** More house fires are caused by plumbers and HVAC techs soldering near combustibles than by welders on construction sites. A 5-second application of a torch to a copper fitting can ignite insulation, wood, or paper behind the wall. Always use a heat shield and always do a fire watch. Carry your own fire extinguisher -- do not rely on the homeowner having one.

## First Aid Essentials for Trade Professionals

### Electrical Shock Response

1. **Do not touch the victim** if they may still be in contact with the electrical source. Disconnect the power at the panel or disconnect switch. If you cannot reach the disconnect, use a non-conductive object (dry wood, fiberglass rod, rubber-handled tool) to separate the victim from the source.
2. **Call 911 immediately** for any significant electrical shock (anything beyond a minor tingle from low voltage). Even if the victim appears fine, internal injuries and cardiac effects may be delayed.
3. **Check for breathing and pulse.** Begin CPR if the victim is not breathing and has no pulse. Electrical shock most commonly causes ventricular fibrillation -- CPR and an AED give the best chance of survival.
4. **Treat burns** at entry and exit points. Electrical burns may appear minor on the surface but cause severe internal tissue damage along the current path.
5. **Do not move the victim** unless there is an immediate danger (fire, continued electrical hazard). Electrical shock can cause muscle contractions violent enough to produce spinal injuries and fractures.
6. **Monitor for delayed symptoms:** Cardiac arrhythmias can develop hours after a shock. Any worker who receives a significant shock should be evaluated at a hospital even if they feel fine. An EKG is standard protocol.

**Field tip:** If someone is "locked on" to an energized circuit (muscles contracted, cannot let go), turn off the power at the source. If you cannot reach the disconnect, use a dry 2x4, fiberglass hot stick, or leather belt to knock their hand free. Never grab them with your bare hand -- you will become part of the circuit.

### Burns (Thermal and Chemical)

**Thermal burns (contact, flame, steam, flash):**
- Cool the burn with cool (not cold) running water for at least 10-20 minutes. This is the single most effective first aid measure.
- Do not apply ice, butter, toothpaste, or other home remedies.
- Remove rings, watches, and tight clothing near the burn before swelling begins.
- Cover with a clean, non-stick dressing (cling film works well as a temporary covering).
- Do not break blisters -- they protect the underlying tissue.
- For burns larger than the palm of the worker's hand, or burns on the face, hands, feet, genitals, joints, or circumferential burns around a limb, seek immediate medical attention.

**Chemical burns:**
- Remove contaminated clothing immediately. Cut clothing off if necessary -- do not pull it over the head if it may spread the chemical.
- Flush with large quantities of water for at least 20 minutes. For eyes, use an eyewash station and flush for at least 15 minutes, holding the eyelids open.
- Check the SDS for the specific chemical. Some chemicals have specific first aid protocols: hydrofluoric acid requires calcium gluconate gel application. Dry lime should be brushed off before flushing with water. Phenol requires wiping with polyethylene glycol.
- Do not try to neutralize the chemical (do not put acid on a base burn or vice versa -- the neutralization reaction generates heat and makes it worse).
- Seek medical attention for all chemical burns.

**Steam burns (common for plumbers and HVAC techs working on hydronic and steam systems):**
- Treat the same as thermal burns. Steam burns are often deeper than they initially appear because steam carries significantly more thermal energy per unit than boiling water.
- Shut down the system and relieve pressure before attempting to help the victim.
- Steam burns under clothing are especially severe because the clothing traps the steam against the skin.

### Heat Illness Recognition (OSHA Heat Illness Prevention Campaign)

**Heat cramps:**
- Symptoms: Painful muscle cramps, usually in legs, arms, or abdomen. Heavy sweating.
- Response: Move to shade or cool area. Drink water with electrolytes. Stretch the affected muscles. Rest. Usually resolves within an hour.

**Heat exhaustion:**
- Symptoms: Heavy sweating, weakness, cold/pale/clammy skin, fast/weak pulse, nausea or vomiting, dizziness, headache, muscle cramps, dark urine.
- Response: Move to a cool area. Loosen clothing. Apply cool, wet cloths to skin (neck, armpits, forehead). Sip water slowly. If vomiting occurs, symptoms worsen, or no improvement within 15 minutes, call 911 and seek medical attention.

**Heat stroke (life-threatening medical emergency):**
- Symptoms: Body temperature above 103 degrees F, hot/red/dry or damp skin (sweating may stop), rapid/strong pulse, confusion, slurred speech, irrational behavior, loss of consciousness, seizures.
- Response: **Call 911 immediately.** Move the worker to the coolest environment available. Cool them rapidly by any means available: ice packs on neck, armpits, and groin; cold water immersion if possible (the single most effective cooling method); wet sheets with fans. **Do NOT give fluids** if the person is unconscious or confused.
- The transition from heat exhaustion to heat stroke can happen in minutes. Do not wait.

**Prevention (OSHA's Water-Rest-Shade protocol):**
- **Water:** Drink water every 15-20 minutes even if not thirsty. At least one quart per hour in hot conditions. Cool water, not ice cold.
- **Rest:** Take breaks in shade or air conditioning. At heat index 91-103 degrees F: at least 15 minutes every 2 hours. At 103+: at least 15 minutes every hour. At 115+: work should be rescheduled or heavily modified.
- **Shade:** Provide shade for outdoor rest areas when temperatures exceed 80 degrees F.
- **Acclimatization:** New or returning workers (anyone who has been away from heat for a week or more) should gradually increase exposure over 7-14 days. Start at 20% of full workload, increase 20% per day. More than 50% of OSHA heat fatality cases involve workers in their first few days on the job or returning from time off.

**Field tip:** In attics, crawl spaces, and mechanical rooms during summer, temperatures can exceed 130 degrees F. Monitor each other. If someone stops sweating or becomes confused, treat it as heat stroke -- do not wait for a thermometer reading.

### Cold Stress (Frostbite and Hypothermia)

**Frostbite:**
- Affects extremities first: fingers, toes, nose, ears, cheeks.
- **Frostnip (superficial):** Red skin that turns white or pale, numbness, tingling. Reversible with rewarming. No permanent damage.
- **Frostbite (deep):** White or grayish-yellow skin, hard or waxy texture, numbness, blistering after rewarming. Tissue damage may be permanent.
- Response: Move to a warm area. Do not rub the affected area (causes further tissue damage). Immerse in warm (not hot) water at 100-105 degrees F. Do not use direct heat sources (heaters, exhaust, fires) -- the area is numb and burns easily. Do not break blisters. Seek medical attention for anything beyond mild frostnip.

**Hypothermia:**
- Occurs when body core temperature drops below 95 degrees F.
- **Mild (95-90 degrees F):** Shivering, loss of dexterity, impaired judgment.
- **Moderate (90-82 degrees F):** Violent shivering or shivering may stop, confusion, slurred speech, drowsiness, loss of coordination.
- **Severe (below 82 degrees F):** Shivering stops, weak pulse, slow breathing, loss of consciousness. Cardiac arrest risk is high.
- Response: Call 911 for moderate or severe hypothermia. Move to a warm area. Remove wet clothing. Warm the center of the body first (chest, neck, head, groin) using blankets, skin-to-skin contact, or warm compresses. Give warm beverages only if the person is conscious and alert. Handle gently -- rough movement can trigger cardiac arrest in severely hypothermic patients.

**Wind chill thresholds:**
- At a wind chill of -20 degrees F, exposed skin can develop frostbite in 30 minutes.
- At -35 degrees F, frostbite can occur in as little as 10 minutes.
- At -45 degrees F, frostbite can occur in 5 minutes or less.

**Prevention:**
- Dress in layers: moisture-wicking base layer, insulating middle layer (fleece or wool), windproof/waterproof outer layer.
- Keep spare dry clothes on site. Change out of wet clothing immediately.
- Take warming breaks in a heated enclosure.
- Work in pairs so you can monitor each other for signs of cold stress.
- Schedule outdoor work during the warmest part of the day when possible.
- Increase caloric intake -- the body burns more energy maintaining core temperature in cold conditions.
- Avoid caffeine and alcohol -- both impair the body's temperature regulation.

**Field tip:** Plumbers and HVAC techs doing outdoor work in winter (rooftop units, exterior pipe repairs, frozen pipe thawing) are especially vulnerable because they often work with bare hands for dexterity and stay in one position for extended periods. Use thin liner gloves with grip, chemical hand warmers, and rotate tasks so no one is exposed for more than 30-45 minutes continuously in severe cold.

## Quick Reference: OSHA's "Fatal Four" in Construction

These four hazard categories account for more than 60% of construction worker deaths each year:

1. **Falls:** 33.5% of construction deaths. Fall protection is the most-cited OSHA standard year after year.
2. **Struck by object:** 11.1%. Hard hats, awareness of overhead work, secured tools and materials.
3. **Electrocution:** 8.5%. Verify de-energized. Test before you touch. Use LOTO.
4. **Caught in/between:** 5.5%. Trenching cave-ins, unguarded machinery, caught between equipment and fixed objects.

Eliminate these four hazards and you eliminate the majority of construction fatalities.

## OSHA Reporting Requirements

Employers must report to OSHA:
- **Within 8 hours:** Any workplace fatality.
- **Within 24 hours:** Any in-patient hospitalization, amputation, or loss of an eye.
- Report by calling OSHA at 1-800-321-OSHA (6742) or online at osha.gov.

### Workers' Rights Under OSHA

- Every worker has the right to a safe workplace under the OSH Act of 1970.
- Workers can file a complaint with OSHA if they believe conditions are unsafe. Complaints can be filed anonymously.
- Workers have the right to refuse dangerous work if they believe in good faith that there is imminent danger of death or serious injury, they have asked the employer to fix the hazard, and OSHA has not been able to respond in time.
- Employers cannot retaliate against workers who report safety concerns or file OSHA complaints (Section 11(c) of the OSH Act).
- Workers have the right to request an OSHA inspection.
- Workers have the right to access their exposure and medical records.
- Workers have the right to review the OSHA 300 Log of work-related injuries and illnesses.

**Field tip:** If a general contractor or building owner tells you to ignore a safety requirement, you have the right to refuse. Document the request in writing with the date, time, and who said it. Contact your employer and OSHA if necessary. No job is worth your life or your long-term health.
