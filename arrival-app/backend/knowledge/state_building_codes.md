# State Building Code Amendments — Top 10 States by Construction Volume

> Practical field reference for trade professionals. Focuses on what DIFFERS from national
> model codes (IBC/IRC, NEC, UPC/IPC, IMC, IECC). If something is the same as the national
> code, it is not listed here. This is about what will trip you up when you cross state lines.

---

## 1. CALIFORNIA

### Base Codes Adopted
- California Building Code (CBC) — based on 2021 IBC with extensive state amendments (Title 24, Part 2)
- California Residential Code (CRC) — based on 2021 IRC (Title 24, Part 2.5)
- California Electrical Code (CEC) — based on 2020 NEC with state amendments (Title 24, Part 3)
- California Mechanical Code (CMC) — based on 2021 UMC with amendments (Title 24, Part 4)
- California Plumbing Code (CPC) — based on 2021 UPC with amendments (Title 24, Part 5)
- California Energy Code — Title 24, Part 6 (no national equivalent; this is California-only)
- CalGreen — Title 24, Part 11 (mandatory green building standards, no national equivalent)
- California Fire Code — Title 24, Part 9
- Title 24 is updated on a 3-year cycle. Current cycle is 2022 edition, effective January 1, 2023.

### Key HVAC Amendments
- **Heat pump water heater mandate**: New construction single-family homes must be heat pump ready or install heat pump water heaters as of 2023 Energy Code. 240V outlet, condensate drain, and adequate space required in water heater location.
- **Refrigerant regulations**: California Air Resources Board (CARB) has stricter GWP limits than federal EPA rules. R-410A phase-down is ahead of national schedule. Check CARB regulations for current GWP thresholds by equipment type.
- **Duct sealing**: All new duct systems and altered duct systems must be sealed and tested. Duct leakage testing is MANDATORY — max 5% leakage for new ducts (CEC Section 150.0(m)). This is stricter than most states. Must use a certified HERS rater for verification.
- **HERS verification**: Many HVAC installations require a registered Home Energy Rating System (HERS) rater to verify compliance. This is a California-specific inspection layer on top of normal building department inspection.
- **Equipment efficiency**: California appliance efficiency standards (Title 20) often exceed federal minimums. Check Title 20 before specifying equipment — some units legal elsewhere cannot be sold or installed in CA.
- **Ventilation**: Title 24 Part 6 requires mechanical ventilation in all new homes (ASHRAE 62.2 based). Whole-house ventilation fan required even if windows are operable. Must be wired to run continuously or on a timer.
- **Natural gas bans**: Several cities (Berkeley, San Jose, others) have banned or restricted natural gas in new construction. Check LOCAL ordinances — this varies city by city and is separate from state code.
- **Economizer requirements**: Rooftop units over 54,000 BTU/h must have economizers. Threshold is lower than some states.

### Key Electrical Amendments
- **Solar mandate**: All new single-family and low-rise multifamily (3 stories or less) must have solar PV systems installed (effective 2020). System must meet minimum kW based on conditioned floor area and climate zone. Battery storage is incentivized but not yet mandated statewide.
- **EV readiness**: New single-family: one EV-capable space (240V, 40A circuit). New multifamily: percentage of parking spaces must be EV ready or EV capable depending on building size. This is in CalGreen.
- **AFCI requirements**: California follows NEC AFCI requirements but local jurisdictions may have additional requirements. Check with AHJ.
- **GFCI requirements**: Follows NEC but California requires GFCI protection for all 125V-250V receptacles in garages (broader than some NEC editions).
- **Low-voltage lighting**: Title 24 has mandatory high-efficacy lighting requirements. At least 50% of permanent lighting wattage must be high efficacy (LED qualifies). This goes beyond NEC.
- **JA8 lighting compliance**: Light fixtures in Title 24 buildings must meet Joint Appendix JA8 standards for high efficacy. Not all LED fixtures qualify. Check the JA8 list before specifying fixtures.
- **Wire methods**: CEC generally follows NEC but watch for local amendments. San Francisco requires conduit in many applications where Romex would be allowed elsewhere.
- **Panel upgrades for electrification**: Increasing need for 200A+ panels due to heat pump + EV + solar + battery loads. Plan for it.

### Key Plumbing Amendments
- **Water conservation**: California plumbing fixtures must meet CalGreen mandatory standards. Toilets: 1.28 GPF max (some jurisdictions require 1.1 GPF). Lavatory faucets: 1.2 GPM max. Showerheads: 1.8 GPM max (some jurisdictions 1.5 GPM). Kitchen faucets: 1.8 GPM max.
- **Graywater systems**: California allows graywater systems for subsurface irrigation under CPC Appendix K. Clothes washer graywater systems are allowed without a permit if meeting specific criteria (CPC Section 1502.1).
- **Water heater strapping**: Seismic requirement. All water heaters must be strapped to resist earthquake movement. Two straps required — upper third and lower third. Use approved strapping kits. This applies to replacements too, not just new construction.
- **Gas piping**: CSST (corrugated stainless steel tubing) must be bonded per manufacturer instructions and CEC. Some jurisdictions are phasing out new gas connections entirely — check local rules.
- **Backflow prevention**: California requires backflow prevention on all potable water connections to irrigation systems. Annual testing required for RP (reduced pressure) assemblies. State certification required for backflow testers.
- **Tankless water heater venting**: Must comply with CPC and CMC. Category III or IV venting typically required. Concentric vent terminations must meet clearance-to-openings requirements per CMC.

### Key Building/Structural Amendments
- **Seismic design**: CBC Seismic Design Categories are critical. Most of California is SDC D, E, or F. Structural requirements far exceed most other states. Holdown hardware, shear walls, anchor bolts, and foundation details must meet CBC seismic provisions.
- **Soft-story retrofit**: Many cities (LA, San Francisco, others) have mandatory retrofit ordinances for soft-story buildings (wood-frame with parking at ground level). Not a state code but a common local requirement.
- **Wildfire (WUI)**: Wildland-Urban Interface zones have fire-resistant construction requirements — Chapter 7A of CBC. Fire-rated roofing, ignition-resistant siding, ember-resistant vents, defensible space. Check fire hazard severity zone maps.
- **Energy code (Title 24 Part 6)**: 16 climate zones across the state. Insulation, window, and HVAC requirements vary dramatically by zone. Climate Zone 1 (Arcata) has very different requirements than Climate Zone 15 (Palm Springs). Always verify the climate zone before specifying.
- **Cool roof requirements**: Required in many climate zones. Minimum aged solar reflectance of 0.20 for steep-slope, 0.63 for low-slope. Check Title 24 Part 6.

### Licensing Requirements
- **C-20 HVAC contractor license**: Required for HVAC work. Issued by CSLB (Contractors State License Board). Requires 4 years journey-level experience, trade exam, law/business exam, and a bond.
- **C-10 Electrical contractor license**: Required for electrical work. Same CSLB process.
- **C-36 Plumbing contractor license**: Required for plumbing work. Same CSLB process.
- **C-38 Refrigeration contractor license**: Separate from C-20 for refrigeration-only work.
- **General B license**: Allows general building contracting but NOT specialty trade work unless you also hold the specialty license.
- **No state journeyman license**: California licenses contractors, not individual tradespeople. But certified journeyman programs exist through unions.
- **Reciprocity**: Very limited. California does not broadly recognize other state licenses. Must pass California exams.

### Permit and Inspection Process
- Building permits required for almost all HVAC, electrical, and plumbing work beyond like-for-like replacement.
- **HERS testing**: Required for many energy-related installations. Schedule HERS rater BEFORE final inspection.
- **CF-1R / CF-2R / CF-3R forms**: California-specific compliance forms for energy code. CF-1R is the certificate of compliance (design), CF-2R is the installer certificate, CF-3R is the HERS verification. All three must be registered in the state HERS registry.
- Inspections typically: rough, pre-cover, and final for each trade.
- Plan check turnaround varies wildly by jurisdiction — 2 weeks to 3+ months.
- ADU (Accessory Dwelling Unit) permits have been streamlined by state law — 60-day processing limit.

## 2. TEXAS

### Base Codes Adopted
- Texas does NOT have a single mandatory statewide building code for all construction.
- **Residential**: IRC 2021 is the basis for the Texas residential code, adopted by municipalities that choose to enforce codes. Unincorporated areas may have NO building code enforcement.
- **Commercial**: IBC is commonly adopted by municipalities.
- **Energy**: Texas adopted IECC 2015 for residential (mandatory statewide for new residential construction per state law, even in unincorporated areas). Commercial energy code varies by municipality.
- **Electrical**: NFPA 70 (NEC) is adopted by municipalities that enforce codes. Cycle varies — some cities are on 2020 NEC, others are still on 2017.
- **Plumbing**: Texas has a statewide plumbing code — the Texas Plumbing License Law and state plumbing rules reference the UPC or IPC depending on jurisdiction. Many large cities use IPC.
- **Mechanical**: IMC adopted by municipalities that enforce it. Some use UMC.
- **Key point**: Code adoption is largely a LOCAL decision in Texas outside of energy and plumbing. Always check the specific city.

### Key HVAC Amendments
- **Energy code compliance**: IECC 2015 is mandatory statewide for residential. Duct leakage testing to outside: max 4 CFM25 per 100 sq ft of conditioned floor area (or total duct leakage max 12 CFM25 per 100 sq ft). Manual J load calculations required.
- **Equipment access**: Texas heat means attic installations are extremely common. IRC requires 30"x30" minimum working platform at equipment, solid flooring from access to unit, and a light and switch at the equipment.
- **Condensate drain**: Texas code follows IMC/IRC but with practical emphasis — secondary drain pan with separate drain line required for attic installations. Float switch on primary drain to shut down unit is common local amendment.
- **Refrigerant**: Follows EPA federal rules. No state-specific amendments beyond federal.
- **No state HVAC license**: Texas does not have a statewide HVAC license. HVAC licensing is by municipality. In Houston, you need a city HVAC license. In unincorporated Harris County, you may not. TDLR handles AC and refrigeration contractor registration statewide.
- **TDLR ACR license**: Texas Department of Licensing and Regulation requires ACR (Air Conditioning and Refrigeration) contractor license for AC/refrigeration work statewide. This is separate from municipal HVAC licensing.

### Key Electrical Amendments
- **No statewide electrician license**: Texas licenses electrical work at the local level. Some cities require a master electrician license, others do not. TDLR does issue state electrical licenses that some municipalities accept.
- **TDLR electrical licenses**: Master Electrician, Journeyman Electrician, and other categories available through TDLR. Not mandatory in all jurisdictions but increasingly recognized.
- **NEC cycle**: Varies by city. Dallas, Houston, San Antonio typically adopt within 1-2 cycles of current NEC. Verify which edition is enforced locally.
- **Solar/EV**: No statewide solar or EV readiness mandate. Some cities (Austin, San Antonio) have local requirements.
- **Wire methods**: Romex (NM-B) is widely permitted in residential. No statewide conduit requirements beyond NEC.
- **AFCI/GFCI**: Follows adopted NEC edition. No state amendments beyond NEC.

### Key Plumbing Amendments
- **State plumbing license**: Texas DOES require a statewide plumbing license through TSBPE (Texas State Board of Plumbing Examiners). Journeyman and Master Plumber licenses. One of the few trades with true statewide licensing in Texas.
- **Water heater installation**: TPR valve discharge must terminate 6" above grade or floor drain. Pan required for attic and interior installations. Gas water heaters in garages must be elevated 18" (ignition source) — standard code but strictly enforced.
- **Gas piping**: CSST bonding required. LP gas is common in rural Texas — different regulator and orifice requirements than natural gas. Always verify fuel type.
- **Backflow prevention**: Required per state plumbing rules. Annual testing required for commercial RP assemblies.
- **Water conservation**: No statewide low-flow mandates beyond federal EPAct standards, but cities like Austin and San Antonio have stricter local water conservation codes.

### Key Building/Structural Amendments
- **Wind/hurricane codes**: Coastal counties from Beaumont to Brownsville are in designated windborne debris regions. Impact-rated glazing or shutters required. Design wind speeds 130-150+ mph along the coast. Texas Windstorm Insurance Association (TWIA) requires WPI-8 inspection certificate for wind-resistant construction in designated areas.
- **WPI-8 certificate**: If the structure is in a TWIA-designated area (first-tier and second-tier coastal counties), construction must pass a windstorm inspection to get insurance. This is a separate inspection from the building department.
- **Slab foundations**: Very common in Texas due to expansive clay soils. Post-tension and conventional rebar slabs both used. Foundation design must account for soil conditions — geotechnical report commonly required.
- **No statewide building code enforcement in unincorporated areas**: Outside city limits, there may be no building inspections at all for non-residential energy code items. But IECC residential and plumbing are still state law.

### Licensing Requirements
- **Plumbing**: State license required (TSBPE). Journeyman, Master, Medical Gas endorsements.
- **Electrical**: TDLR state license available. Not required everywhere but recommended. Many cities accept it.
- **HVAC**: TDLR ACR license required statewide for AC and refrigeration. Municipal licenses may also be needed.
- **General contractor**: No state license. Some cities require registration.
- **Reciprocity**: Limited. Texas does not broadly recognize other state licenses.

### Permit and Inspection Process
- Permit requirements vary 100% by jurisdiction.
- In cities: permits required for most trade work. Plan review, inspections at rough and final.
- In unincorporated areas: may have zero permit requirements beyond plumbing and energy.
- Houston is notably unique — no zoning code, but does enforce building codes within city limits.
- Inspection scheduling is typically online or phone-based in major cities.

## 3. FLORIDA

### Base Codes Adopted
- **Florida Building Code (FBC)** — this is NOT a simple adoption of the IBC. Florida has its own code development process. FBC is based on IBC/IRC but with extensive Florida-specific amendments.
- FBC 7th Edition (2020), based on 2018 IBC/IRC with Florida amendments.
- FBC 8th Edition (2023), based on 2021 IBC/IRC, effective December 31, 2023.
- **Florida Building Code — Mechanical**: Based on IMC with amendments.
- **Florida Building Code — Plumbing**: Based on IPC with amendments.
- **Florida Building Code — Fuel Gas**: Based on IFGC with amendments.
- **Electrical**: Florida adopts NEC. Currently NEC 2023 (as of FBC 8th Edition). Administered separately through the Florida Electrical Code.
- **Energy**: Florida Building Code — Energy Conservation, based on IECC with amendments.
- All codes are adopted statewide. Local governments can adopt MORE restrictive amendments only through a formal process with the Florida Building Commission.

### Key HVAC Amendments
- **Hurricane protection for outdoor equipment**: Rooftop and outdoor HVAC equipment must be anchored to resist design wind loads. Equipment tie-downs and curb attachments must be engineered for the specific wind zone.
- **High-velocity hurricane zone (HVHZ)**: Miami-Dade and Broward counties have the most stringent requirements in the nation. Equipment, ductwork, and supports in HVHZ must meet additional testing requirements (Miami-Dade NOA — Notice of Acceptance — or Florida Product Approval).
- **Duct systems in HVHZ**: Ductwork must be secured to resist 180 mph winds (HVHZ). Duct joints must be mechanically fastened AND sealed. More stringent than standard FBC.
- **Energy code**: Florida's energy code is generally less stringent than California's but applies statewide. Duct leakage testing required: max 4 CFM25 per 100 sq ft to outside.
- **Condensate drain requirements**: Primary and secondary drains required when damage to building components would result from overflow. Float switch or secondary drain required for attic and above-ceiling installations. Condensate drain must terminate in a visible location.
- **Mold/humidity**: Not explicitly in code but Florida inspectors pay close attention to moisture management. Supply duct insulation must be vapor-sealed. Return air pathways must not create negative pressure that draws humid outside air in.

### Key Electrical Amendments
- **NEC adoption**: Florida adopts NEC with amendments through the Florida Electrical Code process. Currently on 2023 NEC.
- **AFCI/GFCI**: Follows NEC requirements. No state-level relaxation of AFCI requirements (unlike some states).
- **Surge protection**: NEC 2020 requires whole-house surge protection (SPD Type 1 or Type 2) for dwelling units. Florida enforces this due to high lightning exposure.
- **Generator installations**: Extremely common in Florida. Transfer switches must be properly installed with utility disconnects. Standby generator installations must meet NEC Article 702.
- **Wind-rated electrical equipment**: In HVHZ, electrical panels, meters, and service equipment must be rated for wind exposure or installed in protected locations. Meter cans and weather heads must be properly secured.
- **Solar**: Florida Solar Rights Act prohibits HOAs from banning solar installations. No statewide solar mandate for new construction like California, but solar is widely installed.
- **EV readiness**: No statewide EV readiness mandate as of 2024, but some local jurisdictions are adopting requirements.

### Key Plumbing Amendments
- **Backflow prevention**: Florida is strict on backflow. Cross-connection control programs are mandatory in most jurisdictions. Annual testing required for RP and DC assemblies.
- **Polybutylene (PB) piping**: While not explicitly banned by current code, PB pipe (common in 1980s Florida construction) is a known failure point. Re-piping with approved materials (copper, CPVC, PEX) is a major industry in Florida.
- **Water heater installation**: TPR discharge to outside or approved location. Drain pan with drain for indoor installations where leakage could cause damage.
- **Gas piping**: Natural gas is less common in Florida than many states. LP gas is used in many areas. CSST bonding required per NEC and manufacturer instructions.
- **Water conservation**: Federal EPAct standards apply. No state mandate beyond federal for low-flow fixtures, but local water utilities may have stricter requirements.

### Key Building/Structural Amendments
- **Wind design**: This is THE defining feature of the FBC. All structures must be designed for wind. Design wind speeds range from 130 mph inland to 180+ mph in HVHZ (Miami-Dade, Broward). FBC Section 1620 and ASCE 7 govern wind design.
- **Impact-rated openings**: In the Wind-Borne Debris Region (roughly the coast and all of South Florida), all glazed openings must be protected with impact-rated glazing or approved shutters. Large missile test (9-lb 2x4 at 50 fps) and small missile test apply.
- **Miami-Dade NOA**: In the HVHZ, products must have a Miami-Dade County Notice of Acceptance (NOA) proving they meet HVHZ testing requirements. This applies to roofing, windows, doors, shutters, HVAC equipment, and many other products. A Florida Product Approval may be accepted in some cases.
- **Roof-to-wall connections**: Enhanced nailing schedules, hurricane clips or straps required connecting rafters/trusses to wall plates. Specific requirements vary by wind zone.
- **Flood zone construction**: FEMA flood zone requirements are heavily enforced. Base Flood Elevation (BFE) + freeboard requirements. Coastal construction must resist wave action, erosion, and flooding.
- **Energy code**: IECC-based with Florida amendments. Insulation requirements are moderate (R-30 ceiling, R-13 wall typical) but air sealing and duct sealing are emphasized.

### Licensing Requirements
- **HVAC**: State license required through DBPR (Department of Business and Professional Regulation). Class A (unlimited) and Class B (limited to specific tonnage/BTU). Journeyman certification also available.
- **Electrical**: State license required. Master Electrician and Journeyman Electrician through DBPR.
- **Plumbing**: State license required. Master Plumber and Journeyman Plumber through DBPR.
- **General Contractor**: State license required for work over $5,000. Certified (state) or Registered (local).
- **Reciprocity**: Florida has some reciprocity agreements. Check DBPR for current reciprocal states.
- **Continuing education**: All Florida trade licenses require continuing education, including hours on the Florida Building Code and Florida-specific requirements.

### Permit and Inspection Process
- Permits required statewide for all regulated work.
- Most jurisdictions offer online permitting.
- **Product approval system**: Florida Building Product Approval system — verify products are FL-approved BEFORE installation. Search the FL Product Approval database.
- **Threshold inspections**: For certain large buildings, a Special Inspector (threshold inspector) is required in addition to building department inspections.
- Inspection stages: foundation, framing/rough (each trade), insulation, final.
- In HVHZ: additional inspections for roof deck, roof cover, opening protection.
- **Private provider inspections**: Florida allows private inspection providers as an alternative to building department inspections. Can speed up the process.

## 4. NEW YORK

### Base Codes Adopted
- **New York State**: Adopts the Uniform Fire Prevention and Building Code, based on IBC/IRC 2021, NEC 2020, IMC 2021, IPC 2021, IFGC 2021, IECC 2021. Applies statewide EXCEPT in New York City.
- **New York City**: Has its OWN completely separate code system. NYC Building Code, NYC Mechanical Code, NYC Plumbing Code, NYC Fuel Gas Code, NYC Energy Conservation Code (NYCECC). NYC codes are based on IBC/IRC but heavily amended.
- **NYC Electrical Code**: Based on NEC but with NYC-specific amendments administered by the DOB.
- **Key point**: NYC is essentially a separate code universe. If you work in both NYC and the rest of New York State, you must know two completely different code systems.

### Key HVAC Amendments
- **NYC**: Equipment installation in NYC often requires professional engineer (PE) or registered architect (RA) sign-off for DOB filing. Even routine replacements may need a permit with PE/RA-stamped documents.
- **NYC Local Law 97**: Greenhouse gas emissions limits for large buildings (over 25,000 sq ft). This affects HVAC system selection — building owners face fines for excessive emissions. Electrification of heating is being pushed to comply.
- **NYC Local Law 154**: Restricts fossil fuel combustion in new buildings (effective 2024 for buildings under 7 stories, 2027 for taller buildings). New buildings must use electric heating, heat pumps, or qualifying hybrid systems. Massive impact on HVAC design.
- **NYS Energy Code**: Based on IECC 2021 with amendments. Insulation requirements, equipment efficiency, and duct sealing requirements. More stringent than many southern states.
- **Refrigerant**: Follows EPA federal rules. NYC Department of Environmental Protection (DEP) requires notification for large refrigerant charges.
- **Steam systems**: NYC has extensive steam heating infrastructure. Knowledge of steam systems (one-pipe, two-pipe, steam traps, PRVs) is essential for NYC HVAC work. Not common in most other states.

### Key Electrical Amendments
- **NYC Electrical Code**: Based on NEC but with amendments. Key difference: NYC still requires many wiring methods that are more restrictive than NEC minimums.
- **Conduit requirements in NYC**: NM-B (Romex) is severely restricted in NYC. Most electrical work requires conduit (EMT, RMC, or FMC). This is a massive difference from most other states where Romex is standard residential wiring.
- **Licensed electrician requirement**: ALL electrical work in NYC must be performed by or under the direct supervision of a NYC Licensed Master Electrician or Special Electrician. Work must be filed with DOB under the master electrician's license.
- **AFCI/GFCI**: NYC follows its own electrical code adoption. Check the current NYC Electrical Code for specific AFCI/GFCI requirements — they may lag behind the latest NEC edition.
- **Service equipment**: NYC has specific requirements for meter placement, service entrance, and coordination with the utility (Con Edison, PSEG, etc.). Utility coordination is a more formal process than in many jurisdictions.
- **Solar**: NYC has solar incentive programs and building code provisions for solar. NYC Building Code requires solar-ready zones on certain new buildings (Local Law 92/94).
- **EV readiness**: NYC Local Law 130 requires EV charging infrastructure in new parking facilities. Percentage of spaces must be EV-ready.

### Key Plumbing Amendments
- **NYC Plumbing Code**: Separate from IPC. Based on IPC but with extensive NYC amendments.
- **NYC Licensed Master Plumber**: ALL plumbing work must be performed by or under a NYC Licensed Master Plumber. Work filed with DOB under the master plumber's license.
- **Backflow prevention**: NYC DEP has a rigorous cross-connection control program. Annual testing, certified testers, reports filed with DEP.
- **Water conservation**: NYC has water conservation requirements. Low-flow fixtures required per NYC Plumbing Code.
- **Gas piping**: NYC has strict requirements for gas piping inspection and testing. After the 2014 and 2015 gas explosions, Local Law 152 requires periodic gas piping inspections for buildings. Gas piping work requires a Licensed Master Plumber.
- **Lead pipe replacement**: Ongoing concern in older NYC buildings. Lead service lines and interior lead piping must be addressed during renovations.

### Key Building/Structural Amendments
- **NYC Building Code**: Entirely separate from IBC. Construction classifications, occupancy groups, and means of egress calculations differ.
- **NYC DOB filing**: All regulated work requires a DOB filing (work permit). Filings are categorized as New Building (NB), Alteration Type 1 (Alt1), Alteration Type 2 (Alt2), or Alteration Type 3 (Alt3). Most trade work is Alt2 or Alt3.
- **NYC Energy Conservation Code (NYCECC)**: Based on IECC but with NYC amendments. Local Law 97 emissions limits drive stricter requirements than the energy code alone.
- **Facade inspection**: Local Law 11 (FISP — Facade Inspection Safety Program) requires periodic facade inspections for buildings over 6 stories. Not directly a trade code but affects exterior work.
- **Seismic**: NYC is in Seismic Design Category C (moderate). While not as severe as California, seismic requirements do exist and must be followed.

### Licensing Requirements
- **NYC Master Electrician**: Required for all NYC electrical work. Very difficult to obtain — 7.5 years of experience (3.5 as journeyman), exam, background check. NYC license only; not valid elsewhere in NYS.
- **NYC Master Plumber**: Required for all NYC plumbing work. 7 years experience, exam, background check. NYC license only.
- **NYC Licensed Refrigerating System Operating Engineer**: Required for operating large refrigeration systems.
- **NYS outside NYC**: Different licensing requirements by municipality. No single statewide trade license requirement, but many jurisdictions require licensing.
- **Reciprocity**: Minimal. NYC licenses are NYC-specific. No reciprocity.
- **Fire suppression**: NYC requires a Certificate of Fitness (COF) for various fire safety activities from FDNY.

### Permit and Inspection Process
- **NYC DOB**: All work requires a DOB filing. Filed electronically through DOB NOW.
- **Professional certification**: NYC allows Professionally Certified (self-certified) filings for certain work types, where a PE/RA certifies compliance instead of DOB plan review. Faster but the PE/RA assumes liability.
- **DOB inspections**: Required at multiple stages. Progress inspections, rough inspections, final inspections.
- **Certificate of Occupancy**: Required for new buildings and certain alterations. Temporary Certificate of Occupancy (TCO) commonly issued while final items are completed.
- **Violations**: NYC DOB issues violations (DOB violations, ECB violations) that carry fines. Violations must be resolved before sign-off.
- **Special inspections**: Required for structural work, concrete, steel, fireproofing. Must be performed by a NYC Special Inspection Agency.

## 5. PENNSYLVANIA

### Base Codes Adopted
- Pennsylvania Uniform Construction Code (UCC) — statewide mandatory code.
- Based on ICC codes: IBC 2018, IRC 2018, IMC 2018, IPC 2018, IFGC 2018, IECC 2018 (with PA amendments). Note: PA is often 1-2 cycles behind current ICC editions.
- NEC 2017 adopted through UCC (check for updates — may have moved to 2020 NEC).
- **Important**: The UCC applies to all commercial construction and residential construction statewide. However, municipalities can opt out of residential enforcement only — in those places, there may be no residential building inspections.
- Over 1,500 municipalities have opted out of residential enforcement. Check if the project municipality enforces residential UCC.

### Key HVAC Amendments
- **Energy code**: IECC 2018 with PA amendments. Duct leakage testing required for new systems in conditioned space.
- **Equipment efficiency**: Follows IECC minimums. No state requirements beyond federal/IECC.
- **Refrigerant**: Follows EPA federal rules. No state-specific amendments.
- **Pennsylvania-specific**: No heat pump mandates, no gas bans. Conventional fuel-fired equipment widely used.
- **Combustion air**: PA follows IMC for combustion air requirements. Tight construction and energy code compliance can create combustion air issues — combustion air must be properly provided for atmospheric equipment.

### Key Electrical Amendments
- **NEC 2017**: Adopted with PA amendments (verify if updated to 2020). PA may lag behind states that adopt NEC faster.
- **AFCI**: PA follows adopted NEC edition requirements. Check if any provisions have been amended out.
- **Wire methods**: NM-B (Romex) permitted in residential per NEC. No statewide conduit requirements beyond NEC.
- **Solar/EV**: No statewide mandates for solar or EV readiness in new construction.

### Key Plumbing Amendments
- **IPC adopted**: Pennsylvania uses IPC (not UPC). This matters if you are coming from a UPC state (like California) — different fixture count methods, different trap arm lengths, different sizing methods.
- **Water conservation**: Follows federal EPAct standards. No state mandates beyond federal.
- **Backflow**: Required per IPC. Local water authorities may have additional cross-connection control requirements.

### Key Building/Structural Amendments
- **Seismic**: Eastern PA (Philadelphia area) is in moderate seismic zones (SDC B-C). Western PA is lower. Seismic design required per IBC.
- **Snow loads**: Northern PA has significant snow loads (40-70 psf ground snow load). Roof structures must be designed accordingly. This exceeds what techs from southern states may be familiar with.
- **Energy code**: IECC 2018. Climate zones 4A, 5A, and 6A in PA. Zone 5A (most of PA) requires R-49 ceiling, R-20 wall, substantial air sealing. Much more insulation than southern states.
- **Radon**: PA has significant radon risk areas. Radon-resistant new construction (RRNC) is required in many jurisdictions. Includes sub-slab depressurization system rough-in (sealed slab, vent pipe, electrical for fan).

### Licensing Requirements
- **No statewide trade licensing for electricians, plumbers, or HVAC techs at the state level for journey workers.** This is a significant difference from states like Florida or California.
- **Municipalities license trades locally.** Philadelphia, Pittsburgh, and many other cities require trade licenses. Requirements vary by municipality.
- **Home improvement contractor registration**: State registration required for home improvement contractors through the PA Attorney General's office.
- **Reciprocity**: N/A at state level. Local licensing varies.

### Permit and Inspection Process
- **UCC municipalities**: Permits required, inspections by municipal code officials or third-party agencies.
- **Opted-out municipalities (residential)**: No permit or inspection required for residential construction. Commercial still requires UCC compliance.
- **Third-party agencies**: PA allows third-party inspection agencies for both plan review and inspections. Common in municipalities without their own code enforcement staff.
- **Inspection stages**: Foundation, framing/rough, insulation/energy, final.

## 6. ILLINOIS

### Base Codes Adopted
- Illinois does NOT have a mandatory statewide building code for all municipalities.
- **Residential**: Illinois Energy Conservation Code is mandatory statewide (IECC 2018 with IL amendments). Other residential codes are adopted locally.
- **Commercial**: Illinois adopts IBC for state-owned buildings. Local municipalities adopt their own commercial building codes.
- **Chicago**: Completely separate code system. Chicago Building Code, Chicago Electrical Code, Chicago Plumbing Code, Chicago Mechanical Code. These are CITY codes, not based on ICC model codes.
- **Outside Chicago**: Most municipalities adopt ICC codes (IBC, IRC, NEC, IPC, IMC) but at varying cycles.

### Key HVAC Amendments
- **Chicago**: Chicago Mechanical Code governs HVAC work in the city. Differs from IMC in many specifics. Permitting through Chicago Department of Buildings (DOB).
- **Energy code statewide**: IECC 2018 with Illinois amendments is mandatory. Equipment efficiency and duct sealing requirements per IECC apply even in jurisdictions without other code enforcement.
- **No state HVAC license**: Illinois does not have a statewide HVAC license. Chicago and some other municipalities require local licenses.

### Key Electrical Amendments
- **Chicago EMT conduit mandate**: THE biggest difference. Chicago requires EMT (Electrical Metallic Tubing) conduit for virtually ALL electrical wiring in ALL building types. NM-B (Romex) is NOT permitted in Chicago, even in single-family residential. This is unique among major US cities.
- **Chicago Electrical Code**: Separate from NEC. Based on NEC but with extensive amendments. The conduit requirement is the most notable.
- **Outside Chicago**: Most municipalities adopt NEC. Romex permitted in residential per NEC. The contrast between Chicago and suburban electrical work is stark.
- **Illinois state**: No statewide electrician license. Chicago requires a Chicago electrical license.
- **AFCI in Chicago**: Chicago has historically been slower to adopt AFCI requirements. Check current Chicago Electrical Code for specific AFCI requirements.

### Key Plumbing Amendments
- **Chicago Plumbing Code**: Separate from IPC. Has unique requirements for fixture counts, pipe sizing, and materials.
- **Illinois Plumbing License Act**: Illinois DOES have a statewide plumbing license. Licensed Plumber required for plumbing work throughout the state. Administered by IDFPR (Illinois Department of Financial and Professional Regulation).
- **IPC vs Chicago**: Most of Illinois outside Chicago follows IPC. Chicago follows its own plumbing code. Materials and methods differ.
- **Lead service line replacement**: Illinois has requirements for lead service line identification and replacement. Significant issue in older buildings.

### Key Building/Structural Amendments
- **Chicago Building Code**: Prescriptive code with fire-resistive construction emphasis. Chicago's history with major fires shapes its code — fire separation, noncombustible construction, and fire protection are paramount.
- **Seismic**: Illinois is in moderate seismic zones in the southern portion (New Madrid zone influence). SDC B-C in most areas.
- **Energy code**: IECC 2018 statewide. Climate zones 4A (southern IL) and 5A (most of IL including Chicago). R-49 ceiling, R-20 wall typical for zone 5A.
- **Snow loads**: 25-40 psf ground snow load typical across Illinois. Roof design must account for this.

### Licensing Requirements
- **Plumbing**: Statewide license required (IDFPR). Apprentice, Journeyman, and Master Plumber.
- **Electrical**: No statewide license. Chicago requires Chicago-specific electrician license. Other municipalities vary.
- **HVAC**: No statewide license. Chicago and some municipalities require local HVAC licenses.
- **Roofing**: Illinois requires a Roofing Contractor license for roofing work over $1,000.
- **Reciprocity**: Very limited. Chicago licenses are Chicago-specific.

### Permit and Inspection Process
- **Chicago**: All regulated work requires Chicago DOB permit. Filings through Chicago DOB online system. Inspections by Chicago DOB inspectors.
- **Outside Chicago**: Varies by municipality. Many suburban jurisdictions have robust permit and inspection programs. Some rural areas have minimal enforcement.
- **Chicago inspections**: Known for strict enforcement. Conduit requirements are heavily inspected. Expect multiple inspections.

## 7. OHIO

### Base Codes Adopted
- **Ohio Building Code (OBC)**: Statewide mandatory code for commercial construction. Based on IBC 2018 with Ohio amendments. Administered by the Ohio Board of Building Standards.
- **Residential Code of Ohio (RCO)**: Based on IRC 2018 with Ohio amendments. Applies to one-, two-, and three-family dwellings.
- **Ohio Mechanical Code**: Based on IMC 2018.
- **Ohio Plumbing Code**: Based on IPC 2018.
- **Electrical**: NEC 2017 adopted statewide (check for updates).
- **Energy**: IECC 2018 (commercial) and IRC Chapter 11 (residential).
- **Key point**: Ohio has a true statewide code for both residential and commercial. Unlike Texas or Illinois, codes apply uniformly across the state.

### Key HVAC Amendments
- **Ohio amendments to IMC**: Generally minor. Ohio follows IMC closely.
- **Energy code**: IECC 2018. Climate zones 4A (southern OH) and 5A (most of OH). Duct leakage testing, insulation requirements, equipment efficiency per IECC.
- **No state HVAC license**: Ohio does not have a statewide HVAC contractor or technician license. Licensing is local. Some municipalities (Cleveland, Columbus, Cincinnati) require HVAC licenses.
- **Refrigerant**: EPA Section 608 certification required (federal). No state-specific additions.

### Key Electrical Amendments
- **NEC 2017 adopted**: Ohio may lag behind states on faster NEC adoption cycles.
- **No statewide electrician license**: Ohio licenses electrical work locally. Many municipalities have their own electrician licensing. Columbus, Cleveland, and Cincinnati all have local licensing.
- **Wire methods**: Romex permitted in residential per NEC. No statewide conduit requirements beyond NEC.
- **AFCI/GFCI**: Follows adopted NEC. No state amendments relaxing NEC requirements.

### Key Plumbing Amendments
- **Ohio Plumbing Code**: Based on IPC. Ohio has some specific amendments regarding backflow prevention and testing.
- **State plumbing license**: Ohio DOES require a statewide plumbing license. Administered by the Ohio Construction Industry Licensing Board (OCILB). Contractor license required.
- **Backflow**: Ohio EPA has requirements for backflow prevention and cross-connection control. Annual testing for RP assemblies.
- **Water heater requirements**: TPR valve, discharge piping, drain pan where required. No amendments significantly different from IPC.

### Key Building/Structural Amendments
- **Seismic**: SDC A-B for most of Ohio. Moderate concern only. Basic seismic provisions apply.
- **Snow loads**: 20-30 psf ground snow load across most of Ohio. Higher in northeast (lake effect zones — up to 40 psf near Lake Erie).
- **Energy code**: Climate Zone 5A for most of the state. R-49 ceiling, R-20 or R-13+5ci wall. Blower door testing for air sealing verification may be required.
- **Radon**: Ohio has radon risk areas. RRNC recommended in many counties but not mandated statewide in the building code. Check local amendments.

### Licensing Requirements
- **Plumbing**: Statewide license through OCILB.
- **Electrical**: Local licenses. No statewide electrician license.
- **HVAC**: Local licenses. No statewide HVAC license.
- **General contractor**: No statewide GC license. Local registration in some municipalities.
- **Reciprocity**: Limited. Local licensing varies too much for broad reciprocity.

### Permit and Inspection Process
- **Statewide codes enforced**: OBC and RCO are mandatory. Building departments enforce them.
- **Certified building departments**: Ohio certifies local building departments. Municipalities without certified departments use the state (Ohio Board of Building Standards) for plan review and inspections.
- **Industrial exemption**: Ohio has an Industrial Exemption for certain industrial facilities where the owner assumes responsibility for code compliance instead of the building department.
- **Inspections**: Foundation, framing/rough, insulation, final. Each trade inspected at rough and final.

## 8. GEORGIA

### Base Codes Adopted
- Georgia adopts state minimum codes through the Georgia Department of Community Affairs (DCA).
- **IBC 2018** with Georgia amendments (commercial).
- **IRC 2018** with Georgia amendments (residential).
- **IMC 2018** with Georgia amendments.
- **IPC 2018** with Georgia amendments.
- **IFGC 2018** with Georgia amendments.
- **NEC 2020** (Georgia adopted 2020 NEC — verify current cycle).
- **IECC 2015** with Georgia amendments for residential. IECC 2015/ASHRAE 90.1-2013 for commercial.
- **Key point**: Georgia's energy code is a cycle or more behind current IECC. This is intentional — Georgia has been slower to adopt the most recent energy codes.

### Key HVAC Amendments
- **Energy code (IECC 2015)**: Less stringent than states on 2018 or 2021 IECC. Duct leakage testing required for new construction: max 4 CFM25 per 100 sq ft to outside (or 12 CFM25 total). Manual J required.
- **Refrigerant**: Federal EPA rules apply. No state-specific amendments.
- **Equipment access**: Georgia follows IMC. Attic installations require working platform, light, switch, and solid flooring to equipment.
- **No state HVAC license**: Georgia does not have a statewide HVAC technician license. Conditioned Air Contractor license required through the Georgia Construction Industry Licensing Board. Two classes: Class I (unlimited) and Class II (limited).

### Key Electrical Amendments
- **NEC 2020 adopted**: Georgia is relatively current on NEC adoption.
- **State electrical license**: Georgia requires statewide electrical licenses through the Georgia Construction Industry Licensing Board. Journeyman Electrician, Master Electrician, and Electrical Contractor.
- **AFCI**: Georgia follows NEC 2020 AFCI requirements without significant amendments.
- **Wire methods**: Romex permitted in residential per NEC. No conduit mandates beyond NEC.
- **Solar/EV**: No statewide mandates for solar or EV readiness in new construction.

### Key Plumbing Amendments
- **IPC adopted**: Georgia uses IPC (not UPC).
- **State plumbing license**: Georgia requires statewide plumbing licenses. Journeyman Plumber, Master Plumber, and Plumbing Contractor through the Construction Industry Licensing Board.
- **Backflow**: Georgia EPD (Environmental Protection Division) has requirements for backflow prevention. Local water authorities administer cross-connection control programs.
- **Water conservation**: Georgia has WaterSense requirements for state-funded buildings. No statewide residential mandate beyond federal EPAct.

### Key Building/Structural Amendments
- **Seismic**: Most of Georgia is SDC A-B. Low to moderate seismic concern. Basic seismic provisions of IBC apply.
- **Wind**: Coastal Georgia (Savannah area) has increased wind speed requirements (130+ mph design wind). Inland areas are lower (115 mph typical).
- **Energy code (IECC 2015)**: Climate zone 2A (south GA) through 4A (north GA mountains). Zone 3A covers most of the state. R-38 ceiling, R-13 wall typical for zone 3A. Less insulation than northern states.
- **Georgia amendments to energy code**: Georgia has specific amendments that relax some IECC requirements. For example, certain equipment efficiency requirements may follow Georgia-specific tables rather than IECC defaults.
- **Termite/pest**: Georgia requires termite treatment for new construction. Not a building code item per se, but required for Certificate of Occupancy in most jurisdictions.

### Licensing Requirements
- **Electrical**: State license required (Construction Industry Licensing Board).
- **Plumbing**: State license required (Construction Industry Licensing Board).
- **HVAC**: Conditioned Air Contractor license required (Construction Industry Licensing Board). Also need EPA 608 for refrigerant handling.
- **General contractor**: State license required for GC and certain specialty contractors.
- **Reciprocity**: Georgia has limited reciprocity agreements. Check with the licensing board for current reciprocal states.

### Permit and Inspection Process
- **Local enforcement**: Counties and municipalities enforce the state minimum codes. Some jurisdictions may be more stringent.
- **Permits required**: For all regulated work — new construction, additions, alterations, and equipment replacements.
- **Inspections**: Standard sequence — foundation, framing, rough (each trade), insulation, final.
- **DCA involvement**: Georgia DCA provides training, certification, and oversight for local code officials.
- **Unincorporated areas**: Some unincorporated areas of Georgia have limited code enforcement. Verify with the county.

## 9. NORTH CAROLINA

### Base Codes Adopted
- **NC State Building Code**: North Carolina has a statewide mandatory building code. Administered by the NC Building Code Council under OSFM (Office of State Fire Marshal).
- Based on IBC 2018 with NC amendments (NC Building Code).
- IRC 2018 with NC amendments (NC Residential Code).
- IMC 2018 with NC amendments (NC Mechanical Code).
- IPC 2018 with NC amendments (NC Plumbing Code). NOTE: NC previously used a state plumbing code based on neither IPC nor UPC — now based on IPC.
- IFGC 2018 with NC amendments (NC Fuel Gas Code).
- NEC 2020 with NC amendments (NC Electrical Code).
- IECC 2018 with NC amendments (NC Energy Conservation Code).
- **Important**: Local jurisdictions in NC CANNOT adopt codes more or less restrictive than the state code. The NC Building Code is the uniform standard statewide.

### Key HVAC Amendments
- **NC Mechanical Code**: Based on IMC with NC amendments. Generally follows IMC closely.
- **Energy code (IECC 2018)**: Duct leakage testing required. NC amendments specify testing protocols.
- **Equipment changeout requirements**: NC has specific requirements for equipment replacement. Manual J (or equivalent) load calculation may be required for equipment changeouts in some jurisdictions. Duct assessment required.
- **Refrigerant**: Federal EPA rules. No state-specific amendments.
- **HVAC licensing**: NC requires statewide HVAC licensing. H-1, H-2, H-3 classifications through the NC Board of Examiners of Plumbing, Heating and Fire Sprinkler Contractors.

### Key Electrical Amendments
- **NEC 2020 with NC amendments**: NC has specific amendments to NEC. Check the NC Electrical Code for deviations.
- **State electrical license**: NC requires statewide electrical licenses through the NC Board of Examiners of Electrical Contractors. Unlimited, Intermediate, Limited, and SP (special restricted) classifications.
- **AFCI**: NC follows NEC 2020 AFCI requirements. NC has not amended out AFCI requirements.
- **Wire methods**: Romex permitted in residential per NEC. No conduit mandates beyond NEC.
- **Ground-fault protection**: NC follows NEC. No additional state requirements.

### Key Plumbing Amendments
- **NC Plumbing Code**: Now based on IPC with NC amendments. Historical NC plumbing code had many unique provisions — some may persist as amendments.
- **State plumbing license**: NC requires statewide plumbing licenses through the NC Board of Examiners of Plumbing, Heating and Fire Sprinkler Contractors. P-I, P-II classifications.
- **Backflow**: NC DEQ (Department of Environmental Quality) oversees cross-connection control. Local water systems implement programs.
- **Well water**: Common in rural NC. Wells must meet NC well construction standards. Plumbing systems on well water have specific requirements.

### Key Building/Structural Amendments
- **Hurricane/wind**: Coastal NC is in hurricane zones. Design wind speeds 130-160+ mph along the Outer Banks and coast. Wind-borne debris region applies.
- **Flood zone**: Coastal plain of NC has extensive flood zones. FEMA flood zone requirements strictly enforced.
- **Seismic**: SDC A-B for most of NC. Western NC mountains may be SDC B-C due to geological factors.
- **Energy code (IECC 2018)**: Climate zones 3A (coastal), 4A (piedmont), 5A (mountains). Insulation requirements vary: R-38 ceiling / R-13 wall (zone 3A) to R-49 ceiling / R-20 wall (zone 5A).
- **Wood frame construction**: NC allows Type V-B (unprotected wood frame) for residential and small commercial. Termite treatment required in many areas.

### Licensing Requirements
- **HVAC**: State license required. NC Board of Examiners of Plumbing, Heating and Fire Sprinkler Contractors. H-1 (unlimited), H-2 (limited), H-3 (restricted).
- **Plumbing**: State license required. Same board. P-I (unlimited), P-II (limited).
- **Electrical**: State license required. NC Board of Examiners of Electrical Contractors. Multiple classifications.
- **General Contractor**: State license required for work over $30,000. NC Licensing Board for General Contractors.
- **Reciprocity**: NC has some reciprocity agreements for electrical and plumbing licenses. Check with the respective boards.

### Permit and Inspection Process
- **Statewide enforcement**: NC code is mandatory statewide. Every jurisdiction must enforce it.
- **Permits required**: For all regulated construction, alteration, and equipment installation.
- **No local amendments**: Jurisdictions cannot modify the state code — what the state code says IS the code everywhere in NC.
- **Inspections**: Foundation, framing, rough (each trade), insulation, final. Standard ICC-style inspection process.
- **Code officials**: Must be certified through the NC Code Officials Qualification Board.

## 10. ARIZONA

### Base Codes Adopted
- Arizona does NOT have a mandatory statewide building code for private construction.
- **State buildings**: Arizona adopts IBC and related ICC codes for state-owned buildings.
- **Local adoption**: Municipalities adopt their own codes. Most major cities (Phoenix, Tucson, Mesa, Scottsdale, Chandler) adopt ICC codes.
- Phoenix: IBC 2018, IRC 2018, IMC 2018, IPC 2018, NEC 2017 (verify current editions).
- Tucson: Similar ICC adoption, sometimes different cycle.
- **Unincorporated county areas**: Maricopa County, Pima County adopt and enforce codes. Some rural counties have minimal enforcement.
- **Energy**: Many jurisdictions adopt IECC. Arizona has not adopted a statewide residential energy code.

### Key HVAC Amendments
- **Desert cooling loads**: Arizona HVAC work is dominated by cooling. Design temperatures of 110-115F+ require oversized condensers, proper airflow, and careful refrigerant charging. Manual J calculations must use correct design temperatures for the specific Arizona location.
- **Evaporative coolers**: Widely used in low-humidity areas of Arizona. Evaporative cooler installations must meet local mechanical codes. Ductwork for evaporative coolers has different sizing requirements than refrigerated air.
- **Refrigerant**: EPA Section 608 required. Arizona's extreme heat puts additional stress on refrigerant systems — proper charge is critical.
- **Equipment placement**: Rooftop package units extremely common. Curb and mounting must withstand desert sun, heat cycling, and occasional high winds (dust storms / haboobs).
- **Duct insulation**: Attic ductwork in Arizona experiences extreme heat — 150F+ attic temperatures. R-8 duct insulation is minimum for supply ducts in unconditioned spaces. Many contractors use R-8 minimum even where code allows R-6.
- **Condensate**: In most of Arizona, condensate evaporates quickly. However, humidity during monsoon season (July-September) increases condensate production. Systems must have functional condensate drains even in dry climates.

### Key Electrical Amendments
- **NEC adoption**: Local jurisdictions adopt NEC at their own pace. Phoenix and Tucson are typically 1-2 cycles of current NEC.
- **Solar**: Arizona is a major solar market. While no statewide mandate, solar installations are extremely common. Rapid shutdown requirements (NEC 690.12) strictly enforced. AHJ-specific solar permit processes — many have streamlined solar permitting.
- **EV readiness**: No statewide mandate. Some municipalities are adopting EV-ready provisions.
- **Wire methods**: Romex permitted in residential per NEC. No conduit mandates beyond NEC.
- **AFCI/GFCI**: Follows adopted NEC. Verify which NEC edition the specific jurisdiction has adopted.
- **Electrical licensing**: Arizona requires a statewide electrical contractor license through the Arizona Registrar of Contractors (ROC). Individual electrician licensing (journeyman) is local.

### Key Plumbing Amendments
- **Water conservation**: This is critical in Arizona. Many jurisdictions have adopted water conservation measures exceeding federal standards.
- Phoenix: Follows IPC with local amendments for water conservation. Low-flow fixtures required.
- Tucson: Has some of the strictest water conservation codes in the country. Rainwater harvesting requirements for commercial buildings. Graywater systems explicitly allowed and encouraged.
- **IPC adopted**: Most Arizona jurisdictions use IPC.
- **Backflow prevention**: Required on all irrigation connections. Annual testing for RP assemblies. Arizona Department of Environmental Quality (ADEQ) provides guidance.
- **Gas piping**: Natural gas and LP gas both common. Desert heat affects gas pressure — regulators must account for temperature extremes. CSST bonding required.
- **Water heater installation**: Recirculating hot water systems encouraged/required in some jurisdictions to reduce water waste while waiting for hot water.
- **Polyethylene gas piping**: PE pipe for underground gas service is common in Arizona due to soil conditions. Must be installed per IFGC and manufacturer instructions.
- **Plumbing licensing**: Arizona requires a statewide plumbing contractor license through the ROC. Journeyman licensing is local.

### Key Building/Structural Amendments
- **Seismic**: Most of Arizona is SDC B. Low to moderate seismic concern. Basic seismic provisions apply.
- **Wind**: Moderate wind loads for most of Arizona. Dust storms (haboobs) are a practical concern but do not typically drive structural design beyond code minimums.
- **Energy code**: Where adopted, IECC-based. Climate zone 2B (hot-dry) for Phoenix/Tucson area. Climate zone 3B for higher elevations (Flagstaff area is actually zone 5B — cold climate). Insulation requirements for zone 2B: R-38 ceiling, R-13 wall typical. Much less than northern states.
- **Cool roof**: Highly recommended in Arizona. Many jurisdictions require or incentivize high-reflectance roofing materials.
- **Swimming pools**: Arizona has extensive requirements for residential swimming pool barriers and safety features per IRC and local amendments. Pool equipment (pumps, heaters, automation) must meet electrical code (NEC Article 680).
- **Expansive soils**: Parts of Arizona have expansive clay soils. Foundation design must address soil conditions — post-tension slabs and pier foundations common.

### Licensing Requirements
- **All construction trades**: Arizona requires contractor licensing through the Registrar of Contractors (ROC) for work over $1,000 (including labor and materials). This is a CONTRACTOR license, not a journeyman/technician license.
- **ROC license classifications**: CR-39 (Air Conditioning and Refrigeration), CR-11 (Electrical), CR-37 (Plumbing), CR-67 (Low Voltage), and many others. Residential (R-) and Commercial (C-) prefixes.
- **Journeyman licenses**: Local. Phoenix, Tucson, and other cities have their own journeyman licensing requirements.
- **EPA 608**: Required for refrigerant handling (federal).
- **Reciprocity**: Arizona has limited reciprocity. ROC license is Arizona-specific.
- **Bond and insurance**: ROC requires a contractor bond and proof of workers' compensation insurance (if applicable).

### Permit and Inspection Process
- **Major cities**: Robust permit and inspection programs. Online permitting available in Phoenix, Tucson, Mesa, Scottsdale.
- **Phoenix**: Expedited plan review available for a fee. Same-day permits for simple replacements (water heater, furnace, AC changeout) through over-the-counter permits.
- **Unincorporated areas**: County building departments handle permits. Some rural counties have limited staff and longer turnaround.
- **Inspection scheduling**: Typically online or phone. Most jurisdictions offer next-day inspection scheduling.
- **Inspections**: Foundation, framing, rough (each trade), insulation (where applicable), final.
- **Solar inspections**: Many Arizona jurisdictions have streamlined solar permitting with same-day or next-day inspections.

## FIELD TIPS FOR TECHS MOVING BETWEEN STATES

### Before You Start Work in a New State
1. **Verify your license is valid or get a new one.** Do NOT assume your home state license works elsewhere. Check with the state licensing board FIRST.
2. **Identify the base codes adopted.** IPC vs UPC changes pipe sizing, trap arm distances, and fixture count methods. NEC cycle affects AFCI/GFCI requirements.
3. **Check the local jurisdiction.** Even in states with statewide codes, local jurisdictions may have amendments (where allowed) or different interpretations.
4. **Pull permits BEFORE starting work.** Permit requirements vary but working without a permit is a violation almost everywhere and can result in fines, required removal of work, and license action.
5. **Know the energy code.** This is where most states differ significantly. Insulation requirements, duct testing, equipment efficiency, and compliance documentation vary dramatically by state and climate zone.

### Common Gotchas
- **California HERS testing**: If you skip this, you will fail final inspection. Schedule the HERS rater before you need them.
- **Chicago conduit**: Do NOT show up to a Chicago job with boxes of Romex. It is all EMT, all the time.
- **Florida product approvals**: If the product is not Florida-approved, it cannot be installed. Check the database before ordering materials.
- **NYC professional filing**: You need a PE or RA for most NYC DOB filings. Budget time and money for this.
- **Texas coastal WPI-8**: If the building is in a TWIA zone, it needs a separate windstorm inspection. This is independent of the building department.
- **Water heater strapping in California**: Two straps, upper and lower third. Inspectors look for this on every water heater installation, including replacements.
- **IPC vs UPC trap arm length**: IPC allows longer trap arm distances than UPC. If you learned on UPC and move to an IPC state (or vice versa), double-check your trap arm distances.
- **Arizona design temperatures**: Do not use a design temperature from back east. Phoenix 1% cooling design temp is around 110F. Undersized systems fail quickly in desert heat.
