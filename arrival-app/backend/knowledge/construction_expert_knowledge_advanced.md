# Construction Expert Knowledge — Advanced Field Guide

## Wall Framing

### Stud Spacing: 16" OC vs 24" OC

The standard residential wall uses 2x4 studs at 16 inches on center. That 16-inch spacing has been the backbone of American framing for a century because it lines up perfectly with 4x8 sheet goods -- plywood, OSB, and drywall all break on a stud at 16" OC. When you frame at 16" OC, every sheet edge lands on a stud without cutting or filler.

24-inch on center framing is used in specific situations: non-load-bearing interior partition walls, some advanced framing (OVE -- Optimum Value Engineering) designs, and when the engineer specifies it. You will also see 24" OC on 2x6 exterior walls in energy-efficient construction because the wider stud compensates for the reduced number of studs, and you get more insulation cavity. However, 24" OC walls feel flimsy if you hang cabinets or heavy fixtures without blocking. If you frame a kitchen wall at 24" OC, you need to add horizontal blocking at 36" and 54" for upper and lower cabinet mounting.

**Layout tips from the field:**
- Always pull your layout from the same corner of the building. If you start your tape from different spots, your sheets will not line up and you will waste material.
- Mark your plates with an X on the side of the line where the stud goes. New guys mark the line and then put studs on random sides -- that throws everything off by 1-1/2 inches.
- Your first stud is at 15-1/4 inches from the end of the plate (not 16 inches) so the center of that stud is at 16 inches. Every stud after that is 16" OC measured from the building corner or plate end.
- For exterior walls, snap a chalk line on the deck at 3-1/2 inches from the edge (for 2x4 walls) or 5-1/2 inches (for 2x6 walls) to set your bottom plate. This keeps the wall flush with the deck edge.

### Load-Bearing vs Non-Load-Bearing Walls

Identifying load-bearing walls is the most critical judgment call in renovation work. Get it wrong and you can cause a structural collapse, sometimes not immediately but over months or years as the structure sags.

**How to identify load-bearing walls:**
- All exterior walls are load-bearing (with rare exceptions in post-and-beam construction).
- Any wall running perpendicular to the floor joists above is likely load-bearing. Look at the joist direction from the basement or attic.
- A wall directly below another wall on the floor above is almost certainly load-bearing.
- A wall in the center of a house running parallel to the ridge line is usually bearing the ceiling joist loads.
- Walls running parallel to the joists above are typically non-load-bearing partitions -- but verify, because sometimes joists are doubled or tripled over a parallel wall that is carrying point loads from above.

**When in doubt, it is load-bearing.** The cost of overbuilding a header is trivial compared to the cost of a ceiling collapse.

### Header Sizing for Load-Bearing Walls

Headers carry the load above a window or door opening and transfer it to the jack studs (trimmers) on each side. Undersized headers sag, crack drywall, and bind windows and doors.

**Standard header sizing for single-story load-bearing walls (basic guidelines -- always check your local code and engineering for specific conditions):**

- Opening up to 4 feet: doubled 2x6 header with 1/2" plywood spacer (total width matches 2x4 wall)
- Opening up to 6 feet: doubled 2x8 header with 1/2" plywood spacer
- Opening up to 8 feet: doubled 2x10 header with 1/2" plywood spacer
- Opening up to 10 feet: doubled 2x12 header with 1/2" plywood spacer
- Opening over 10 feet: engineered lumber (LVL beam) -- get an engineer involved

For two-story bearing walls or walls carrying roof loads plus floor loads, upsize by one lumber dimension or use LVL. A 6-foot opening in a two-story bearing wall should get a doubled 2x10 or an LVL, not a doubled 2x8.

**Header construction detail:** The header sits on jack studs (trimmers) which are nailed to king studs. Jack studs must run continuously from the bottom plate to the bottom of the header -- never piece them together from scraps. Above the header, install cripple studs maintaining the wall's stud layout so your sheathing still breaks on framing.

**Field tip:** In non-load-bearing partition walls, you do not need a structural header. A single flat 2x4 across the top of the opening is sufficient. Many framers install full headers in every wall out of habit, which wastes material and reduces insulation space above windows.

### Floor Joist Spans

Floor joist spans depend on species, grade, spacing, dead load, and live load. These are approximate maximum spans for #2 grade Douglas Fir-Larch or Southern Pine under standard residential loading (40 psf live load, 10 psf dead load):

**At 16" on center spacing:**
- 2x6: approximately 9 feet 6 inches
- 2x8: approximately 13 feet
- 2x10: approximately 16 feet 6 inches
- 2x12: approximately 19 feet 6 inches

**At 12" on center spacing (tighter = longer spans):**
- 2x8: approximately 14 feet 6 inches
- 2x10: approximately 18 feet
- 2x12: approximately 21 feet 6 inches

**At 24" on center spacing (wider = shorter spans):**
- 2x8: approximately 10 feet 6 inches
- 2x10: approximately 13 feet 6 inches
- 2x12: approximately 16 feet

**Critical field notes on floor joists:**
- These spans assume proper bearing at each end (minimum 1-1/2" bearing on wood, 3" on masonry).
- Notches and holes weaken joists dramatically. You can drill holes in the middle third of the joist depth, centered in the depth, with a diameter no more than 1/3 the joist depth. Notches are only allowed in the outer third of the span, in the top or bottom, and no deeper than 1/6 the joist depth. Never notch or drill in the middle of a joist span at the top or bottom flange.
- If a floor bounces when you walk across it, the joists are either undersized, over-spanned, or not properly bridged. Adding a mid-span beam underneath is often the best fix. Sistering joists can help with a single weak joist but does not fix a systemic span problem.
- Blocking or cross-bridging is required at supports and at intervals no greater than 8 feet. Solid blocking is easier to install and more effective than cross-bridging. Use the same depth lumber as the joists.

### Roof Framing

Roof framing is where experienced carpenters separate themselves from everyone else. It requires solid understanding of geometry, angles, and how loads transfer through the structure.

**Rafters:** Common rafters run from the ridge board (or ridge beam) down to the exterior wall top plate. The bird's mouth cut at the wall plate is critical -- it provides a level bearing surface. The seat cut of the bird's mouth should be at least the width of the top plate (3-1/2") but the remaining depth above the seat cut must be at least half the rafter depth. For a 2x8 rafter, the bird's mouth notch can be no deeper than 3-5/8 inches.

**Ridge boards vs ridge beams:** A ridge board is a non-structural member that rafters butt against for alignment. It must be at least 1 inch nominal depth and must be at least as deep as the cut end of the rafter. A ridge beam is a structural member that actually carries the roof load -- it eliminates the need for ceiling joists as ties. Ridge beams require engineering and are typically LVL or steel, sized for the specific span and load.

**Ceiling joists and collar ties:** When you use a ridge board (not a beam), the rafters create an outward thrust on the walls. Ceiling joists tied to the rafters at the plate line resist this thrust. Without ceiling joists, the walls will spread apart over time and the ridge will sag. Collar ties connect opposing rafters in the upper third of the rafter span -- they do NOT substitute for ceiling joists. Collar ties prevent the ridge from separating under uplift loads (wind). Code requires collar ties in the upper third of the attic space, typically one for every other rafter pair at minimum.

**Rafter span rules of thumb (roof live load 20 psf, dead load 10 psf, #2 Southern Pine):**
- 2x6 rafter at 16" OC: approximately 11-12 feet horizontal span (depends on slope)
- 2x8 rafter at 16" OC: approximately 15-16 feet
- 2x10 rafter at 16" OC: approximately 19-20 feet
- Steeper roofs allow longer spans because more of the load transfers axially down the rafter.

**Roof pitch and framing angles:** Roof pitch is expressed as rise over run (for example, 6/12 means 6 inches of rise for every 12 inches of horizontal run). To find the rafter length, use the Pythagorean theorem: for a 6/12 pitch with a 12-foot run, the rafter length along the slope is the square root of (12 squared + 6 squared) = square root of 180 = approximately 13 feet 5 inches. Add for the overhang (tail) and subtract half the ridge board thickness.

### Engineered Lumber (LVL, I-Joists, Glulam)

Engineered lumber is not optional anymore in modern construction. You will encounter it in almost every project.

**LVL (Laminated Veneer Lumber):** Used for headers, beams, and rim boards where dimensional lumber is not strong enough. Common sizes: 1-3/4" x 9-1/4", 1-3/4" x 11-7/8", 1-3/4" x 14", and 1-3/4" x 16". LVLs are doubled or tripled to build up the required width and strength. A doubled 1-3/4" x 11-7/8" LVL is a very common header for openings up to about 10 feet in single-story bearing walls. For larger openings or heavier loads, you triple them or go to a deeper size.

**I-joists:** Consist of an OSB web with LVL or solid lumber flanges. They span farther than dimensional lumber, are lighter, do not shrink or twist, and come in long lengths. Common depths: 9-1/2", 11-7/8", 14", and 16". The web is thin (typically 3/8" or 7/16") so you CANNOT notch the top or bottom flange and you must follow the manufacturer's hole chart precisely for any penetrations through the web. Improper holes in I-joist webs are one of the most common framing inspection failures. Install web stiffeners at bearing points and at concentrated loads.

**Glulam beams:** Made from layers of dimensional lumber glued together. Used for exposed beams, heavy structural spans, and curved applications. They are stronger per unit weight than solid sawn lumber and available in very large sizes. Glulams have a "top" stamp -- always install them with the top side up because the manufacturer places higher-grade laminations on the tension side (bottom).

**PSL (Parallel Strand Lumber):** Similar applications to LVL but made from long parallel strands. Commonly seen as columns and posts (4x4, 4x6 equivalents) because they handle compression loads well and do not check or split like solid wood.

### Stair Framing

Stair framing is one of the most math-intensive tasks in carpentry, and one of the most commonly done wrong.

**Code requirements (IRC for residential):**
- Maximum riser height: 7-3/4 inches (some jurisdictions allow 8-1/4")
- Minimum tread depth: 10 inches (measured from nosing to nosing, or from nosing to riser if no nosing)
- Nosing projection: 3/4" minimum, 1-1/4" maximum
- Maximum variation between any two risers: 3/8 inch -- this is the number that trips up most builders
- Minimum width: 36 inches clear
- Minimum headroom: 6 feet 8 inches measured from the nose of any tread to the ceiling above
- Handrail height: 34 inches minimum, 38 inches maximum, measured from the stair nosing

**Calculating rise and run:**
1. Measure the total rise: floor to floor, including the finish floor thickness on both levels.
2. Divide total rise by 7.5 inches (a comfortable target riser height). Round to the nearest whole number -- that is your number of risers.
3. Divide total rise by the number of risers to get your actual riser height. It must be 7-3/4" or less.
4. Number of treads = number of risers minus 1 (the top floor acts as the last tread).
5. Multiply number of treads by 10 inches (minimum tread depth) to get minimum total run.

**Example:** Total rise of 108 inches (9-foot ceiling with floor structure). 108 divided by 7.5 = 14.4, round to 14 risers. 108 divided by 14 = 7.714 inches per riser. That is under the 7-3/4" maximum, so it works. You need 13 treads at 10" depth minimum = 130 inches (10 feet 10 inches) of total horizontal run.

**Stringer layout:** Use a framing square with stair gauges clamped at the riser height and tread depth. Mark the stringer, then drop the thickness of one tread from the bottom of the stringer (because the finish tread adds that height back at every step except the bottom). This is the most commonly forgotten step, and it results in a bottom riser that is too tall or a top riser that is too short.

**Field tip:** After cutting your first stringer, set it in place and check total rise, individual risers with a level on each tread, and headroom before cutting the rest. Cutting all stringers at once before checking means you might have to recut all of them.

### Common Framing Mistakes and How to Fix Them

**Walls out of plumb:** Check walls with a 4-foot or 6-foot level before sheathing. If a wall is racked (leaning sideways), push it into plumb and brace it with a long diagonal from the top plate to a block on the subfloor. Once sheathing is on, racking is nearly impossible to fix without removing the sheathing.

**Crown in top plates not matching:** When building walls on the deck and tilting them up, crown the top plates all the same direction (up). If you mix crown directions, the top of the wall will have humps and dips that show up in the ceiling. Sight down the plate before nailing and flip any boards with opposing crown.

**Missing or insufficient nailing:** The IRC specifies nail schedules. Three 16d common nails (or three 10d x 3" nails) through the end of each stud into the plates. Two 16d nails through overlapping top plates at intersections. Bottom plate to subfloor gets a 16d nail every 16 inches. Sheathing gets 8d nails at 6 inches on center on edges and 12 inches in the field for standard walls (closer spacing required in high-wind zones -- 4" or 3" on edges per engineering).

**Not doubling top plates at load points:** Where joists or trusses land on a wall, the top plate must be doubled and the joints in the two plates must be offset by at least 4 feet. Single top plate construction (California corner, for example) is allowed in some advanced framing schemes but only with specific tie plate hardware at joints.

**Forgetting backing and blocking:** Before you close up walls, think about what gets attached to them later: towel bars, grab bars, cabinetry, wall-mounted TVs, handrails. Install solid blocking now. It is twenty times easier to add blocking before drywall than after.

## Concrete and Masonry

### Concrete Mix Ratios and PSI Ratings

Concrete strength is specified in PSI (pounds per square inch) at 28 days of curing. The mix design determines the strength, and you need to match the mix to the application.

**Common residential and commercial mixes:**
- 2500 PSI: non-structural applications -- fence posts, backfill, setting mailbox posts. Not suitable for any structural element.
- 3000 PSI: general residential use -- sidewalks, patios, driveways that will not see heavy truck traffic, some footings in low-load situations.
- 3500 PSI: standard residential footings, garage slabs, residential foundation walls. This is the most common mix you will pour on residential jobs.
- 4000 PSI: structural slabs, commercial footings, driveways that see truck traffic, basement floors that will be finished, any slab with heavy loading.
- 4500-5000 PSI: commercial structural applications, parking structures, bridge decks, industrial floors. Also used where early strength gain is needed (high-early mix).
- 5000+ PSI: high-performance applications, precast, post-tensioned slabs. You will not see this on a typical residential job.

**Water-cement ratio matters more than anything.** Adding water to the mix for workability is the single most destructive thing you can do to concrete. Every gallon of water added per yard of concrete reduces the final strength by hundreds of PSI and increases shrinkage cracking. If the mix is too stiff to work with, add a mid-range water reducer (superplasticizer) instead of water. A good rule: the slump should be 4-5 inches for most flatwork. If it shows up at 7-inch slump, send it back.

### Rebar Sizing and Placement

**Rebar sizes used in residential and light commercial:**
- #3 rebar (3/8" diameter): temperature and shrinkage steel in slabs, small pads.
- #4 rebar (1/2" diameter): the workhorse of residential construction. Used in footings, foundation walls, retaining walls, slabs. Most residential footings call for two or three #4 bars continuous.
- #5 rebar (5/8" diameter): heavier footings, grade beams, retaining walls over 4 feet.
- #6 rebar (3/4" diameter): commercial footings, heavy retaining walls, pier foundations.
- #7 and larger: commercial and structural applications, designed by the engineer.

**Placement rules:**
- Rebar must have minimum concrete cover: 3 inches when poured against earth (footings), 2 inches for formed walls exposed to weather, 1-1/2 inches for interior non-exposed concrete, and 3/4 inch for slabs not exposed to weather.
- In footings, rebar runs continuously along the length. Splice (overlap) a minimum of 40 bar diameters -- for #4 bar, that is 40 x 0.5 = 20 inches minimum splice. Wire-tie the splice at three points.
- In slabs, rebar or welded wire mesh sits at mid-depth or slightly above mid-depth. The old method of "hooking it up with your boot as you pour" does not work. Use rebar chairs (dobies) to hold it at the correct height before the pour.
- In foundation walls, vertical rebar is placed per the engineer's specification -- typically #4 or #5 at 24" to 48" on center for residential basement walls, with horizontal bars every 24" to 48" as well.

**Field tip:** Rebar must be clean and free of loose rust, mud, oil, and form release agent. Light surface rust is actually beneficial -- it improves the bond between steel and concrete. But flaking, scaling rust reduces the bar diameter and must be wire-brushed off.

### Control Joints (Contraction Joints)

Concrete cracks. That is a fact. The purpose of control joints is to make it crack where you want it to, in a straight line that looks intentional, rather than randomly across the middle of a slab.

**Spacing rules:**
- Joint spacing in feet should be no more than 2 to 3 times the slab thickness in inches. For a 4-inch slab, joints should be no more than 8 to 12 feet apart.
- Keep panels roughly square. A panel that is 4 feet by 12 feet will crack because the length-to-width ratio is too high. Panels should not exceed a 1.5:1 length-to-width ratio.
- Joints should be cut to a depth of at least 1/4 the slab thickness. For a 4-inch slab, cut at least 1 inch deep. This creates a weakened plane where the crack will form.
- Tool joints into the fresh concrete during finishing, or saw-cut them within 6 to 18 hours after placing (depending on temperature and mix). Waiting too long allows random cracking to start before you cut.

**Where to place control joints:**
- At all re-entrant corners (inside corners). A slab that wraps around a column or turns a corner will crack from that corner if you do not control it.
- In line with columns, edges of openings, or any other point of stress concentration.
- At changes in slab thickness, at construction joints (cold joints), and where new concrete meets existing concrete.

### Curing Methods and Timing

Concrete does not dry -- it cures through a chemical reaction (hydration) that requires water. The first 7 days are critical, and full design strength is reached at 28 days. Concrete that dries out too fast will be weak, dusty on the surface, and prone to cracking.

**Curing methods:**
- Water curing: the gold standard. Keep the surface wet for at least 7 days. This means sprinklers, wet burlap, or soaker hoses. Not practical in all situations but produces the strongest, most durable surface.
- Curing compound: spray-on liquid membrane that seals in moisture. Apply immediately after finishing. This is the most common method for flatwork. Use a white-pigmented compound so you can see coverage. Apply at the manufacturer's rate -- too thin and it does not seal, too thick and it can cause discoloration.
- Plastic sheeting: lay 6-mil poly over the surface. Seal the edges. Effective but causes discoloration (mottled appearance) from uneven moisture contact. Not suitable for decorative or exposed concrete.
- Wet curing blankets: insulated blankets that hold moisture and heat. Essential for cold weather pours.

**Timing benchmarks:**
- At 24 hours: concrete has reached roughly 10-15% of design strength. You can walk on it carefully.
- At 3 days: approximately 40-50% strength. Light foot traffic and forming work on top is okay.
- At 7 days: approximately 65-75% strength. Most curing is done. You can strip most forms and place moderate loads.
- At 28 days: design strength. The structure can carry its rated load.
- Concrete continues to gain strength for years, but the rate slows dramatically after 28 days.

### Cold Weather and Hot Weather Pouring

**Cold weather (below 40 degrees F):**
- Concrete that freezes before reaching 500 PSI can be permanently damaged -- it will never reach design strength. That 500 PSI threshold typically takes 24 to 48 hours in normal conditions but much longer in cold weather.
- Use hot water in the mix (not hot enough to flash-set the cement). Concrete should be placed at a temperature of at least 50 degrees F.
- Use insulated blankets or heated enclosures for at least 72 hours after placing. Monitor the concrete temperature -- it should stay above 50 degrees.
- Do not pour on frozen ground. Frozen subgrade will thaw and settle unevenly under the slab.
- Accelerators (calcium chloride up to 2% by weight of cement) speed early strength gain but should never be used in reinforced concrete because chlorides promote rebar corrosion. Non-chloride accelerators are available for reinforced work.

**Hot weather (above 90 degrees F):**
- High temperatures accelerate hydration, reducing working time and increasing the risk of plastic shrinkage cracking (cracks that form while the concrete is still wet because the surface dries faster than bleed water replaces it).
- Schedule pours for early morning. Wet the subgrade and forms before placing. Use cold water or ice in the mix (ice must be fully melted by the time the concrete arrives at the site).
- Apply evaporation retarder (a spray-on film) to the surface between finishing passes if the wind is blowing or humidity is low.
- Begin curing immediately after finishing. Do not wait.
- Avoid pouring thin slabs in direct sun with wind. A 2-inch topping slab on a hot, windy day will crack no matter what you do. Use windbreaks and shade.

### Foundation Types

**Slab-on-grade:** The simplest and cheapest foundation. A concrete slab poured directly on prepared ground serves as both the foundation and the floor. Used extensively in warm climates (Southern US, Southwest) and areas with high water tables where basements are impractical. The slab perimeter is thickened to form an integral footing. Typical slab thickness is 4 inches with a thickened edge of 12 to 24 inches deep and 12 to 16 inches wide. Plumbing must be roughed in before the pour because it is encased in or under the slab. A vapor barrier (10-mil or 15-mil poly) under the slab is essential.

**Crawl space:** Foundation walls (stem walls) or piers support the floor structure with an open space underneath. Minimum crawl space height is 18 inches from the bottom of the floor joists to the ground (24 inches is better for access). Crawl spaces must have ventilation (1 square foot of vent area per 150 square feet of crawl space, or 1:1500 with a Class I vapor retarder on the ground) or be conditioned (sealed, insulated, and connected to the HVAC system). An unconditioned crawl space with no vapor barrier will develop moisture problems, mold, and wood decay.

**Full basement:** Foundation walls extend 7 to 8 feet or more below grade, creating a usable space. Walls are typically 8-inch or 10-inch poured concrete or 8-inch or 12-inch CMU. Waterproofing the exterior of basement walls is mandatory -- dampproofing (a thin asphalt coating) is not the same as waterproofing and is inadequate in areas with any hydrostatic pressure. A drainage board over the waterproofing membrane, connected to a footing drain that daylights or drains to a sump, is the correct assembly.

**Pier and beam:** Individual concrete piers (or helical piles) support a beam-and-joist floor structure. Common in areas with expansive soils (Texas, Oklahoma), flood zones, and hillside construction. Pier spacing depends on beam sizing -- typical residential pier spacing is 6 to 10 feet along the beam with beams spaced 8 to 12 feet apart. Piers must extend below the frost line and bear on stable soil.

### Common Concrete Problems

**Plastic shrinkage cracking:** Occurs within the first few hours, while the concrete is still plastic. Cracks are typically short, random, and shallow. Caused by rapid surface evaporation (wind, sun, low humidity). Prevention: windbreaks, fogging, evaporation retarder, and prompt curing.

**Drying shrinkage cracking:** Occurs days to weeks after placement as the concrete loses moisture and shrinks. These are the cracks that control joints are designed to manage. If cracks appear between control joints, the joints were spaced too far apart or not deep enough.

**Crazing (map cracking):** A network of fine, shallow cracks covering the surface. Caused by over-finishing (working the surface too much, especially with a steel trowel) which brings excess fines and water to the surface. Also caused by curing too fast. Not structural but ugly. Prevention: do not overwork the surface, do not sprinkle water on the surface to aid finishing, and cure properly.

**Spalling:** Chunks of concrete breaking off the surface. In cold climates, this is usually caused by freeze-thaw cycles acting on concrete that was not air-entrained. All exterior concrete in freeze-thaw zones must have 5-7% entrained air in the mix. Deicing salts (especially calcium chloride and magnesium chloride) dramatically accelerate spalling. Scaling is a milder form of the same problem -- a thin layer flakes off the surface.

**Popouts:** Small cone-shaped holes in the surface caused by aggregates that absorb water and expand, or by reactive particles (chert, shale, soft limestone). Not usually structural but can be unsightly. Fill with non-shrink repair mortar.

### CMU (Concrete Masonry Unit) Block Wall Construction

Standard CMU is 8x8x16 nominal (actually 7-5/8 x 7-5/8 x 15-5/8 to allow for 3/8-inch mortar joints). Other sizes: 4-inch, 6-inch, 10-inch, and 12-inch widths.

**Mortar types (strongest to weakest):**
- Type M: 2500 PSI. Used below grade, in contact with earth, retaining walls, and foundation walls.
- Type S: 1800 PSI. Most common for structural masonry, load-bearing walls, and exterior above-grade walls subject to lateral loads (wind, soil pressure).
- Type N: 750 PSI. General purpose above-grade exterior walls, non-load-bearing. Good for soft brick that might crack with stronger mortar.
- Type O: 350 PSI. Interior non-load-bearing only.

**Field rules for CMU:**
- Every wall must start at the corners. Build corners up 3-4 courses, then fill in between using a mason's line.
- Check level, plumb, and alignment every single course. Small errors compound fast -- 1/8 inch off on course one becomes 1/2 inch off by course four.
- Grout-filled cells with rebar are required at corners, at each side of openings, at maximum 48-inch spacing along the wall (code may require closer), and at the top course (bond beam). The bond beam is a channel-shaped block that runs horizontally along the top of the wall, filled with grout and horizontal rebar to tie the wall together and anchor the top plate.
- Anchor bolts (typically 1/2-inch diameter J-bolts) are embedded in the grouted bond beam at 6 feet on center maximum and within 12 inches of each corner and each side of openings.

## Roofing

### Asphalt Shingles

**Three-tab shingles:** The traditional flat-look shingle. One continuous piece with cutouts creating three "tabs." They are lighter (about 210-240 lbs per square), less expensive, and have a shorter warranty (typically 20-25 years). They are becoming less common on new construction but are still used for repairs, budget jobs, and matching existing roofs.

**Architectural (dimensional/laminated) shingles:** Multiple layers bonded together create a thicker, textured appearance. They weigh more (about 240-400 lbs per square depending on the line), have better wind resistance (most rated 110-130 mph), and carry longer warranties (30-year to lifetime). Architectural shingles are the standard for new residential construction.

**Nail pattern and placement (critical):**
- Four nails per shingle minimum in normal conditions, six nails in high-wind zones (varies by manufacturer -- always follow the specific manufacturer's nailing instructions for warranty compliance).
- Nails go in the nailing strip (the area just above the cutout on three-tab, or the area indicated by the line on architectural). Too high and the nail misses the shingle below; too low and you hit the exposure area where the nail can work through.
- Hand-nailing gives better control than a coil nailer. If using a nailer, set the pressure so the nail head is flush with the shingle surface -- not overdriven (breaks the fiberglass mat) and not underdriven (sticks up and causes a bump under the shingle above).
- Use galvanized roofing nails, 11 or 12 gauge, with a 3/8-inch head minimum. Nail length: 1-1/4" for new construction over one layer of sheathing, 1-3/4" to 2" for reroofs over existing shingles (but tear-off is always preferred over a second layer).

### Underlayment

Underlayment is the water-resistant layer between the shingles and the roof deck. It is your secondary line of defense.

**30-lb felt (tar paper):** The traditional choice. It is cheap, easy to work with, and has a long track record. However, it tears easily in wind during installation, absorbs water and wrinkles, and breaks down faster under UV if left exposed. Use cap nails, not staples (staples pull through).

**Synthetic underlayment:** Much stronger, lighter, lays flat, does not wrinkle, and resists UV better. It is slippery when wet -- get the type with the textured walking surface. More expensive than felt but worth it on anything but the lowest-budget job. Most manufacturers require their synthetic underlayment for their warranty.

**Ice and water shield (self-adhering membrane):** Required by code in cold climates from the eave edge to a point at least 24 inches inside the interior wall line. This prevents damage from ice dams. Also required in valleys, around roof penetrations (chimneys, skylights, vent pipes), and at any roof-to-wall intersection where water can back up. It is self-healing around nail penetrations, which is why it goes in the most vulnerable areas.

### Flashing

Flashing failures cause more roof leaks than shingle failures. If you learn one thing about roofing, learn flashing.

**Step flashing:** Used where a roof slope meets a vertical wall (like a dormer or a side wall). Each piece of step flashing is an L-shaped piece of metal (typically 4x4 inches bent at 90 degrees, 8 to 10 inches long). One piece is woven into each course of shingles, with the vertical leg going up behind the wall siding. The mistake most roofers make is running one continuous piece of flat flashing up the wall instead of stepping individual pieces with each course. Continuous flashing allows water to run sideways under the shingles.

**Counter flashing:** The metal that goes over the top of step flashing, embedded in a mortar joint or reglet cut in a masonry wall, bent down to cover the top edge of the step flashing. Counter flashing sheds water over the step flashing so it cannot get behind it. On chimneys, the counter flashing is typically set into a saw-cut reglet in the mortar joint and sealed with high-quality polyurethane sealant.

**Valley flashing:** Valleys concentrate water flow, so they need robust flashing. Two methods: open valley (metal flashing visible, shingles trimmed back from the center) and closed valley (shingles woven or cut to cover the valley). Open valleys with aluminum or galvanized steel flashing are more durable and easier to maintain. The valley metal should be at least 24 inches wide (12 inches on each side of the center) with a center crimp to prevent water from sheeting across.

**Drip edge:** Metal flashing at the eaves and rakes (gable ends). At the eave, drip edge goes UNDER the underlayment. At the rake, drip edge goes OVER the underlayment. This sequence ensures water flowing off the roof goes into the gutter (at the eave) and water blowing sideways (at the rake) cannot get under the underlayment. Drip edge is now required by code in most jurisdictions.

### Roof Ventilation

Proper attic ventilation prevents ice dams in cold climates, extends shingle life in hot climates, prevents moisture damage year-round, and reduces cooling costs.

**Ventilation ratio:** Code requires a minimum of 1 square foot of net free ventilation area (NFA) per 150 square feet of attic floor area (1:150 ratio). This ratio can be reduced to 1:300 if at least 50% of the ventilation is in the upper portion of the attic (ridge) and the rest is at the eaves (soffit), OR if a Class I or II vapor retarder is installed on the warm side of the ceiling.

**The system must be balanced:** approximately equal intake at the soffits and exhaust at the ridge. Most attics are under-ventilated at the soffits because insulation blocks the soffit vents or baffles were not installed. Insulation baffles (rafter vents, chutes) must be installed at every rafter bay at the eave to keep a clear air path from the soffit vent to the attic space above the insulation. This is the most commonly missed item in attic insulation work.

**Do not mix vent types on the same roof:** Do not combine a ridge vent with a powered attic fan or a turbine vent. The powered vent creates a localized low-pressure zone that pulls air IN through the ridge vent instead of OUT, short-circuiting the ventilation system and potentially pulling rain and snow into the attic.

### Metal Roofing

**Standing seam:** Raised seams that run vertically from ridge to eave. The seams are either mechanically locked or snap-locked. No exposed fasteners on the roof surface. This is the premium metal roof. Material: 24-gauge or 26-gauge steel (Galvalume is the standard coating -- zinc and aluminum alloy over steel), or aluminum in coastal environments. Standing seam panels are typically 12 to 18 inches wide. They expand and contract with temperature, so they must be attached with clips that allow movement -- do not face-nail standing seam panels.

**Exposed fastener (screw-down):** Corrugated or ribbed panels attached with self-drilling screws through the face. Less expensive, easier to install, but the fasteners are the weak point. Neoprene washers under the screw heads eventually dry out, crack, and leak (typically 15 to 25 years). Plan on re-screwing the entire roof at least once in its lifetime. Use screws designed for the specific panel profile.

**Minimum slope for metal roofing:** Standing seam can go as low as 1:12 (1 inch rise per 12 inches of run) with proper seaming. Exposed fastener panels need at least 3:12. Below these minimums, you are inviting leaks at the seam overlaps.

### Roof Slope Measurement

Roof slope (pitch) is measured as rise over run. Use a 12-inch level and a tape measure: hold the level horizontally against the roof surface, measure 12 inches along the level, then measure the vertical distance from the end of the level down to the roof surface. That vertical measurement is the rise, and you express it as X:12.

**Slope categories:**
- Low slope: below 3:12. Requires special materials and methods (built-up roof, single-ply membrane, or standing seam metal). Asphalt shingles cannot be used below 2:12, and between 2:12 and 4:12 they require double underlayment and special application methods.
- Conventional slope: 4:12 to 9:12. Standard shingle territory. Most residential roofs fall in this range.
- Steep slope: 9:12 and above. Requires toe boards or roof jacks for safe working. Materials last longer on steep slopes because water sheds faster and UV exposure is slightly less per unit area.

## Insulation and Energy Efficiency

### R-Value Requirements by Climate Zone

The IRC divides the US into climate zones 1 (hot) through 7/8 (very cold). Here are the current code minimums -- many jurisdictions have adopted higher values:

**Climate Zone 1 (Southern Florida, Hawaii, South Texas):**
- Ceiling: R-30
- Wood frame wall: R-13
- Floor: R-13
- Basement wall: R-0 (none required)

**Climate Zone 2 (Gulf Coast, Southern Arizona):**
- Ceiling: R-38
- Wall: R-13
- Floor: R-13
- Basement: R-0

**Climate Zone 3 (Most of the South, inland California):**
- Ceiling: R-38
- Wall: R-20 or R-13+5 continuous insulation (CI)
- Floor: R-19
- Basement: R-5/R-13

**Climate Zone 4 (Mid-Atlantic, lower Midwest, Pacific NW):**
- Ceiling: R-49
- Wall: R-20 or R-13+5 CI
- Floor: R-19
- Basement: R-10/R-13

**Climate Zone 5 (Upper Midwest, northern Mid-Atlantic, mountain states):**
- Ceiling: R-49
- Wall: R-20 or R-13+5 CI (some codes require R-20+5)
- Floor: R-30
- Basement: R-15/R-19

**Climate Zone 6 (Northern states, southern New England, Montana):**
- Ceiling: R-49
- Wall: R-20+5 CI or R-13+10 CI
- Floor: R-30
- Basement: R-15/R-19

**Climate Zone 7 (Northern Minnesota, Maine, Alaska):**
- Ceiling: R-49
- Wall: R-20+5 CI or R-13+10 CI
- Floor: R-38
- Basement: R-15/R-21

### Insulation Types and Properties

**Fiberglass batts:** The most common and cheapest option. R-value per inch: approximately R-3.2 to R-3.8. Available in standard widths for 16" and 24" framing. Installation quality is everything -- gaps, compression, and voids dramatically reduce performance. A batt that is compressed from 3-1/2" to 3" loses about 20% of its R-value. Batts must fill the cavity completely, with full contact on all six sides. No air gaps behind the batt. Cut around wires and pipes rather than stuffing the batt behind them.

**Blown-in fiberglass or cellulose:** R-value per inch: fiberglass blown-in is about R-2.5 at attic density and R-3.7 in dense-pack walls; cellulose is about R-3.2 at settled attic density and R-3.5 dense-packed. These fill irregular cavities much better than batts. Cellulose (recycled newspaper treated with borate fire retardant) is more resistant to air movement through the insulation (wind-washing) than fiberglass. Dense-pack cellulose in walls (3.5 lbs/cubic foot) also provides significant air sealing. Blown-in attic insulation settles over time -- install about 10-15% extra depth to account for settling.

**Spray foam -- open cell:** R-value per inch: approximately R-3.6 to R-3.8. Expands dramatically (about 100x its liquid volume). Fills cavities completely and provides an air barrier but is NOT a vapor barrier. Absorbs and holds water if it gets wet. Used in walls and roof decks where you want air sealing without trapping moisture. Must be trimmed flush with stud faces. It is soft and spongy when cured.

**Spray foam -- closed cell:** R-value per inch: approximately R-6.5 to R-7.0. Much denser and harder than open cell. Provides an air barrier AND a vapor retarder at 1.5 inches or more. Adds structural racking strength to walls. Used where you need maximum R-value in minimal thickness, in below-grade applications, and where moisture resistance is critical. Much more expensive than open cell.

**Rigid foam boards:**
- EPS (expanded polystyrene -- white beadboard): R-3.6 to R-4.0 per inch. Cheapest rigid foam. Absorbs some moisture over time.
- XPS (extruded polystyrene -- typically blue or pink): R-5.0 per inch. Better moisture resistance. Common for below-grade foundation insulation.
- Polyiso (polyisocyanate -- foil-faced): R-6.0 to R-6.5 per inch at moderate temperatures. Performance drops significantly in cold temperatures (below about 50 degrees F the R-value can drop to R-4.5 or lower per inch). Not recommended as the primary thermal layer in very cold climates below grade. Excellent as continuous insulation on exterior walls above grade.

### Vapor Barriers and Vapor Retarders

This is one of the most misunderstood topics in construction. Getting vapor management wrong causes mold, rot, and structural damage.

**The basic rule:** Vapor retarders go on the warm side of the insulation. In cold climates (heating-dominated), that is the interior side. In hot-humid climates (cooling-dominated -- Zones 1 and 2), the vapor retarder goes on the exterior side (or is omitted entirely for the wall to dry inward).

**Classes of vapor retarders:**
- Class I (vapor barrier): 0.1 perm or less. Polyethylene sheeting, foil-faced rigid foam, closed-cell spray foam at 1.5 inches or more. These essentially stop all vapor movement.
- Class II (vapor retarder): 0.1 to 1.0 perm. Kraft-faced fiberglass batts, some latex vapor-retarder paints. These slow vapor movement significantly.
- Class III (vapor semi-permeable): 1.0 to 10 perm. Standard latex paint on drywall (about 5-8 perms), house wraps. These allow the wall to dry while still providing some vapor resistance.

**Climate Zone guidelines:**
- Zones 5, 6, 7, and Marine 4: Class I or II vapor retarder on the interior (warm) side of the wall. Kraft-faced batts or 6-mil poly.
- Zone 4 (except Marine): Class II or III interior vapor retarder. Kraft-faced batts or vapor retarder paint.
- Zones 1, 2, 3: No interior vapor retarder. The wall needs to dry to the inside in cooling-dominated climates. Do NOT install poly sheeting on the interior of walls in warm climates -- this traps moisture and causes mold.

**The biggest mistake:** Installing 6-mil poly on the interior of walls in warm or mixed climates. In summer, humid outdoor air hits the cool poly surface (cooled by air conditioning on the other side), condenses, and soaks the wall cavity. This has destroyed thousands of walls. Poly belongs in Zones 5-7 only, and even there, many builders are moving to "smart" vapor retarders (like Certainteed MemBrain) that change permeability based on humidity -- tight when dry, open when wet, allowing walls to dry in both directions.

### Air Sealing Priority Areas

Air leakage typically accounts for 25-40% of a home's heating and cooling energy loss. Sealing air leaks gives you more energy savings per dollar than adding insulation. Hit these areas first:

1. **Attic penetrations:** Every wire, pipe, duct, and vent that goes through the ceiling into the attic is a hole. Seal around all penetrations with fire-rated caulk or canned foam. Recessed lights are one of the biggest offenders -- use IC-rated airtight housings or build airtight boxes over non-IC cans (maintaining required clearances from insulation).
2. **Top plates:** The double top plate of every wall has gaps where it meets the drywall ceiling. Seal with caulk or foam before insulating the attic.
3. **Chases and soffits:** Interior soffits (above kitchen cabinets, above tub/shower enclosures, dropped ceiling areas) are often open to the attic and act as massive thermal bypasses. Seal the tops with rigid material (drywall, rigid foam, OSB) and caulk the edges.
4. **Rim/band joist area:** In basements and crawl spaces, the rim joist area is almost always leaky and uninsulated. Seal and insulate with cut rigid foam or closed-cell spray foam.
5. **Windows and doors:** Seal the gap between the window/door frame and the rough opening with low-expansion foam designed for windows and doors (not the high-expansion stuff, which can bow the frame and bind the window).

## Decks and Exterior Construction

### Post Sizing and Footing Requirements

Deck posts must be sized for the load they carry and the height they span. Undersized posts buckle, and undersized footings sink.

**Post sizing (minimum):**
- 4x4 posts: limited to about 8 feet in height and light loads. Many codes no longer allow 4x4 posts for deck structures. They are prone to twisting and have minimal buckling resistance.
- 6x6 posts: the standard for residential decks. Can support up to about 14 feet of height for typical residential loading. Use 6x6 as a minimum for any deck more than 3 feet off the ground.
- For elevated decks over 14 feet, use 8x8 posts or engineered columns per an engineer's specification.

**Footing sizing:**
- Footing diameter depends on the tributary load area and soil bearing capacity. Typical residential soil bearing is 1500-2000 psf (assumed unless a soils report says otherwise).
- A common rule of thumb: 12-inch diameter footing for light loads (small deck, low snow load), 16-inch for moderate loads, 24-inch for heavy loads or poor soils.
- Footings must extend below the frost line. Frost depth varies: 12 inches in the deep South, 36 inches in the upper Midwest, 48-60 inches in northern states. Check your local code for the required depth.
- The bottom of the footing bears on undisturbed soil. Do not pour on backfill. If you dig too deep, fill with compacted gravel, not loose dirt.

**Post-to-footing connection:** Posts must be positively connected to footings with approved hardware (post bases). The old method of burying a wood post directly in a concrete pier is a code violation in most jurisdictions and leads to post rot. Use a galvanized or stainless steel post base that holds the post above the concrete surface.

### Ledger Board Attachment

The ledger board is the most structurally critical connection on a deck. Ledger board failures (the deck pulling away from the house) cause more injuries than any other deck failure.

**Fastener requirements (per IRC):**
- 1/2-inch diameter lag screws or through-bolts. Nails are NOT acceptable for ledger attachment.
- Lag screws: 1/2" diameter x length sufficient to penetrate 3" into the band joist (not just the rim board -- into solid framing). Pre-drill a 5/16" pilot hole for the shank.
- Through-bolts: preferred over lag screws because they cannot pull out. Requires access to the other side to install the nut and washer.
- Fastener spacing: typically 16 inches on center in a staggered pattern (top row and bottom row), kept at least 2 inches from top and bottom edges and 3/4 inch from side edges of the ledger.

**Flashing (the most overlooked detail):** Water infiltration behind the ledger board causes rot in the band joist, leading to ledger failure. Install self-adhesive flashing membrane on the house sheathing before installing the ledger. Then install a metal drip-edge (Z-flashing or through-wall flashing) that tucks under the siding above and extends over the top of the ledger. Water must be directed away from the band joist at every layer.

**Do not attach ledger boards to:** cantilevered floor framing, brick veneer (attach through the veneer into the framing with long bolts and spacers), stucco (cut back the stucco and attach to the framing behind), or any surface where you cannot verify the structural framing behind. When in doubt, build a freestanding deck with its own beam at the house end.

### Deck Joist Spacing and Spans

**Joist spacing:** 16 inches on center is standard. 12 inches OC for higher-load applications or when using composite decking that needs closer support. 24 inches OC only for heavy lumber decking (2x6 or thicker).

**Approximate joist spans (Southern Pine #2, 40 psf live load, 10 psf dead load):**
- 2x6 at 16" OC: approximately 9 feet
- 2x8 at 16" OC: approximately 12 feet
- 2x10 at 16" OC: approximately 15 feet
- 2x12 at 16" OC: approximately 18 feet

**Cantilever limit:** Joists can cantilever past the beam a maximum of one-quarter of the allowable joist span. So if a 2x10 at 16" OC spans 15 feet, the maximum cantilever is about 3 feet 9 inches.

### Railing Requirements

**Height:** 36 inches minimum for residential decks (measured from the deck surface to the top of the rail). 42 inches for commercial applications and residential decks more than 30 inches above grade in some jurisdictions. Check local amendments.

**Baluster spacing:** A 4-inch sphere must not pass through any opening in the railing. This is the "baby head test." Balusters are typically spaced at 3-3/4 inches clear between them. The gap between the bottom rail and the deck surface must also not allow a 4-inch sphere to pass (some codes allow a 6-inch triangle opening at the bottom).

**Railing must withstand:** 200 lbs concentrated load at any point along the top rail (people leaning on it), and 50 lbs per linear foot along the top rail (crowd load). Railing posts must be bolted through the rim joist or attached with approved hardware -- screws alone are not sufficient.

**When a railing is required:** Any deck or porch surface more than 30 inches above grade (measured from the deck surface to the grade directly below) requires a railing.

### Exterior Stair Requirements

- Same rise and run rules as interior stairs (7-3/4" max rise, 10" min tread depth).
- Minimum width: 36 inches.
- Handrail required on at least one side if four or more risers.
- Handrail height: 34" to 38" measured from the stair nosing.
- Open risers are permitted on exterior stairs but the opening must not allow a 4-inch sphere to pass through. Alternatively, risers can be omitted entirely on some exterior stairs per local codes, but verify this.
- Stair stringers must bear on a concrete pad or footing at the bottom -- not directly on soil or gravel.

## Drywall and Finishing

### Hanging Techniques

**The fundamental rule: ceilings first, then walls.** The wall sheets butt up against the ceiling sheet, supporting its edges. Top wall sheet goes up next, tight to the ceiling, then the bottom sheet. The bottom sheet is lifted 1/2 inch off the floor (use a drywall foot lifter) to keep it out of any moisture that might be on the subfloor.

**Sheet orientation:** Hang sheets horizontally (long dimension running left to right) on walls. This creates fewer joints and puts the tapered long edges where they are easiest to tape. On ceilings, run the sheets perpendicular to the joists for maximum structural value. When hanging 12-foot sheets, run them the direction that creates the fewest butt joints (butt joints are where two non-tapered factory edges meet -- these are harder to finish flat).

**Backing and framing for drywall:**
- Every edge of every sheet must land on framing or a backing support. If two sheets meet between studs, add a backer (piece of framing) so both edges have something to screw into.
- Inside corners: the stud or intersecting wall framing must provide a nailing surface for both sheets meeting at the corner. Drywall clips (sometimes called backstop clips) can eliminate the need for extra framing at inside corners and reduce thermal bridging.
- Around windows and doors, the drywall edge must not land on the corner of the opening (this causes stress cracks). Cut L-shaped pieces so joints are offset from the corners of openings by at least 8 inches.

### Screw Patterns and Spacing

**Screw spacing for single-layer drywall:**
- Ceilings: screws at 12 inches on center maximum along each joist.
- Walls: screws at 16 inches on center maximum along each stud.
- Edges: screws at 8 inches on center along all edges for ceilings and walls.
- Keep screws at least 3/8 inch from edges to prevent the edge from breaking out.

**Screw depth:** The screw head should sit just slightly below the paper surface without breaking through the paper. If you see white gypsum core around the screw head, you have driven it too deep and broken the paper. That screw has lost most of its holding power and you need to add another screw 2 inches away. Use a drywall screw gun with a depth-setting nose piece -- do not use a regular drill with a Phillips bit and guess at the depth.

**Screw size:** #6 x 1-1/4" fine-thread drywall screws for 1/2" drywall on wood framing. Use 1-5/8" screws for 5/8" (fire-rated) drywall. For metal framing, use fine-thread screws. For wood framing, coarse-thread screws grab better.

### Taping and Mudding — Finish Levels

The Gypsum Association defines six levels of finish. Knowing which level a project needs saves enormous time and money.

**Level 0:** No taping, no finishing. Used in temporary construction or areas above ceilings that are never seen. Essentially just hanging the board.

**Level 1:** Tape embedded in joint compound at all joints and interior angles. No additional coats, no sanding. The tape can show, the surface can be rough. Used in areas above ceilings in commercial construction where the space must act as a smoke barrier, or in garages, attics, and utility areas where appearance does not matter.

**Level 2:** Tape embedded in compound at all joints and angles, with one additional thin coat of compound over the tape and over fastener heads. No sanding or smoothing required. This is the minimum for water-resistant tile backer and for areas that will receive thick texture.

**Level 3:** Tape embedded in compound, plus two additional coats over tape joints and three coats over fastener heads. Surfaces are smooth enough for heavy or medium wall texture (orange peel, knockdown). Not suitable for flat paint or any smooth finish -- the joints will show.

**Level 4:** Tape embedded in compound, plus three additional coats over tape joints and three coats over fastener heads. All joints and fastener heads are smooth and feathered into the surrounding surface. This is the standard residential finish for walls that will be painted with flat or eggshell paint and for light texture. Most residential drywall work is Level 4.

**Level 5:** Level 4 plus a thin skim coat of compound over the entire surface. This fills the porosity difference between the compound over joints and the paper surface of the drywall, creating a uniformly smooth surface. Required for gloss or semi-gloss paint, dark colors, and any situation where critical lighting (such as windows casting raking light across the wall) would reveal joint lines. Level 5 is expensive and time-consuming. Most painters will tell you that a good Level 4 with high-quality primer is sufficient for all but the most demanding applications.

### Taping and Mudding Process

**First coat (tape coat):** Apply a bed of compound to the joint (about 4 inches wide, 1/16 inch thick), embed the tape into the compound using a 6-inch drywall knife, and wipe away the excess compound so the tape is flat with a thin, even layer of compound over and under it. Paper tape is superior to mesh tape for most applications: it is stronger, resists cracking better, and produces a flatter joint. Mesh tape should only be used with setting-type compound (the powder you mix) because pre-mixed compound does not bond well enough with mesh tape to prevent cracking.

**Second coat (fill coat or block coat):** After the first coat dries (typically overnight), apply a wider layer of compound (about 8 to 10 inches wide) over the taped joints using a 10-inch knife or a flat box. Fill fastener heads with a second coat. The goal is to build up the surface slightly higher than the surrounding drywall and feather the edges out smoothly.

**Third coat (skim coat):** After the second coat dries, lightly sand any ridges or high spots (do not sand aggressively or you will scuff the paper tape). Apply the final coat with a 12-inch knife, feathering out to 12 to 14 inches wide on flat joints. This coat is very thin -- just enough to fill any remaining low spots and create a smooth transition. Inside corners get three coats as well, doing one side of the corner at a time (let one side dry before doing the other to avoid messing up the first side).

**Drying time:** Pre-mixed joint compound dries by evaporation. In warm, dry conditions, each coat may dry in 12-24 hours. In cold or humid conditions, it can take much longer. Do not apply the next coat until the previous coat is completely dry (uniform color, no dark wet spots). Setting-type compound (hot mud) dries by chemical reaction and can be recoated much faster (20 minutes to 90 minutes depending on the type), but it is harder to sand and must be mixed from powder.

### Common Drywall Problems and Fixes

**Nail or screw pops:** Caused by lumber shrinkage pulling the drywall away from the framing, or by nails that were not fully driven. The fix: drive a new screw 2 inches above or below the pop, push the panel flat against the framing, then remove the popped fastener (or drive it back in). Apply compound over both spots. Pops that come back repeatedly indicate framing movement that needs to be addressed.

**Joint cracking:** Cracks along joints are usually caused by building movement, inadequate compound over the tape, or using mesh tape with pre-mixed compound. Fix: widen the crack slightly with a utility knife, apply setting-type compound into the crack, embed new paper tape, and apply two finish coats.

**Blistering tape (bubbles under the tape):** The tape was not pressed firmly enough into the bed coat and an air pocket remained underneath. There is no fix except to cut out the blistered section of tape, apply a fresh bed coat, re-tape, and finish with two more coats.

**Shadowing (photographing, joint banding):** Joint lines visible through the paint. This happens with flat or dark paints when the finish level is not high enough (typically Level 3 used where Level 4 or 5 was needed), or when a primer was not used. Fix: apply a full skim coat (Level 5), prime with a high-build drywall primer, and repaint.

**Cracked corners (especially around windows and doors):** Stress cracks at the corners of openings are caused by structural movement (settling, wind, thermal cycling). Paper tape alone cannot bridge these cracks permanently. Use a flexible joint compound or install a crack-repair tape (fiberglass mesh or specialty high-flex tape) bedded in setting-type compound. For recurring cracks, an expansion joint trim piece may be needed.

**Water-damaged drywall:** Drywall that has gotten wet and dried may look fine but the paper face is compromised and the core may be soft. Press on it. If it gives, replace it. Drywall that shows any signs of mold must be removed entirely (not just treated). Cut back at least 12 inches past the visible damage and replace with new material. Identify and fix the water source before closing up the wall.

## Practical Field Wisdom

### Measuring Twice, Cutting Once — And Other Things That Sound Obvious But Get Ignored Every Day

**Always check your materials.** Lumber from the big box stores is frequently warped, twisted, wet, and out of dimension. Sight down every board before you buy it. A twisted stud becomes a wavy wall. A crowned joist becomes a bouncy floor.

**Keep a consistent tape measure.** Different brands of tape measures can vary by 1/16 inch or more over long distances. On a framing job, everyone should use the same brand and model of tape. Better yet, one person does all the layout.

**Square matters.** Check the building for square before you start framing. Measure diagonals: if the diagonals of a rectangle are equal, it is square. If they are not, adjust before you start laying plates. A building that starts out of square only gets worse as you build up.

**The 3-4-5 rule for checking square:** Measure 3 feet along one wall from the corner, 4 feet along the other wall from the same corner. The diagonal between those two points should be exactly 5 feet if the corner is 90 degrees. For larger buildings, use multiples: 6-8-10, 9-12-15, or 12-16-20 for greater accuracy.

**Level, plumb, and flat are three different things.** A wall can be plumb (vertical) but not flat (it bows in the middle). A floor can be level but not flat (humps and dips). Check all three. Flat matters most for finishes. A wall that is 1/4 inch out of plumb over 8 feet is fine. A wall with a 1/4 inch bow in the middle looks terrible when the baseboard is installed.

**Protect your work.** Cover framing that will be exposed to weather. Wet lumber warps, swells, and grows mold. Protect concrete from rain within the first 24 hours. Protect drywall from moisture at all times. Protecting your work from the weather is not extra effort -- it is part of the job.
